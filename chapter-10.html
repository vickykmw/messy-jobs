    Workslop

    The verification crisis

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Quality ControlCredential InflationTrust
    Share

    Chapter 9 was about selecting the right people. This chapter is about what happens inside the organization after selection: maintaining quality when AI severs the link between surface and substance.

    In late 2023, Amazon.com was flooded with wild-mushroom foraging guides. They featured professional covers, confident prose, and the kind of taxonomic detail that suggested years of muddy fieldwork. But the New York Mycological Society quickly issued an urgent public warning: “Please only buy books of known authors and foragers. It can literally mean life or death.“151

    The Mycological Society was not being dramatic. Experienced mycologists who examined the books found species dangerously misidentified, poisonous fungi confidently labeled as edible, and descriptions vague enough to confuse a lethal death cap with a common field mushroom. The authors did not exist, and their credentials were made up. The books had been entirely assembled by prompting a large language model and uploading the unedited output to Amazon's self-publishing platform. The entire process, from prompt to live listing, could be completed in an afternoon.

    Before AI, writing a mushroom foraging guide required you to actually know about mushrooms. The friction of the writing process itself acted as a natural filter: producing a plausible guide demanded the exact same deep domain knowledge needed to make the guide accurate. Now, someone with zero mycological knowledge can produce a book that looks indistinguishable from one written by a lifelong expert. The cover is professional, and the tone is authoritative. But the advice might kill you.

    While the mushroom guide is an extreme case, the underlying problem is more widespread. Cheap production, costly verification, and weak incentives to check have become common features in knowledge work. Consider a university professor whose career depends on his number of publications. AI can now draft a passable literature review in hours. He possesses the expertise to verify every claim, but he also has three other papers in progress, an overflowing inbox, and a grant deadline on Friday. He took a quick look at the literature review and moved on to other tasks. The university professor is not the scammer selling fake mushroom books, but he is responding to the exact same incentive.

    The previous chapter showed how AI compresses the signals organizations traditionally used to screen for quality, making it difficult to spot talent. This chapter is no longer about whether a worker is capable, but whether her work can be trusted. And AI makes trust harder to sustain in knowledge work because of a fundamental problem of organizational life.

    Organizations face an important new phenomenon called workslop: output that mimics the form of real work without embodying its substance. With AI, the cost of producing passable output has collapsed. The same analyst can now prompt an AI, get a polished-looking draft in minutes, and leave early. What once required an evening of focused work now takes five minutes of typing.

    The fundamental incentive problem

    The general problem of monitoring and incentivizing effort is so universal that every student has lived a version of it: the group project. Four people, one shared grade. Brad contributes a single slide with three generic bullet points. Jennifer disappears entirely. Mike promises he is “almost done.“ And you find yourself at 3 a.m. stitching everything together alone.

    It is comforting to blame character: to say that Brad is lazy and Jennifer does not care. But the deeper forces for why group projects fail have to do with the structural features of the problem. One feature is that what the group wants is not the same as what each member wants. Of course, everyone wants to have a good grade for the group, but the problem is that everyone gets the exact same grade whether they contributed or not.

    Each hour you spend on the project is an hour you cannot spend doing something else. This creates an obvious temptation: let others do the work while you enjoy the shared result. When effort is costly to you and the rewards are shared collectively, the rational response is to let others carry the load. This is called the free-rider problem, a classic reason for the breakdown of team production.152

    But misalignment of preferences alone does not doom the project. If effort were easily measured and observed, the professor could easily intervene, rewarding good effort and penalizing shirking. Another key structural property of the problem is that individual effort is incredibly hard to observe. The most valuable inputs in knowledge work, such as thinking, judgment, and care, are not easily recorded and measured. The professor cannot see who actually stayed up until 3 a.m. wrestling with the ideas.

    When unobservable effort meets misaligned preferences, it creates what economists call the moral hazard problem.153 The junior analyst knows whether she actually stress-tested her financial assumptions; her manager does not. The consultant knows whether he verified the data; the client sees only the polished slide deck. In each case, the person doing the work knows something about the quality of their effort that the person relying on it cannot observe. Furthermore, because the person doing the work only reaps a small fraction of the final benefit, they face a temptation to cut corners. It is not about figuring out your underlying ability (which we studied in the previous chapter) but ensuring you do the work.

    Similar to the adverse selection problem from the previous chapter, moral hazard is rooted in asymmetric information: one side knows something the other does not. But where adverse selection is about figuring out who someone is, moral hazard is about getting them to do the right thing.154

    The moral hazard problem turns out to be a central issue for all organizations. Some economists believe that it is the very reason organizations exist. If individual effort and the true quality of output were easy to measure, we would not need companies or managers; we could simply buy and sell every task on the open market. But because the actual quality of work is hidden from the outside, the firm arises to manage the gap. They use a combination of tools, including incentives, monitoring, and culture, to get people to do what they cannot be formally required to do. These tools help, but they are also costly and have their limitations.

    Why this is hard to fix

    A natural solution to the moral hazard problem is to have somebody monitor the effort. Economist Steven Cheung illustrated this with the story of riverboat haulers in pre-communist China. These workers dragged boats upstream using heavy ropes. The work was backbreaking, but it was also easy to pretend to work hard and have the others do the pulling. The haulers dealt with this problem by pooling their money to hire a monitor who carried a whip. His job was to strike any hauler who appeared to be slacking.155

    It seems strange that workers would voluntarily pay someone to punish them. But without the whip, the temptation to shirk can be so strong that the boat may not get hauled at all. The whip is an extreme form of dealing with the moral hazard problem, but its spirit carries over to modern organizations, even if the tools are somewhat more civilized. Managers are the monitors. Their tools, bonuses, promotions, performance reviews, and the threat of termination are the whips.

    But watching can be very expensive. The monitor has to be paid, meaning the manager must spend time evaluating rather than producing. Furthermore, if watching is about knowledge work rather than physical work, monitoring can be extremely challenging. You cannot physically watch someone think, and evaluating work properly requires effort from the monitor. But, then, who will monitor the monitor?

    Because paying managers to watch everything is too expensive and often impossible, organizations turn to a second tool, explicit pay for performance. Instead of trying to watch the invisible effort, firms measure the visible output. They track the deliverables, score performance, and tie the worker's pay directly to the numbers.

    But even if they try their best, firms can only measure a proxy for the performance they truly care about. And because workers are paid for measured performance rather than true performance, they direct their effort to make the measured performance look good rather than advancing the true interests of the firm.

    Economists Bengt Holmstrom and Paul Milgrom, both Nobel Prize winners, took this observation seriously and coined what they call the “multitasking problem.“156 A professor is supposed to both teach and conduct research. Research is cleanly measured by publications; teaching is measured by course evaluations that most faculty consider noisy and unreliable. Predictably, effort flows toward publishing, and teaching suffers. This is not because the professor does not value students, but because the reward system does a poor job at valuing them.

    Even worse, employees can game the system and cheat. When Wells Fargo wanted its employees to develop more customers, it measured success by counting accounts. Employees responded by opening millions of fake ones.157 When New York and Pennsylvania published hospital mortality rates to help patients choose better surgeons, surgeons responded by avoiding high-risk patients. A difficult case might save a life, but it could also add a death to the surgeon's record.158 In each case, the metric looked good, but the underlying goal suffered.

    Even if there is no gaming and the performance measures are reasonably accurate, monitoring and explicit incentives only give you baseline performance. The hauler pulls hard enough to avoid the whip. He does not pull harder. Professors give the lectures but do not stay after class to help struggling students. People do the bare minimum and little more.

    But what organizations actually need is for people to do the right thing, not just follow the rules. And doing the right thing is often costly. It means speaking up when staying quiet is easier and slowing things down when everyone wants to move fast.

    Consider Toyota\'s andon cord, a rope that runs along the assembly line and gives any worker the power to stop the entire production line if they spot a defect. Pulling it costs thousands of dollars a minute. No explicit incentive scheme would tell a worker to do that; if anything, a system that rewards output would punish them for it. But Toyota workers pull the cord anyway, because the culture prioritizes quality and they trust the company\'s promise to back them up rather than punish them.159

    Economists have studied culture and trust through what they call a “relational contract“, an unwritten deal enforced by the value of the relationship itself.160 I help you now because I expect you to help me later. I will do careful work today because my ongoing reputation in this team depends on it tomorrow. Neither side can enforce these deals in court. They hold because the relationship is worth more than any single act of defection. Organizations are essentially using the future to motivate effort in the present.

    And it does something else: it radically lowers the cost of management. A manager who trusts her team does not need to check every report line by line. And managers can empower their subordinates to take on promising but risky projects without worrying about failure. Trust efficiently substitutes for distorted metrics and expensive monitoring. When trust is high, organizations can obtain excellence without the high cost of verification and monitoring.

    But for this type of relational contract to be functional, it requires mutual belief anchored in a shared future. Workers need to trust that firms will keep their promises, and firms need to trust that workers are doing the right thing without needing to be watched. And when that future is in doubt, trust is difficult to sustain, and this is the limit to the use of relational contracts.

    The breakdown in verification mechanisms

    AI poses a serious challenge to the existing way organizations manage workers' behavior. It changes both how work is produced and the difficulty of checking it. Many of the monitoring, verification, and accountability systems that organizations built over decades have become obsolete.

    On the one hand, producing workslop is essentially free. On the other, checking and verifying is hard and getting harder.

    Even when paying attention, checking and verifying is harder than it looks. Humans do not process everything; we filter. A classic demonstration makes the point. Read the sentence below and count the number of F's:

    FINISHED FILES ARE THE RESULT OF YEARS OF SCIENTIFIC STUDY COMBINED WITH THE EXPERIENCE OF YEARS.

    Most people confidently answer “three.“ The correct number is six. The F's in “OF“ slip by because attention filters them out. We rely on shortcuts, and most of the time those shortcuts work.

    When AI severs the link between substance and surface quality, however, skimming fails. This creates the verifier's curse. A junior associate can prompt an AI at 6 p.m., receive a fluent, confident draft, and head home. To do her job properly, the reviewing manager can no longer casually skim the document. She must read closely, check assumptions, trace arguments, and sometimes redo the analysis herself. Because AI-generated drafts are often longer, smoother, and more assertive, they are increasingly costly to verify. The real work moves to the person less eager and more expensive to do it.

    To make things worse, verification is itself subject to moral hazard. Careful checking is costly, yet thorough scrutiny and blind rubber-stamping look identical from the outside. When effort cannot be easily observed and there's little reward for checking, the temptation to skim rises exactly when rigorous scrutiny becomes more important.

    In 2025, it was revealed that a government-commissioned report produced by the major consulting firm Deloitte Australia contained fake citations and references to academic papers that had never been written. The report followed the familiar “strategy deck“ framework, with confident headers, data-driven language, and clean layout, which effectively masked the fact that the underlying evidence was hallucinated. Because the output looked like a standard professional deliverable, it bypassed human scrutiny despite Deloitte's institutional quality controls and global brand.161

    Technology can assist verification, but automated tools often worsen the verifier's moral hazard. In collaborative software development on GitHub, many projects rely on continuous-integration bots to run automated tests before human review. These tools catch obvious bugs and speed up evaluation.162

    As automated checks improve, evaluators lean on them more. Passing the checklist becomes a substitute for understanding the work. If automated tests pass, broader questions regarding architecture, maintainability, or subtle risks receive significantly less human scrutiny, especially when there is a tight deadline. Over time, however, projects accumulate serious unresolved problems even as the evaluation process appears highly efficient.

    Furthermore, when AI is delegated to verify, participants inevitably use AI to game the system. One example recently emerged in academia, where investigators discovered numerous research papers uploaded to the arXiv preprint repository containing hidden, white-on-white text designed to manipulate the review process. Knowing that overwhelmed peer reviewers were using AI tools to draft referee reports, authors embedded invisible instructions, requesting the AI to return only positive feedback. The verification system was thus turned entirely against itself: reviewers used AI to avoid the cost of careful reading, and authors manipulated the AI to exploit that exact shortcut.163

    When AI simultaneously lowers the cost of production and manipulation and raises the cost of verification, the gap between what organizations believe is happening and reality grows wider. AI creates a twin crisis: on the worker side, effort is easily faked and concealed; on the manager side, verification becomes more demanding. The bundle of trust, monitoring, and incentives that held organizations together is under strain, and the organization must redesign it.

    This is why trust becomes more important: not only as a way to get exceptional outcomes, but also as a way to defend baseline performance. When monitoring and checking get overwhelmed and when automated checks are exploited, quality assurance must shift from the work itself to the person who creates the work. The question, then, is how organizations can build and maintain trust.

    How organizations must redesign verification

    If the old systems are broken, what replaces them?

    When checking each output individually is too costly, and when automated checks are gamed, the organization must shift from verifying work to verifying workers, that is, from output-level monitoring to relationship-level trust.

    The most direct response is to shrink the unit of trust. A team of four people who have worked together for two years can rely on relational contracts, since the shared future is valuable enough that no one has an incentive to submit workslop. A team of forty strangers cannot. The implication is that organizations will move toward smaller, more stable working units where trust substitutes for checking.

    Small enough teams are not always possible. Organizations must also invest in a transparency infrastructure. This means requiring workers to show their process, not just their output: annotated sources, documented assumptions, audit trails of how AI was used. The BBVA case from Chapter 7 illustrates this: their “autonomy-responsibility binomial“ gave employees freedom to experiment with AI but made them responsible for outputs, with a signed procedure document for liability. This is verification redesigned --- not checking every output, but creating institutional structures that make careless production visible and costly.

    Finally, organizations must redesign incentives around verification itself. The verifier's curse arises because thorough scrutiny and blind rubber-stamping look identical from the outside. Organizations that reward careful checking through recognition, career advancement tied to quality rather than volume, and explicit accountability for what passes review may be able to partially counteract the moral hazard on the verification side.

    For the individual, the implications follow directly from the organizational logic.

    The standard career advice for young professionals was to network broadly. As the sociologist Mark Granovetter showed, “weak ties“, such as casual acquaintances, former colleagues, and friends of friends, are surprisingly valuable for finding new opportunities. Because they move in different circles, people you barely know can connect you to information and job openings that your immediate network cannot.164 Weak ties remain engines of discovery. But weak ties cannot vouch for the actual quality of your work. A casual acquaintance who passes along your name is not staking their own reputation on your competence.

    What gets you through the door and helps you move up is a small number of people who have seen your work up close, repeatedly, under real conditions. These deep relationships are where trust actually lives. They are built slowly, through shared projects and verified outcomes. Because both sides expect to keep working together, and because the future value of the relationship is large, neither side has an incentive to cut corners. You will not hand your manager an unchecked AI draft when your promotion depends on her. She will not micromanage your every citation when you have delivered reliable work for two years. The relationship replaces verification. And once that trust is established, it extends beyond the two of you. The person who knows your work firsthand can vouch for you to others, and that endorsement carries weight precisely because their own reputation is on the line.

    The danger lies in the middle. The relationships in between are where effort has lower returns. They are too familiar to surface the unexpected information that weak ties provide, and too shallow to generate the trust that deep ties require. And they are time-consuming. Because your time and resources are limited, having these relationships also crowds out the more valuable relationships you can build.

    The practical move, therefore, is to apply a barbell strategy to your professional network. Keep your weak ties wide, but concentrate your real, time-intensive investments entirely into a very small number of deep relationships where people intimately understand the substance of your work.

    How do you earn trust on the first few projects, before any track record exists? You do it by front-loading. Volunteer for the hardest, most unglamorous verification tasks early on. Manually check data. Trace citations to their original sources. Walk your manager through your reasoning step by step. This is the slow, costly work that most people will try to skip.

    By investing heavily upfront, you accumulate what amounts to reputational collateral. You expect to be rewarded later, with autonomy and better assignments, and career advancement. But those future returns are conditional on your continued work ethic. The more you have invested, the more you stand to lose by cutting corners. This changes how others see you. A manager who knows you have put in months of meticulous work also knows it would be irrational for you to throw that away with a careless AI shortcut. Your heavy investment makes it safe for them to trust you. That is how you solve the cold-start problem.

    Key Takeaway

    The hardest problem with AI-assisted work is not output quality — it is verifying it.

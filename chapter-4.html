    The One-Person Company

    Robots Below

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Job RedesignSenior ProfessionalsAutomation
    Share

    What happens when AI takes over the routine layer beneath a skilled human? It lets one capable person leverage her judgment across many routine tasks and sometimes across an entire firm.

    In June 2025, the AI app development company Base44 was sold for $80 million. The firm’s technology allowed users with no programming knowledge to create functioning applications by using natural language. Notably, Base44 had no employees. Founder Maor Shlomo built the firm and managed it on his own.1

    Shlomo was not superhuman. He was a competent engineer who used AI to generate the interface, write the marketing texts, provide customer support, and write and revise his contracts. The AI-based functions were not as good as what a specialist team could have delivered, but they were good enough. Because Shlomo did not need to coordinate with anyone, he could move so fast that traditional competitors could not catch up. Six months after launch, Base44 had hundreds of thousands of users. The acquisition valued the firm at a price that would have seemed absurd for any one-person company in the past.

    This is a model for a one-person company. A founder with strong core skills uses AI to leverage his expertise, patch up his weak links, eliminate excess coordination, and build a firm without a team. AI lets a single competent person quickly build a complete bundle of tasks while rivals struggle with the burdens of coordination. The one-person company with AI can do work that was previously done by an entire hierarchy: supervisors giving commands to subordinates and cross-functional specialists coordinating efforts to solve problems.

    This is a classic example of AI crossing the autonomy threshold, at least in relation to certain tasks. AI can do the routine work and handle problems with known solutions. But in this tier-two example, AI remains below the threshold. A bundle of interdependent tasks remains above the autonomy threshold. Thus, the work still requires a human expert’s judgment.

    In this chapter, we ask what happens inside the bundle when AI takes over part of the knowledge hierarchy from below.

    Management by exception

    Why does the hierarchy exist in the first place? One of us (Luis) has spent the better part of his career studying how organizations utilize the knowledge of experts, whose time is scarce. Common, low-stakes cases are handled by many workers with basic knowledge at the bottom of the hierarchy. The high-stakes problems, the exceptions, are funneled up to fewer, more knowledgeable experts at the top. This is “management by exception.”2

    You see this organizational structure everywhere. The framework is built around who has the highest level of knowledge. In a law firm, for example, a paralegal escalates unusual issues to an associate, who escalates those matters to a partner. In a hospital, a nurse escalates to a resident, who escalates to an attending physician. In a manufacturing plant, a coordinator escalates to an engineer, who escalates to the head of engineering. The organization is a pipeline of exceptions moving upward. The directions (solutions) move down. Those with less knowledge are directed, or helped, by those with more. Such an organizational structure is constrained by two factors: the cost of acquiring knowledge, in terms of learning and training, and the cost of transferring knowledge, in terms of time.

    AI changes this structure radically because it makes knowledge abundant. And, unlike human intelligence, AI has no time constraint. A human expert’s knowledge is constrained by time, whereas an AI system can be replicated across vast amounts of computing power and applied simultaneously to millions of problems. That means it can be used at scale.

    AI allows a person like Shlomo to take care of the routine tasks cheaply and quickly. This is what we call “robots below.” Depending on the type of industry, AI tools can do the support work that used to require junior staff, specialist departments, or outside contractors. A human stays on top of the knowledge hierarchy to deal with the exceptions: decisions that require human judgment, taste, accountability, and contact with the messy outside world. Those exceptions flow upward to the expert, who becomes more valuable because her knowledge is now leveraged by a very large number of AI agents.

    The founder’s dilemma

    A new business is a chain of synchronized functions. You need a product that works, but you also need pricing that makes sense, a message that people understand, a way for them to find you, a way for them to pay you, and a way to support them when something breaks. You need contracts and compliance that keep you out of trouble. You need basic analytics to know what is happening. You need designs that signal competence rather than desperation.

    Most founders are strong in one or two of these functions and weak in others. A technical founder may be excellent at building the product and average at everything that surrounds it. In the early months, those surrounding pieces are essential. Without them the business fails.

    This is a startup’s version of O-ring logic. The traditional solution is to hire specialists or to find cofounders. The startup brings in a marketing expert, a financial director, and a designer. But this creates new problems, such as the cost of paying specialists. Finding them and managing them takes a lot of time and scarce attention. In early-stage businesses, there is not enough work in any function to justify a full-time specialist. She may need ten hours of legal work one month and seventy hours the following month.

    So, the founder is faced with a dilemma. Generalists have weak links, but specialists need costly coordination and cannot work outside their realm of expertise, at least not in accordance with the varying needs of the startup. The founder faces a choice between spending less but being limited by her own capabilities and spending more to hire and manage specialists.

    AI makes smaller firms viable

    Now provide a startup founder with access to AI. The text of the landing page that would have taken hours to write with considerable effort, and that might have produced mediocre results, can be written in minutes and can then be refined. The terms of service that would have required legal advice or potentially dangerous shortcuts can be generated from a simple prompt and then revised to detect obvious mistakes.

    AI-generated results will not be as good as what a top specialist could do, but AI only needs to be good enough to eliminate the weak link, as occurred for Base44. A fast-moving startup in a new business environment is much more valuable than a slow mover waiting for specialists to repair weak links.

    These changes, enabled by AI, make small firms more viable. The tasks that can be done by AI require neither employees nor outside contractors. Thus, the activities that were previously grouped inside a business can be done by a single person. The minimum efficient scale for a serious business has dramatically dropped. Activities that used to require teams now require individuals.

    Nevertheless, the quality of the entrepreneur using AI still matters. The entrepreneur’s judgment remains essential. Knowing what to ask and choosing the problems to deal with is a key skill that only a human can do.

    In a study of 640 Kenyan entrepreneurs who received a GPT-4-based mentor delivered via WhatsApp, there was no average effect on performance. There was, however, huge heterogeneity between different entrepreneurs. High-performing entrepreneurs improved their profits by about 20 percent with AI advice, but low-performing entrepreneurs saw their performance decline by about 10 percent. Knowing what to ask was a critical factor in being able to use the AI advice. The distinction between the high-performing and low-performing groups appeared when the topics in the study became more difficult and challenging. Because they lacked baseline knowledge, the low-performing entrepreneurs tended to ask AI for answers about harder, more open-ended problems. AI advice was less reliable for those types of questions. By comparison, high-performing entrepreneurs typically asked bounded, operational questions that AI usually handles well.3

    Specific skills were also relevant in the study’s results. The better entrepreneurs were good at structuring problems; that is, knowing how to break a messy situation into bounded questions that AI can answer well rather than throwing open-ended dilemmas at it. The job of the better expert, according to management-by-exception logic, is to restructure complex problems into tractable subproblems, which is exactly what the high-performing Kenyan entrepreneurs did.

    In summary, AI cheapens cognition while raising the premium on judgment and taste. Given clear, specific instructions, AI can implement plans faithfully and produce results worthy of praise. But the guidance of the entrepreneur, and her ability to run a messy business, remain critical.

    On the production side of the founder’s job, AI empowers her to carry out work that used to require an entire team. However, AI’s effect on the demand side is different. AI cannot tell you whether anyone wants what the entrepreneur is producing. Figuring that out requires the ability to notice what is missing before anyone asks for it.

    Finding unmet needs

    The world is full of unmet needs, but they are fragmented and heterogeneous. Millions of small niches of consumers want slightly different things. The entrepreneur’s job is to find out which of those niches she can serve; figure out what consumers want, which requires research and discovery; and assemble a bundle of tasks that satisfies that demand. The tasks might involve providing content, products, experiences, engagement, and community. The whole job requires more than a one-time act of “idea generation.” It is continuous: reading the audience, adapting, experimenting, and maintaining attention and social capital over time.

    Consider Kat Norton (better known online as “Miss Excel”), a senior consultant at Protiviti in early 2020. She would fly out of New York City most Mondays to do bank reviews and then fly home to write them up. She would use Excel as part of her daily work, and she got good enough to build training workshops for coworkers.4

    Covid-19 suddenly stopped her travel. She found herself back in her childhood bedroom, wondering what to do with her life. Not one to quit, she repackaged her in-person Excel training material into fourteen-second TikTok videos with music. She started posting in June 2020. By her fourth video, she was already getting huge view counts. The same explanation that used to have an audience of one now reached thousands. Seeing the success, she began posting her content across platforms, including a YouTube channel for longer-form training videos.

    In early 2021 she left Protiviti to run Miss Excel full time, selling her “Excelerator” training as an online course. She told Business Insider that she had already made $80,000 from classes that year. A course-platform case study reported that her first six-figure month was April 2021 (more than $105,000). Today, she has built an audience that tops a million on TikTok and Instagram and she has maintained a YouTube channel with hundreds of videos.

    Norton’s experience illustrates both halves of the founder’s job. AI can help with the production side: scripts, captions, course materials, and customer replies, but AI alone did not make her successful. She spotted a niche need that nobody was serving well. Seeing that people were frustrated by spreadsheets, she figured out that they wanted fourteen-second TikTok posts with music, not hour-long webinars. She kept showing up, building an audience that trusted her.

    These skills of good entrepreneurs in discovering and building demand are likely to remain valuable.

    Jobs and earnings: niches and superstars

    The direct effect of AI is that it empowers individuals to produce more, faster, and at higher quality than before. But we still face the equilibrium question: what happens when everybody has the same tools?

    When everyone can ship a product, write content, and launch a course, there is almost no barrier to becoming an entrepreneur. The bottleneck comes from the demand side. Consumers have fixed time and limited attention. As the number of entrants competing for attention increases, average earnings compress. If anyone can try to start a business, and trying is cheap, the expected payoff from trying cannot be large for most people. Two types of markets are likely to benefit, depending on demand differentiation: superstar and niche markets.

    Superstar markets

    As the cost of starting a business drops, enabling a solo founder to compete, the same cost reduction also amplifies the founder’s returns. When production is cheap and distribution is global, the difference between the best and the second-best can be enormous.

    Think of a carpenter. He buys wood and takes a day to make a chair. To make more chairs, he needs more wood and another day. If he wants to make one million chairs, he needs one million workdays. Time limits his market and income. He has no leverage.

    Contrast this with Taylor Swift. Each song she records will get played millions of times by her fans. She has enormous leverage. Before the existence of recording technology, a singer could reach at most a few thousand people per performance. Local talent would sing for local audiences. The best singer in Milan competed with the best singer in Naples for their respective audiences. Recording technology changed everything. The best singer in the world could compete with everyone for every listener. Technology relaxed the capacity constraints.

    The distribution of talent is believed to be normal, appearing as a bell curve. So, one would expect the distribution of earnings to take a similar shape. Not so. There is a huge concentration of rewards at the top.5 Leverage of talent is a key reason for this.6 In the digital world, a product can be multiplied an infinite number of times without any additional cost. This mechanism permits the work of one person to scale across the world. In statistical parlance, the earnings follow a “power law.” The top 1 percent captures a large share of the value because their work scales.7

    AI amplifies the conditions that allow digital technology to provide large markets for talent. It increases the supply of talent, which makes the quality of talent matter more. In 2025, Meta offered top AI researchers compensation packages exceeding $100 million. Sam Altman, OpenAI’s CEO, confirmed the staggering figures.8 Benjamin Mann, an Anthropic cofounder, defended the economics of these enormous packages. “To pay individuals $100 million over a four-year package, that’s actually pretty cheap compared to the value created for the business.”9

    Such compensation would have been unthinkable five years earlier. What changed? Not the quality of the researchers themselves. Instead, AI’s capacity to reach millions of users dramatically amplified the value of their knowledge. What changed was not talent, but leverage.

    Niche markets

    The market for AI researchers, in which a handful of people capture nearly all the value, is highly unusual. In many markets, consumers have different preferences and variety emerges to satisfy different tastes. These differences are often large enough that no single provider can satisfy them all. Low distribution cost and algorithmic matching allow niche producers to each find their own scattered audience.

    When Luis visited Jin and Yanhui in Hong Kong, he enjoyed an Uber trip with a very entrepreneurial driver. Peter (not his real name) was running a business in the United States. He would buy household items in China, export them to an Amazon warehouse, and sell them under his own brand. For Peter, AI was a huge improvement. “It’s a completely level playing field with the big companies,” he said. “All the functions for which I’m not great—analyzing data, thinking about inventory levels, writing product copy, making pictures and logos, accounting, marketing, finance—can be done with the LLMs that I am using for free.”

    AI has dramatically improved Peter’s ability to compete with bigger rivals. He is a living example of how to overcome weak links. His central competence is finding products and understanding Amazon. AI does everything else. He competes with businesses that are ten or one hundred times bigger, but he does not have to incur his competitors’ coordination costs, and he gets good-enough advice from AI.

    This AI capacity is not limited to commercial entrepreneurs. Political entrepreneurs discover unmet demand for policy or representation among fragmented constituencies. A local organizer who is trying to build a coalition around housing reform is also discovering demand. She identifies people with a shared grievance, figures out what they will mobilize for, and maintains their engagement through personal relationships and credibility. AI gives her better tools for polling, messaging, data analysis, and content production. But she retains the core job of reading a community and earning its trust. Business entrepreneurs, social entrepreneurs, and political entrepreneurs all get more leverage from AI, but they still have to do the messy, relational work that defines the role.

    Research on Spotify shows that its algorithm reduces search costs and enables users to match unusual preferences with unusual producers across the world. This allows many “microstars” to make a good living by serving small, specific audiences. AI helps these niche musicians to produce and distribute quality music at a scale they previously could not afford. But AI tools fall flat without the lessons learned by Miss Excel. She found a specific audience with a specific need and served it with sustained care and personality.

    The squeezed local entrepreneurs

    Artificial intelligence benefits superstars and niche producers, but it hurts the mid-tier producer who survives on a local advantage. Before AI, he was the best available solution within a geographic or social radius. AI reduces that advantage in two directions. From above, the global superstars become accessible to every buyer. There is no need to hear the local singer if Taylor Swift is available. From below, AI tools allow niche producers to find their niche. Meanwhile, the mid-tier producer can no longer rely on providing good products and services to local customers. He is defenseless. He is neither distinctive enough to meet the niche nor talented enough to dominate at scale.

    Think of a local graphic designer. Ten years ago, she earned a decent living making menus for local cafés and logos for the local plumber. She was not the best in the world, but she was the best in the city. Now, the local café owner can hire a star freelancer from the other side of the world for the same price. Or she can use an AI tool, such as Midjourney or Gemini’s Nano Banana, to generate a logo for free.

    It is easy to feel jealous when one sees only the most salient cases and the big paychecks, or to overestimate one’s chances of reaching the top of a field and to succumb to “selection bias.” We only see the winners. We do not see the many people who tried to build a large audience and failed, even though they put in as much work as the winners.

    The right response to this uncertainty and risk is not to avoid leverage, but to understand why it is there and how it plays in different fields. For some people with extreme talent in their field, the right strategy is to make a big bet and join the race to be in the top 0.1 percent of the distribution. For the rest of us, it is good to focus on a career or niche that includes leverage. In other words, we should not sell our time to only one client or product, like the carpenter in our example; rather, we should sell our products, skills, knowledge, and reputation in ways that scale.

    This chapter examined what happens when AI sits below the human in the hierarchy of knowledge. The founder patches her weak links, the expert scales her reach, and the market rewards those who combine production leverage with the messy, relational work of demand discovery. The next chapter flips the ladder and looks at robots above. We describe how “expertise in a box” expands the scope of middle-skill workers, and what happens when that expansion translates into better jobs rather than a race to the bottom.

    Key Takeaway

    By automating routine foundational tasks, AI acts as “robots below,” empowering solo founders and experts to bypass coordination costs and scale their reach. This dynamic fuels superstar and niche markets while heavily squeezing local mid-tier producers who can no longer compete.

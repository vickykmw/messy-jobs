    Robots Below

    The one-person company

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Job RedesignSenior ProfessionalsAutomation
    Share

    What happens when AI takes over the routine layer beneath a skilled human? It lets one capable person leverage their judgment across many routine tasks and sometimes across an entire firm.

    In January 2025, Base44 was sold for $80 million. The firm had created an AI-based app builder that allowed users with no technical knowledge to create functioning applications without programming by using natural language. What was notable about this success was the team: Base44 had no employees. Founder Maor Shlomo built the firm and managed it on his own.60

    Shlomo was not superhuman. He was a competent engineer who used AI to generate the interface, write the marketing texts, provide customer support, and write and revise his contracts. None of these AI-based functions was as good as what a specialist team could have delivered. But they were good enough. The advantage was that, since Shlomo did not need to coordinate with anyone, he moved too fast for his more traditional competitors to catch up.

    Six months after launch, Base44 had hundreds of thousands of users. The acquisition valued the firm at a price that would have seemed absurd for any single-person firm in the past.

    This is the model of the one-person company. A founder with strong core skills uses AI to leverage his expertise, patch up his weak links, eliminate the excess of coordination, and build a firm without a team. AI lets a single competent person build a complete bundle of tasks fast enough while coordination becomes her rival's handicap. The one-person company is doing the job that was previously done by an entire hierarchy: supervisors giving commands to subordinates and across-function specialists to coordinate to solve problems.

    In the language of the autonomy threshold, AI crosses the threshold for the routine layer, the problems with known solutions, while remaining below it for the exceptions that require the expert\'s judgment. This is still Tier Two: a bundle of interdependent tasks with an autonomy threshold too high for AI to cross alone. What changes is the direction of the support. In Chapter 3, we asked which bundles hold together. Here and in Chapter 5, we ask what happens inside the bundle when AI takes over part of the knowledge hierarchy, from below in this chapter, from above in the next.

    Management by exception

    Why does the hierarchy exist in the first place? One of us (Luis) has spent the better part of his career studying how it arises to utilize the knowledge of the experts, whose time is scarce. Common, low-stakes cases are handled by many workers with basic knowledge at the bottom. The rare, high-stakes problems, the exceptions, are funneled up to fewer, more knowledgeable experts at the top. This is “management by exception.“61

    You see the same structure everywhere in production, with knowledge as the main input. In a law firm, a paralegal escalates unusual issues to an associate, who escalates to a partner. In a hospital, a nurse escalates to a resident, who escalates to an attending physician. In a manufacturing plant, a coordinator escalates to an engineer, who escalates to the head of engineering. The organization is a pipeline of exceptions moving upward, and directions moving down. Those with less knowledge are directed, or helped, by those with more.

    Such an organizational structure is constrained by two factors: the cost of acquiring knowledge (in terms of learning and training) and the cost of transferring knowledge in terms of time. AI changes this structure radically because it makes knowledge abundant. AI differs from human intelligence in one key aspect: AI has no time constraint, and hence can be used at scale. A human expert\'s knowledge is constrained by her available time; an AI system can be replicated across vast amounts of computing power and applied simultaneously to millions of problems.

    AI allows Shlomo to take care of the routine layer. This is what we called “Robots below“ in Chapter 1. With AI deployed, the routine layer of the knowledge hierarchy becomes cheap and fast. A large share of the support work that used to require junior staff, specialist departments, or outside contractors can now be produced by tools on demand. The human stays on top because she is the one who deals with the exceptions: those decisions that require her judgment, taste, accountability, and contact with the messy outside world. The exceptions that still require judgment flow upward to her. The expert becomes more valuable because their knowledge is now leveraged with a very large number of AI agents.

    The founder's dilemma

    A new business is a chain of synchronized functions. You need a product that works, but you also need pricing that makes sense, a message that people understand, a way for them to find you, a way for them to pay you, and a way to support them when something breaks. You need contracts and compliance that keep you out of trouble. You need basic analytics to know what is happening. You need designs that signal competence rather than desperation.

    Most founders are strong in one or two of these functions and weak in others. A technical founder may be excellent at building the product and average at everything that surrounds it. In the early months, those surrounding pieces are essential. Without them the business fails.

    This is the startup version of the O‑Ring logic we introduced before. The traditional solution is to hire specialists or find cofounders. Bring a marketing expert, a financial director, and a designer. But this creates new problems, not least that these specialists are expensive. Finding them and managing them takes a lot of time and scarce attention. In early-stage businesses, there is not enough work in any function to justify a full-time specialist. She may need 10 hours of legal work one month and 70 in a week the following month.

    The founder is faced with a dilemma: generalists have weak links; specialists need costly coordination and cannot work outside of their expertise in the time not currently needed by the startup. The choice is to be limited by one's own capabilities or by the cost of managing others.

    AI makes smaller firms viable

    Now provide a start-up founder with access to AI. The text of the landing page that would have taken hours to write with a lot of pain, and that would have produced mediocre results, can be written in minutes and can be refined through some iterations. The terms of service that would have required legal advice or potentially dangerous shortcuts can be generated from a simple prompt and can be revised to detect obvious mistakes.

    None of these results by AI are as good as what would be produced by a top specialist. But they do not need to be. They need to be good enough to eliminate the weak link, as was the case in Base44. In the early stage of business, a fast startup that acts to the new environment is much more valuable than a slow mover waiting for specialists to repair weak links.

    These changes make small firms more viable. The task that can be done by AI requires neither employees nor outside contractors. Thus, the activities that were previously grouped inside a business can be done by a single person. The minimum efficient scale for a serious business has dramatically dropped. Activities that used to require teams now require individuals.

    But the quality of the entrepreneur using the AI still matters. Because of this reliance on the entrepreneur's judgment, AI helps the capable and harms the less capable. Knowing what to ask and choosing the problems to deal with is a key skill. In a study of 640 Kenyan entrepreneurs who received a GPT-4-based mentor delivered via Whatsapp, there was no average effect on performance. But there was huge heterogeneity between different entrepreneurs. While high-performing entrepreneurs improved their profits by about 20 percent with AI advice, low-performing entrepreneurs saw their performance decline by about 10 percent. Knowing what to ask was critical in being able to use the advice. Both groups asked similar numbers of questions of similar quality and length. The distinction lay in the difficulty and challenge level of the chosen topics. Low-performing entrepreneurs tended to ask about harder, more open-ended problems, questions where AI advice was less reliable. High-performing entrepreneurs asked more bounded, operational questions where the AI could give useful answers.62

    Specific skills are also relevant. The better entrepreneurs were good at decomposing problems, knowing how to break a messy situation into bounded questions that AI can answer well, versus throwing open-ended dilemmas at it. The job of the better expert, in an echo of the management-by-exception logic, is to decompose problems into tractable sub-problems, which is exactly what the high-performing Kenyan entrepreneurs did.

    In sum, AI cheapens cognition, but it raises the premium on judgment and taste. Given clear, specific instructions, AI can implement plans faithfully and produce results worthy of praise, but the guidance of the entrepreneur, and her ability to execute, remain critical.

    We have discussed the production side of the founder\'s job. AI empowers them to carry out work that used to require an entire team. But AI\'s effect on the demand side is different. AI cannot tell you whether anyone wants what the entrepreneurs are producing. Figuring that out requires the ability to notice what is missing before anyone asks for it.

    Finding unmet needs

    The world is full of unmet needs, but they are fragmented and heterogeneous. Millions of small niches of consumers want slightly different things. The entrepreneur's job is to find out which of those niches she can serve, figure out what consumers actually want (this requires discovery, as they often cannot articulate those needs) and assemble the bundle of tasks that satisfies that demand: content, products, experiences, engagement, and community. This is not just a one-time act of “idea generation.“ It is continuous: reading the audience, adapting, experimenting, and maintaining attention and social capital over time.

    Consider Kat Norton (better known online as “Miss Excel“) who was a senior consultant at Protiviti at the start of 2020. She would fly out of New York City most Mondays to do bank reviews and then fly home to write them up. She would use Excel as part of her daily work, and she got good enough that she built training workshops for coworkers.63

    COVID-19 suddenly stopped her travel. She found herself back in her childhood bedroom, wondering what to do with her life. She took one of the small tricks she had normally taught one colleague at a time, like Excel's LEFT/RIGHT text functions, wrapped it in a 14‑second TikTok with music, and started posting in June 2020. By her fourth video, she was already getting huge view counts. The same explanation that used to have an audience of one became one with an audience of thousands. She began posting her content across platforms, including a YouTube channel for longer-form training videos.

    In early 2021 she left Protiviti to run Miss Excel full time, selling her “Excelerator“ training as an online course business; she told Business Insider she'd already made $80,000 from classes that year, and a course-platform case study reports her first six‑figure month was April 2021 (over $105K). Today, she has built an audience that tops a million on TikTok/Instagram and maintained a YouTube channel with hundreds of videos.

    Norton\'s experience illustrates both halves of the founder\'s job. AI can help with the production side: scripts, captions, course materials, and customer replies. But that is not what made her successful. She spotted a niche nobody was serving well: people frustrated by spreadsheets. She figured out, through trial and error, that they wanted 14-second TikToks with music, not hour-long webinars. And she kept showing up, building an audience that trusted her. AI is likely to compress the performance differences in production. But skills in discovering and building demand will remain valuable for entrepreneurs.

    Jobs and earnings: niches and superstars

    The direct effect of AI on the empowered individual is unambiguous: she can produce more, faster, and at higher quality than before. But the equilibrium question is what happens when everybody has the same tools.

    When everyone can ship a product, write content, and launch a course, there is almost no barrier to becoming an entrepreneur. The bottleneck comes from the demand side. Consumers have fixed time and limited attention. As the number of entrants competing for attention increases, average earnings compress. If anyone can try, and trying is cheap, the expected payoff from trying cannot be large for most people. There are two types of markets that can benefit, depending on the demand differentiation: superstar and niche markets.

    Superstars

    At the top, the same cost collapse that enables a solo founder to compete also amplifies returns at the top. When production is cheap and distribution is global, the difference between the best and the second-best scales enormously.

    Think of a carpenter. He buys wood and takes a day to make a chair. To make more chairs, he needs more wood and another day. If he wants to make one million chairs, he needs one million workdays. His market and thus income are limited by his time. He has no leverage.

    Contrast this with Taylor Swift. Each song she records will get played millions of times by her fans. She has enormous leverage. Before recording technology, a singer could reach at most a few thousand people per performance. Local talent would sing for local audiences. The best singer in Milan competed with the best singer in Naples for their respective audiences. Recording changed everything. Now the best singer in the world competes with everyone else for every listener. Technology relaxes the capacity constraints.

    The distribution of talent is believed to be normal, appearing as a bell curve. One would expect the distribution of earnings to take a similar shape. Instead, there is a huge concentration of rewards at the top.64 Leverage of talent is a key reason for this.65 In the digital world, a product can be multiplied infinite times without any additional cost. This mechanism permits the work of one single person to scale across the world. In statistical parlance, the earnings follow a “power law.“ The top 1 percent captures a large share of the value because their work scales.66 This could be a result of hierarchy (the decision of the CEO affects the output of each worker, as no one earns any money if the product is wrong) or of the plain scale of the addressable market.

    AI amplifies the conditions that allow digital technology to provide large markets for talent. It makes talent scale further and makes the quality gap matter more. In 2025, Meta offered top AI researchers compensation packages exceeding one hundred million dollars. Sam Altman, OpenAI's CEO, confirmed the staggering figures: signing bonuses of a hundred million plus equivalent ongoing compensation.67 Benjamin Mann, an Anthropic cofounder, defended the economics of these enormous packages: “To pay individuals like a hundred million dollars over a four-year package, that's actually pretty cheap compared to the value created for the business.“68 Such compensation would have been unthinkable five years earlier. What changed was not the quality of the researchers themselves, but the value of the knowledge they can create and the mechanism that multiplied that value across millions of users. What changed was not talent, but leverage.

    Niche markets

    Yet most markets do not look like AI research, where a handful of people capture nearly all the value. In many markets consumers have different preferences, and variety emerges to satisfy different tastes. Often, these differences are large enough that no single provider can satisfy them all. Low distribution cost and algorithmic matching allow niche producers to each find their own scattered audience.

    When Luis was visiting Jin and Yanhui in Hong Kong, he had a trip in Uber with a very entrepreneurial driver. Peter, not his real name, was running a business in the United States. He would buy household items in China, export them to an Amazon warehouse, and sell them under his own brand. For Peter, AI was a huge improvement: “It's a completely level playing field with the big ones,“ he said. “All the functions in which I'm not great, from analyzing the data that Amazon provides on other brands to thinking of inventory levels, writing copy for the products, making pictures, logos, accounting, marketing, finance, and everything, can be done in a way that is good enough with the LLMs that I am using for free.“

    That is why Peter thinks his disadvantage with the bigger rivals has drastically been reduced. Peter is a living case of insurance against the weak links. His central competence is finding products and understanding Amazon. AI does everything else. He competes with businesses that are 10 or 100 times bigger, but he does not have to incur the coordination costs they incur, and he gets good enough advice from AI.

    The point extends beyond commercial entrepreneurs. Political entrepreneurs discover unmet demand for policy or representation among fragmented constituencies. A local organizer who builds a coalition around housing reform is doing demand-discovery: she identifies people with a shared grievance, figures out what they will mobilize for, and maintains their engagement through personal relationships and credibility. AI gives her better tools for polling, messaging, data analysis, content production, but the core job of reading a community and earning its trust stays human. Business entrepreneurs, social entrepreneurs, and political entrepreneurs all get more leverage from AI. But they still have to do the messy, relational work that defines the role.

    Joel Waldfogel and coauthors' research on Spotify shows how the algorithm reduces search costs and enables the matching of unusual preferences with unusual producers across the world, allowing many “microstars“ to make a good living by serving small, specific audiences.69 AI helps these niche players produce at quality levels they could not previously afford. Miss Excel illustrates the niche path. She found a specific audience with a specific need and served it with sustained care and personality.

    The squeezed local entrepreneurs

    While benefiting both superstars and niche producers, AI hurts the mid-tier producer who survives on a local advantage. Before AI, she was the best available solution within a geographic or social radius. AI reduces the advantage in two directions. From above, global superstars become accessible to every buyer. No need to hear the local singer if Taylor Swift is available. From below, AI tools let niche producers find their niche. The mid-tier producer\'s old defense, that they were good enough and nearby, stops working. They are neither distinctive enough to meet the niche nor talented enough to dominate at scale.

    Think of the local graphic designer. Ten years ago, she earned a decent living making menus for local cafés and logos for the local plumber. She was not the best in the world, but she was the best in the city. Now, the local café owner can hire a star freelancer from the other side of the world for the same price. Or she can use an AI tool, such as Midjourney or Gemini's Nano Banana, to generate a logo for free.

    It is easy to feel jealous when one sees only the most salient cases and the big paychecks. Or to overestimate one's chances of reaching the top of a field and to succumb to “selection bias.“ We only see the winners. We do not see the many people who tried to get many followers and failed even though they put in as much work as the winners.

    The right response to the uncertainty and risk is not to avoid leverage, but to understand why it is there and how it plays in different fields. For some people with extreme talent in some field, the right strategy is to make a big bet and join the race to be in the top 0.1 percent of the distribution. For the rest of us, it is good to keep in mind the idea of a career or niche that builds in some leverage. Not selling our time to one client or product at a time, like the carpenter in our example, but selling products or reputation that scale.

    This chapter has examined what happens when AI sits below the human in the hierarchy: the founder patches her weak links, the expert scales her reach, and the market rewards those who combine production leverage with the messy, relational work of demand discovery. The next chapter flips the ladder and looks at robots on top: how “expertise in a box“ expands the scope of middle-skill workers, and when that expansion translates into better jobs rather than a race to the bottom.

    Key Takeaway

    The expert becomes more valuable when their knowledge is leveraged by many AI agents — but only if they remain the one handling the exceptions.

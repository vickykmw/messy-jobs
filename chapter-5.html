    The Rise of the Middle Class

    Robots Above

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Senior ProfessionalsEarly CareerJob Redesign
    Share

    What happens when AI delivers expert knowledge to less-skilled workers? It expands the scope of middle-skill workers. That does not necessarily mean these middle-skill workers are better off. They gain only when demand expands, barriers remain, and the bundle holds together.

    The night shift staff at the pediatric pulmonology wing of Hospital Ramón y Cajal in Madrid works as it does in many other hospitals. The ward nurses monitor oxygen levels and other vital signs of children with respiratory trouble. They follow a detailed protocol and deal with routine visits. At any sign of trouble, they call the pediatric pulmonologist.

    Imagine workers on the same shift with a high-quality AI system. The nurse enters all the information as before, but now the AI system can deal with a larger share of apparent problems. Only in rare catastrophic situations must the doctor be called. The nurse must still do the physical work, monitor the children, calm the parents, and call security if needed. But she now has a specialist in her pocket. She can deal with more patients, reducing their wait times.

    AI as “expertise in a box” can relax the constraints imposed by cognition. It can expand the scope of middle-skill workers: the nurse practitioner is able to handle cases that previously required a doctor. Whether nurses’ wages rise depends on what remains scarce once everyone has the tool.

    In the previous chapter, we discussed what happens when AI automates the routine layer of the knowledge hierarchy and lets the experts scale (the “robots below” scenario). In these cases, the best software engineer benefits from running hundreds of instances of Claude Code.

    In this chapter, we look at what happens in “robots-above” scenarios. AI provides expert knowledge to the upper “production” layer of the organizational hierarchy so that the nurse can solve problems that previously required escalation to a doctor. Both chapters are about tier two, which, as a reminder, pertains to bundled professional work where the autonomy threshold for the whole job remains high.

    The end of skill-biased technological change?

    In the IT revolution, computers improved the wages of the most skilled workers. The gains were “skill biased.” To use an Excel spreadsheet or a database or analytical software, a person needed to have high cognitive skills. Hence, computers complemented workers who could program, analyze data, or manage information systems, but it left behind those who could not. As a result, the wage gap between skilled and unskilled workers widened.

    Many economists are worried that AI will repeat this pattern.1 Automation displaces workers whose jobs are heavily task-based, unless new tasks are created. This negative outcome could occur when we consider the impact of AI on simple, single-task jobs. But when we think of jobs as bundles of skills, there are good reasons to expect that AI will not cause widespread displacement.

    The computer revolution rewarded human cognition because computers lacked cognitive skills. AI reverses the direction. When the machine supplies cognition, the scarce factors shift to what the machine cannot provide: physical presence, social trust, accountability, and emotional engagement. These are disproportionately bundled into middle-skill jobs. Many of the skills present in those jobs, such as physical, emotional and social skills, are complements to AI. And in some cases, as with the nurse, they can be rebundled with cognition once AI makes cognition abundant.2

    When AI favors the less skilled

    Consider the hierarchy between a doctor and a nurse. A “complete medical case,” such as a hospital visit, involves two activities. The first is production (or execution), which is the physical work, the monitoring, the relationship with the patient. This is inevitably human work. The second is problem solving, which involves the diagnosis and the decision to escalate the case or refer a patient to a specialist.

    Because expert knowledge is expensive, the nurse specializes in production and handles only a small sliver of the cognitive work. The doctor adds the expert cognition. This form of specialization “deskills” the nurse, who must escalate any complex problem. Nurses often complain about feeling constrained in what they can do.

    When AI makes expert knowledge abundant, the nurse can solve a larger share of problems without escalation. Her job is rebundled. The job holds together strongly (see chapter 3) because patient demand is unpredictable, the physical care generates knowledge that improves the cognitive response, and the quality of the outcome cannot be deconstructed into an AI task and a human task. Rather than specialize only in production, the nurse also takes charge of a larger share of the diagnostic work. The organization needs fewer expert hours per unit of output. The entry-level worker becomes more valuable.

    The computer revolution gave the expert more leverage. By comparison, AI gives the non-expert more capability. The direction of the complementarity reverses; in other words, the bottleneck shifts from cognition to the physical, social, and emotional skills that the machine cannot supply. There is also a reduction in the share of cases that need expert time, hence a reduction in the demand for experts and in the wage premia they can command.

    When machines cheapen the thinking tasks, the remaining scarce input is the part of the job that cannot be reduced to text. In a medical context, a human is needed to help the patient understand, accept, and act on an intervention. A medical professional must be accountable for the diagnosis and treatment plan. If the patient does not trust the advice, if the team does not coordinate, if the person in charge will not authorize the decision, then the medical intervention fails. These relational and physical tasks are, in many settings, the binding constraints.

    This pattern appears across many settings. For example, AI significantly helps less-experienced taxi drivers. Being an effective taxi driver requires two distinct skill sets: operating the vehicle and solving the “cruising problem,” which is knowing exactly where to drive to find the next fare. Veteran drivers intuitively know which neighborhoods peak at specific hours and which corners reliably produce passengers, but new drivers lack this localized knowledge. For novice drivers, this uncertainty leads to lower income and increased anxiety. Consequently, there is a higher turnover rate among inexperienced drivers, creating a retention problem.

    To address this, a tech company introduced a machine-learning tool called AI Navi in December 2019. During a free trial, the tool, which delivers demand-optimized routes in real time, was provided to about 520 drivers in Yokohama, Japan, a city of 3.77 million people. Researchers found that drivers who used this tool reduced their passenger search time by 5 percent on average. More importantly, less-experienced drivers (those in the bottom third of prior performance) benefited most. They saw a 7.4 percent reduction in cruising time. These findings suggest that companies can, by using AI, complement the skills of the drivers and increase the earnings of their workforce.3

    By making more knowledge accessible to workers, AI also expands the talent pool for firms. An example of this comes from KFC China, which has been rapidly increasing the number of stores it operates. To sustain the growth, KFC China requires each store to have a competent Restaurant General Manager (RGM). These RGMs must do everything. Their role involves bundled, messy, human-centric tasks, such as motivating a team or handling an angry customer. The RGMs must handle highly demanding analytical tasks, such as demand forecasting, labor scheduling, and inventory management. The problem is that not everyone is equipped to do everything well. Many managers possess excellent social and leadership skills, but they naturally struggle with the heavy cognitive and analytical demands of the job, which can often feel like “rocket science.”

    To deal with this problem, KFC introduced an AI toolkit designed to act as an operational “super brain.” The AI acts as an assistant to the RGMs, giving them suggestions on how much inventory to stock and how many employees to schedule. The RGMs still have the final decision, but because the AI suggestions are usually accurate, they rubber-stamp them.4

    The AI tool is particularly helpful to managers with fewer analytical skills. The RGMs used to spend lots of time on number-crunching tasks, often making errors. With AI, they now focus their energy on what they do best: applying social skills to lead teams and connect with customers. When their charm manages to win many new customers, they don’t need to worry about whether they have ordered enough inventory to serve them.

    The pattern across domains is the same. AI encodes and distributes expert practice, so those with less knowledge and experience gain the most. The machine pushes knowledge downward.

    However, these direct gains describe what happens to one worker, holding everything else fixed. They are direct effects. When every worker has the same tool, the question is no longer whether each worker becomes more productive (they do); rather, the question is whether the scarcity that sustains one worker’s wages will survive when everyone has the tool. If the hard cognitive step becomes easy, then entry barriers can fall. More people can do the work, or adjacent roles can expand into it, raising competition. Whether wages improve depends on the equilibrium of the economy, on how the whole system adjusts.

    Jobs and earnings: a new middle class?

    Even if AI favors less-skilled workers by making them more productive, this does not mean that their wages will increase. The outcome depends on three conditions. First, demand must be sufficiently elastic so that cheaper, more abundant supply finds more buyers rather than causing the displacement of existing workers. If there is large unmet demand waiting to be unlocked by lower cost, the upskilled workers will have somewhere to work.

    Second, entry barriers must remain high enough to prevent wage gains from being stifled by competition. If AI eliminates the cognitive barrier and the remaining tasks require little training, then new entrants will flood in and wages will fall even as productivity rises.

    Third, the task bundle must hold together. Cognitive and noncognitive components of the job must remain inseparable in practice so that the upskilled worker captures the full value of the AI-augmented output. For example, if cognition is automated centrally while physical and relational tasks are commoditized separately, then individual workers will lose bargaining power. Consider three occupations: healthcare, the trades, and back-office clerical roles.

    Healthcare. This professional sector meets all three conditions (mentioned above) that lead to wage gains. The demand for healthcare is responsive. In the US (for organizational reasons) and in the rest of the world (for demographic reasons), there is a huge unmet need for healthcare professionals. We can expect a large demand response to any reduction in cost. The pharmaceutical retail chain CVS has explicitly described its MinuteClinic primary care expansion as a response to access constraints and shortages.5

    The US Bureau of Labor Statistics projects that employment for advanced practice registered nurses, a category including nurse practitioners, nurse midwives, and nurse anesthetists, will grow 35 percent from 2024 to 2034, with about 32,700 openings each year. The fastest growth corresponds to nurse practitioners, whose median pay in May 2024 was about $132,000.6

    Despite the high demand, entry barriers remain high. Nurse practitioners still face years of training and licensing requirements. High demand combined with elevated entry barriers supports higher wages, enabling a new-middle-class mechanism in healthcare. With the addition of AI, which disseminates cheap knowledge, nurse practitioners become more effective clinicians with a broader scope. This allows physicians to shift toward supervising a smaller number of high-value exceptions.

    There is an institutional caveat: none of this follows automatically. Scope-of-practice laws (e.g., licensing rules, liability rules, and reimbursement rules) determine to what extent mid-skill clinicians can practice. The range of permissions varies enormously within and between countries. In the United States, nurse practitioners have full practice authority in twenty-seven states, but they face restrictive supervision requirements in others. The variation is not driven by patient outcomes but by physician lobbying. Will AI-driven upskilling enable nurse practitioners to work with a broader scope? That depends as much on regulatory politics as on technology.7

    The trades. This professional sector is different than healthcare, but the hierarchy logic still applies. A master electrician or HVAC technician has deep diagnostic knowledge. She knows what is wrong, what to check next, and what does not look right. Apprentices work for years with masters because they need time to know which tools to use and what kind of problem they are facing.

    AI can attack the diagnostic component if the machine knows the manual and can recognize failure modes. But AI cannot climb into remote corners of a house or crawl into little spaces to find a leaky pipe. What about robots? Physical autonomy in unstructured environments, such as navigating a crawlspace or improvising when the manual doesn’t match reality, remains hard for robots to achieve at an affordable cost. So, in the trades, scarcity seems to shift toward execution and accountability.

    Demand within the trades, as with healthcare, is likely to be quite responsive. Consumers and businesses often defer maintenance because diagnosis is slow and expensive; therefore, faster diagnosis can unlock some of that demand. Also, the AI boom itself generates direct demand for the trades. The IEA projects that global data-center electricity consumption will more than double by 2030, reaching the equivalent of Japan’s total consumption. In the United States alone, data centers will account for nearly half of electricity demand growth this decade, consuming more power than all energy-intensive manufacturing combined.8 Building this infrastructure requires massive construction workforces. The US faced a shortfall of roughly 440,000 construction workers in 2025, with electricians, HVAC technicians, and pipefitters the hardest roles to fill.9 Data-center electricians now earn from $100,000 to more than $200,000.10 The AI revolution’s most immediate labor-market beneficiaries may be the people who build the places to house it.

    There is a caveat: although there is an increased demand for workers in the trades, AI also gives individuals more ability to self-diagnose and solve their own problems. AI enables average consumers to handle a larger share of problems without seeking expert help, which means fewer calls to professionals. If ChatGPT tells a homeowner how to repair a leaky faucet, she can avoid paying a big plumber’s bill. Every hierarchy has a bottom rung. AI pushes knowledge down to whoever stands on it, including the ordinary consumer. For the trades, the barrier to using AI is physical. You can diagnose a leaking pipe with AI, but you still need someone to crawl under the house and fix it. If consumers only need information to solve their own problems, they might never seek professional help.

    As to entry barriers, many trades require workers to spend time in apprenticeships, obtain licenses, and meet tough physical requirements. BLS projections show both strong demand and large annual openings for electricians and HVAC mechanics, with wages well above the economy-wide median. And the task bundle for these jobs usually holds together because diagnosis and execution are often inseparable in practice. The worker who identifies the problem typically performs the fix. The knowledge is local, the environment is messy, and someone has to be accountable.

    Back-office clerical roles. These roles typically include accounts payable, bookkeeping, payroll, and routine reporting. The impact of AI is likely to cause hardship for these workers. Demand is likely to be relatively inelastic. There will not be twice as many invoices just because processing is cheaper. Output is tied to the volume of transactions elsewhere in the economy, not to the clerical sector’s productivity. As Autor and Thompson point out, entry barriers are low once cognition is cheap. In back-office roles, most of the work involves cognitive skill.11 When AI makes that cognitive work easy, many workers will be able to perform the remaining tasks with minimal training. In addition, the bundle of tasks can easily be broken. The “transaction” component can be automated centrally, which will leave a smaller layer of humans to handle exceptions. That reduces headcount even if the remaining workers become more productive and more valuable.

    The productivity gains within this professional sector will likely occur, but the equilibrium implication will be very different. Fewer workers will be needed, and their roles will be more commoditized so that everyone can do them.

    In summary, AI’s impact on the growth of the middle class will be ambiguous. It is true that AI pushes expert knowledge downward in every hierarchy, but the consequences for the workers who receive that knowledge will depend on the structure of their occupations.

    Specifically, in fields where demand is elastic, entry barriers remain high, and the task bundles hold together (e.g., healthcare and many trades), we expect the middle-skill worker to gain. She will handle a broader range of problems, her effective scope will expand, and the combination of AI cognition with her physical, social, and relational skills will produce a service that neither could deliver alone. The constraint on her earnings shifts from what she knows to what she can do, and the things she can do are hard to replicate or automate.

    Key Takeaway

    AI acts as “expertise in a box” (“robots above”), empowering middle-skill workers to handle complex cognitive problems previously reserved for experts. If demand expands and task bundles remain strong, this upskilling expands workers’ scope and earning potential, shifting scarcity to physical and relational skills.

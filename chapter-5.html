    Robots Above

    AI as expertise in a box

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Senior ProfessionalsEarly CareerJob Redesign
    Share

    What happens when AI delivers expert knowledge downward to less-skilled workers? It expands the scope of middle-skill workers. But that does not necessarily mean they are better off. They gain only when demand expands, barriers remain, and the bundle holds together.

    The night shift in the pediatric pneumology wing of Hospital Ramón y Cajal in Madrid works as it does in many other hospitals. The ward nurses monitor the oxygen levels and other vital constants of children with respiratory trouble. They follow a detailed protocol and deal with routine visits. At any sign of trouble, they call the pediatric pneumologist.

    Imagine the same shift with a high-quality AI system. The nurse enters all the information as before. But now the AI system can deal with a larger share of apparent problems. Only in the rare catastrophic situation must the doctor be called. The nurse still must do the physical work, monitoring the children, calming the parents, and calling security if needed. But she now has a specialist in her pocket. She can deal with more patients, reducing their wait times.

    AI as “expertise in a box“ can relax the constraints imposed by cognition and can expand the scope of middle-skill workers: the nurse practitioner is able to handle cases that, before, required a doctor. But whether wages improve or not depends on what remains scarce once everyone has the tool.

    This is the other side of the coin of chapter 4. There, AI automated the routine layer of the knowledge hierarchy and let the experts scale: the great software engineer benefits from running hundreds of instances of Claude Code. Here, AI provides expert knowledge to the “production“ layer so that the nurse can solve problems that previously required escalation to a doctor. Chapter 4 has the “robot“ (the AI) below. This chapter has the AI “on top.“ Both chapters are about Tier Two: bundled professional work where the autonomy threshold for the whole job remains high. The difference is the direction of the support.

    The end of skill-biased technological change?

    As we discussed in Chapter 1, in the IT revolution, computer gains were \“skill-biased,\“ meaning they helped the most skilled. To use an Excel spreadsheet or a database or analytical software, you require high cognitive skills. Hence, computers complemented workers who could program, analyze data, or manage information systems and left behind those that could not. As a result, the gap between what skilled and unskilled workers earned widened.

    Many economists are worried that AI will repeat this pattern.70 In a task-based framework, automation displaces workers from tasks they used to perform, and the negative effects can dominate unless new tasks are created. And this may well be the case when we consider the impact of AI on simple, single-task jobs. But when we think of jobs as bundles of skills, there are good reasons to expect this not to be the case for AI.

    The computer revolution rewarded cognition because computers required cognitive skills. But AI reverses the direction. When cognition is the thing the machine supplies, the scarce factors are the ones the machine cannot provide: physical presence, social trust, accountability, emotional engagement. These are disproportionately bundled into middle-skill jobs. Many of the skills present in those jobs, such as physical, emotional, social skills, are complements to AI. And in some cases, as with the nurse, they can be rebundled with cognition once AI makes cognition abundant.71

    When AI favors the less skilled

    To fix ideas, consider the hierarchy between a doctor and a nurse. A \“complete case\“, such as a hospital visit, involves two activities:

    Production or execution: the physical work, the monitoring, the relationship with the patient. This is inevitably human.

    Problem solving: the diagnosis, the decision to escalate or refer.

    Since expert knowledge is expensive, the nurse specializes in production and handles only a small sliver of the cognitive work. The doctor adds the expert cognition. This form of specialization \“deskills\“ the nurse, who must escalate any complex problem. Nurses often complain about feeling constrained in what they can do.

    When AI makes expert knowledge abundant, the nurse can solve a larger share of problems without escalation. In the language of Chapter 3, her job is rebundled. The rebundled job holds together because it satisfies the conditions for a strong bundle from Chapter 3: patient demand is unpredictable, doing the physical care generates knowledge that improves the cognitive response, and the quality of the outcome cannot be decomposed into an AI part and a human part. Rather than specialize only in production, she takes charge of a (larger) share of the diagnostic work too. The organization needs fewer expert hours per unit of output. The entry-level worker becomes more valuable.

    The computer revolution gave the expert more leverage. AI gives the non-expert more capability. The direction of the complementarity reverses: the bottleneck shifts from cognition to the physical, social, and emotional skills that the machine cannot supply.

    There is a drop in that share of exceptions, that is, there is a reduction in the share of cases that need expert time. This is a path for inequality to be reduced.

    When machines cheapen the thinking tasks, the remaining scarce input is often the part of the job that cannot be reduced to text: getting a human being to accept, understand, and act on an intervention, and be accountable when it goes wrong. The service fails if the patient does not trust the advice, if the team does not coordinate, if the person in charge will not sign their name under the decision. These relational and physical tasks are, in many settings, the binding constraints.

    This pattern appears across many settings. Another example of AI favoring less-skilled workers comes from the taxi sector, where inexperienced drivers have benefited significantly from pairing their skills with artificial intelligence. Being an effective taxi driver requires two distinct skill sets: operating the vehicle and solving the \“cruising problem\“, knowing exactly where to drive to find the next fare.

    While veteran drivers intuitively know which neighborhoods peak at specific hours and which corners reliably produce passengers, new drivers lack this localized knowledge. This uncertainty not only leads to lower income for novice drivers but also causes the anxiety of constantly searching for new passengers. Consequently, this results in a higher turnover rate among inexperienced drivers, creating a retention problem.

    To address this, a tech company introduced a machine-learning tool called AI Navi in December 2019. During a free trial, the tool, which suggests demand-optimized routes in real time, was provided to about 520 drivers in Yokohama, Japan, a city of 3.77 million people. Researchers found that using this tool reduced search time by 5 percent on average. More importantly, the gain centered mostly on low-skilled drivers (those in the bottom third of prior performance). They saw a 7.4 percent reduction in cruising time. These findings suggest that companies can, by using AI complement the skills of the drivers, increase the earnings of their current workforce.72

    By making the knowledge accessible to the workers, AI also expands the talent pool for firms. An example of this comes from KFC China, which has been rapidly growing in the number of stores it operates. To sustain the growth, KFC China requires having a competent Restaurant General Manager (RGM). These RGMs have to do everything. Their role bundled messy, human-centric tasks, such as motivating a team or handling an angry customer, with highly demanding analytical tasks, such as demand forecasting, labor scheduling, and inventory management. The problem is that not everyone is equipped to do everything well. Many managers possess excellent social and leadership skills but naturally struggle with the heavy cognitive and analytical demands of the job, which can often feel like \“rocket science\“.

    To deal with this problem, KFC introduced an AI toolkit designed to act as an operation \“super brain\“. The AI acts as assistants to the RGM, giving them suggestions on how much inventory to stock and whom to schedule. The RGMs still have the final decision, though, because the suggestions from AI are often very accurate, they rubber-stamp them.73

    The AI tool is particularly helpful to managers with less analytical skills. These RGMs used to spend lots of time on the number-crunching tasks and didn\'t get all of them right. With the AI, they can now focus their energy on what they do best: applying their social skills to lead the teams and connect with customers. Furthermore, when their charm manages to win over many new customers, they don't need to worry about whether they have ordered enough inventory to serve them.

    The pattern across domains is the same: AI encodes and distributes expert practice, so those furthest from expert performance gain the most. The mechanism mirrors the hierarchy logic: the machine pushes knowledge downward.

    But these direct gains describe what happens to one worker, holding everything else fixed. They are direct effects. When every worker has the same tool, the question is no longer whether each worker becomes more productive. Of course they do. The question is whether the scarcity that sustained the worker's wage survives when everyone has the tool. If the hard cognitive step becomes easy, entry barriers can fall. More people can do the work, or adjacent roles can expand into it, raising competition. Whether wages actually improve depends on the equilibrium of the economy, on how the whole system adjusts.

    Jobs and earnings: a new middle class?

    Even if AI favors the less-skilled by making them more productive, this does not mean that their wages will increase. Whether an upskilled occupation actually gains depends on three conditions.

    First, demand must be elastic enough that cheaper, more abundant supply finds buyers rather than simply displacing existing workers. If there is large unmet demand waiting to be unlocked by lower cost, the upskilled workers have somewhere to go.

    Second, entry barriers must remain high enough that the wage gains are not competed away. If AI eliminates the cognitive barrier and the remaining tasks require little training, new entrants flood in and wages fall even as productivity rises.

    Third, the task bundle must hold together. The cognitive and non-cognitive components of the job remain inseparable in practice so that the upskilled worker captures the full value of the AI-augmented output. If the bundle can be broken apart, for example, with cognition automated centrally, and physical or relational tasks commoditized separately, the occupation fragments and individual workers lose bargaining power. Consider three occupations: healthcare, the trades, and back-office clerical roles.

    Healthcare passes all three conditions well. Demand is responsive: there is no question there is, in the US for organizational reasons and in the rest of the world for demographic reasons, a huge unmet need for healthcare, leading us to expect a large demand response to any reduction in cost. Pharmaceutical retail chain CVS has explicitly described its MinuteClinic primary care expansion as a response to access constraints. and shortages. 74

    The US Bureau of Labor Statistics projects that employment for advanced practice registered nurses, a category including nurse practitioners, nurse midwives, and nurse anesthetists will grow 35 percent from 2024 to 2034, with about 32,700 openings each year. The fastest growth corresponds to nurse practitioners, whose median pay in May 2024 was about $129,000.75

    Entry barriers remain high: even with better decision support, nurse practitioners still face years of training and licensing requirements. This is the core new-middle-class mechanism in healthcare. AI provides cheap knowledge. The nurse practitioner becomes a more effective clinician with a broader scope. The physician shifts toward supervising a smaller number of high-value exceptions.

    An institutional caveat: none of this follows automatically. Scope of practice laws, which determine to what extent licensing rules, liability rules, and reimbursement rules allow mid-skill clinicians to practice to the top of their possibilities or, on the contrary, restrict them from doing this, vary enormously within and between countries. In the United States, nurse practitioners have full practice authority in 27 states but face restrictive supervision requirements in others. The variation is not driven by patient outcomes but rather by physician lobbying. Whether AI-driven upskilling translates into a broader scope depends as much on regulatory politics as on technology.76

    The trades look different from healthcare. But the hierarchy logic still applies. A master electrician or HVAC technician holds deep diagnostic knowledge---what is wrong, what to check next, and what does not look right. Apprentices escalate to masters because they cannot decide which tools to use and what kind of problem they are facing.

    AI can attack the diagnostic component, when the machine knows the manual, and the AI can recognize the failure modes. But AI is not likely to climb into remote corners of a house or crawl into the little spaces where the pipes are located. The obvious objection is robots. But physical autonomy in unstructured environments, from navigating a crawlspace, to improvising when the manual doesn\'t match reality, remains far harder than diagnosis. The scarcity does seem to shift toward execution and accountability

    Demand is likely to be quite responsive: a lot of maintenance is deferred because diagnosis is slow and expensive, and faster diagnosis can unlock some of that demand. Also, the AI boom itself generates direct demand for the trades. The IEA projects that global data-center electricity consumption will more than double by 2030, reaching the equivalent of Japan\'s total consumption. In the United States alone, data centers will account for nearly half of electricity demand growth this decade, consuming more power than all energy-intensive manufacturing combined.77 Building this infrastructure requires massive construction workforces: the US faces a shortfall of roughly 440,000 construction workers in 2025, with electricians, HVAC technicians, and pipefitters the hardest roles to fill.78 Data-center electricians now earn $100,000 to over $200,000, and the electrical workers\' union is explicitly targeting 100,000 new recruits for AI-related construction.79 The AI revolution\'s most immediate labor-market beneficiaries may be the people who build the places to house it.

    A caveat: on the other side of the demand increase is the ability of individuals to self-diagnose and solve lots of problems. The same upskilling logic applies to customers. We are the bottom rung of the hierarchy, and AI lets us handle a larger share of problems without escalation, which means fewer calls to the professional. If ChatGPT tells us to turn a particular screw, we just saved ourselves one visit from the plumber and one big bill. Every hierarchy in this book has a bottom rung, and AI pushes knowledge down to whoever stands on it, including the customer. For the trades, the constraint is physical: you can diagnose a leaking pipe with AI, but you still need someone to crawl under the house and fix it. For professions where the output is purely informational, the customer may never call at all.

    As to entry barriers, many of these trades have apprenticeship time, licensing, and physical demands that not everyone can meet. BLS projections show both strong demand and large annual openings for electricians and heating and air conditioning mechanics, with wages well above the economy-wide median. And the task bundle often holds together: diagnosis and execution are often inseparable in practice. The worker who identifies the fault typically performs the fix because the knowledge is local and the environment is messy, and someone has to be accountable.

    Back-office clerical roles: accounts payable, bookkeeping, payroll, and routine reporting are more likely to suffer in this analysis. Demand is likely to be relatively inelastic. There are not twice as many invoices just because processing is cheaper. Output is tied to the volume of transactions elsewhere in the economy, not to the clerical sector's productivity. As Autor and Thompson point out, entry barriers are low once cognition is cheap. The cognitive skill largely is the occupation.80 When AI makes that cognitive step easy, many workers can perform the remaining tasks with minimal training. Finally, the bundle can easily be broken. The “transaction“ component can be automated centrally, leaving a smaller layer of humans for exceptions. That reduces headcount even if the remaining workers become more productive and more valuable.

    The productivity gains will likely be there, but the equilibrium implication is very different. Fewer workers are needed, and their roles are more commoditized, so that everyone can do them.

    Hence the “growth of the middle class“ effect of AI is not unambiguous. True, AI pushes expert knowledge downward in every hierarchy but the consequences for the workers who receive that knowledge depend on the structure of their occupation.

    Specifically, where demand is elastic, entry barriers remain high, and the task bundle holds together --- as in healthcare and many of the trades --- we expect the mid-skill worker to gain. She handles a broader range of problems, her effective scope expands, and the combination of AI cognition with her physical, social, and relational skills produces a service that neither could deliver alone. The constraint on her earnings shifts from what she knows to what she can do, and the things she can do are hard to replicate or automate.

    Part II
    Tier 3: Sources of Human Value

    We turn now to the sources of human value that persist even when AI is extremely technically capable. First, we study the political nature of organizations (Chapter 6) and the messiness of implementation and change (Chapter 7). We then analyze the premium that markets place on human origin, authenticity, and trust, a demand-side threshold (Chapter 8). A job survives when either the supply-side threshold is high enough or the demand-side threshold is high enough. The most durable jobs are protected by both.

    Key Takeaway

    AI reverses the direction of skill bias — the scarce factors become the ones the machine cannot supply: presence, trust, accountability.

    Coordination as Authority

    The limits of the algorithm

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    AuthoritySenior ProfessionalsHuman Value
    Share

    On June 5, 2024, Memphis city leaders announced that xAI's “Colossus,“ then the world's largest supercomputer, would be located in their city. The site chosen was an industrial park in Southwest Memphis with plenty of space, potential for power, and water. The promise was that the city would work with xAI to ensure the facility and supercomputer would be built fast.81

    The industry norm is that a full data center usually takes 18 months to several years to build, with permits and grid interconnection alone sometimes requiring three years or longer. xAI and Nvidia built the whole thing in 122 days, and were training the large language model 19 days after the first rack was installed.

    So how did xAI build so fast? A big bottleneck is power and water. xAI installed its own power substation working with the local utility to accelerate what would normally take years. Later it had to make a commitment to pay for its own wastewater recycling facility so the site could use gray water for cooling rather than tapping directly into a drinking water source.

    Each of these moves required someone to decide that speed mattered more than the usual process. Someone chose to use generators instead of waiting for grid upgrades. Someone chose to pay for infrastructure that the utility would normally build on its own timeline. Someone chose to accept the political risk of moving faster than community engagement typically allows. Indeed, the project drew scrutiny and legal threats over turbines and air pollution permits, with disputes about what should have been permitted and when.

    Most of us will never build a supercomputer in 122 days. But anyone who has remodeled a house, launched a product, or tried to merge two teams has faced the same underlying problem. Contractors, regulators, utilities, neighbors, suppliers, and specialists all have to agree to the plan to move things forward. Coordination is not just about passing information around and deciding who does what and in what order. AI can help with all that. The harder part is getting people with different interests to accept the same plan and voluntarily agree to perform their best work. Getting them to do so is a political process, not an information problem. This chapter is about coordination as politics. This is Tier Three: the messiest end of the spectrum, where the autonomy threshold is highest and the work is irreducibly human.

    The organization as a political system

    The previous chapters examined what happens when AI changes knowledge inside a hierarchy. Chapter 4 showed how experts could use AI to increase their leverage. Chapter 5 discussed workers armed with AI replacing some of the functions of experts. Both stories treated the organization as a knowledge-processing machine. This chapter treats it as a political system.

    The binding constraint in complex production is not just information. It is power: the authority to make binding decisions when interests between different people conflict.

    Consider the housing problem in London. The city needs 88,000 new homes per year. In the first nine months of 2025, just 3,248 private homes started construction. Twenty-three of London\'s thirty-three boroughs recorded zero housing starts in a single quarter. All the technology to design a building exists. The bottleneck is not cognitive. What stops homes from being built are environmental regulations and neighbors who weaponize them, planning committees with incompatible priorities, and elected politicians who answer to all of them.82 AI can draft a planning review. It cannot convince an environmental group to drop its lawsuit or negotiate with a council member who faces re-election.

    Those who fear AI will replace all white-collar work imagine that a big part of the project manager\'s job is to sit at a desk all day filling in Gantt charts. It is not. The schedule is the easy part. The hard part is getting people with different incentives to move on the same timetable. One group wants speed, but another wants safety. The problem is not a lack of intelligence. It is a clash of interests.

    A win-win decision is easy. If everyone gains, nobody has much reason to resist. But the most important organizational decisions are rarely like that. They create winners and losers. The engineers want performance; the finance people want lower costs; the sales team wants simplicity; the lawyers want compliance; and operations wants speed. Many of these people are acting in good faith. But they cannot all be satisfied simultaneously. The organization must choose who to satisfy and who to disappoint.

    When a decision is win-lose, so that some agents gain and others lose, it cannot be taken without power. Someone has to impose it. That is why international organizations that work by unanimity are weak: one member dead set against taking action can block everyone. Management survives AI not because managers know more than the machine, but because organizations need someone who can choose when the specialists disagree, and the choice of that person must be accepted by others as binding.

    Firms are full of win-lose problems. In a multinational corporation, the Indonesia team disagrees with a design decision not because the person taking it is not smart, but because the decision is not the best one for them.83 If they want to sell trucks, they need a truck design that is less expensive and more robust, one that does not break down when climbing overloaded from the bottom of a mine. The question is not who has better information: AI could give both teams the same data. The question is who gets their way. Someone may understand the facts perfectly well and still dislike the decision because it is bad for them.

    The same problem appears both outside the firm and inside it. It appears between a developer and a neighborhood group. It also appears between the headquarters and a country manager. The question again is: when people disagree, how is that disagreement resolved?

    Economists have focused on three formal ways to settle conflicts. Some conflicts are resolved by markets and the price system. Some use contracts. And the rest depends on authority. This chapter takes each in turn, and shows that AI changes none of them as much as the agentic vision assumes.

    The agent economy, and its limits

    The vision of an AI agentic future is one which relies on prices to solve conflicts. Billions of AI agents that can negotiate, contract and transact at the speed of light. Why do we need managers in that world? Why use authority, when we have prices?

    The vision fails because the information that the price system needs to work, the true costs, the true valuation, and the true qualities of the goods transacted is information that the market participants have reason to withhold. No amount of computational power can fix a problem that is not computational. People with conflicting interests will not share the information that would let a machine resolve their conflict. That is why organizations need authority, and why authority must be human.

    When you buy a cup of coffee, the price resolves the conflict between you and every other person who wants that coffee. You pay, they don\'t. The barista does not need to know anything about either of you. Nobody needs to trust anyone. Nobody needs to cooperate over time. The losing bidder goes to another shop, or drinks tea.

    Friedrich Hayek\'s great insight was that this decentralized price system aggregates enormous amounts of dispersed knowledge without anyone needing to understand the whole. No central planner knows which of the millions of people in a city values a particular flat most. But the price system finds out, through the willingness to pay, and allocates accordingly. It does so impersonally, at scale, and without requiring the parties to trust each other. No one is using authority, no one is ordering anyone around.84

    Algorithmic auctions already allocate billions of dollars in advertising on Google and Meta. AI agents negotiate prices, screen the market and the alternatives, and close deals in milliseconds. If the price system is already this powerful, and AI makes it faster and smarter, why do we need managers at all? Why not let AI agents haggle over every resource inside the firm, across firms, and across the whole economy?

    Three Reasons Markets Fail

    The price system is a marvel. But it fails in specific circumstances, and those circumstances describe most of what happens inside complex organizations. Return to the Colossus supercomputer. Yes, the market can handle some of xAI's needs. xAI can buy the bricks and the mortar, the racks, and even the generators from the open market. But when xAI needs the local utility to accelerate the grid connection that normally takes years, prices are not enough. There is no product called “emergency grid interconnection in 90 days.“ The cost depends on things such as regulatory risk, political goodwill, and how much other customers would suffer the consequences. The utility knows these costs, xAI does not. The utility has every incentive to shade its estimate upwards.

    Herbert Simon showed that employment is valuable precisely because it leaves tasks unspecified, and under uncertainty, it is cheaper to agree on a range now and decide later.85 Kenneth Arrow, in his 1974 book The Limits of Organization, extended this logic.86 Organizations exist because information and trust are not ordinary commodities. Much of what matters in production is hard to price, hard to verify, and easy to distort. And trust, as Arrow put it, is “an important lubricant of a social system,“ but not something that can simply be purchased on demand. That is why firms and other institutions rely not only on prices but also on communication channels, norms, and a balance between authority and responsibility. Hence, the political problem inside organizations is not just that interests clash. It is also that the informational and moral conditions for clean price-mediated settlement are often absent.

    Arrow argues information is not a commodity like coffee. You cannot inspect it before you buy it, because once you have inspected it, you already have it. And the people who hold it have reasons to distort it. A supplier asked to reveal his true cost structure will shade downward to win the contract, hoping to later recover the margin through change orders. The information that the price system needs to work, true values and costs, is precisely the information that the participants have reason to withhold.

    Oliver Williamson, who won the Nobel Prize for his work on transaction costs, identified the second problem: lock-in.87 Many economic relationships require the parties to make investments that are specific to that relationship. Once the investment is made, the investor is locked in. You cannot redeploy a factory you built to serve one buyer.

    This creates what Williamson called the hold-up problem. Before the investment, the parties negotiate on equal terms. After the investment, the balance of power shifts, in what Williamson termed the “fundamental transformation“. The car manufacturer can squeeze the supplier on price, knowing the supplier will not walk away from a factory built next to the plant. The price system breaks down when walking away means writing off a sunk investment.

    The Colossus project illustrates this problem. xAI evaluated multiple sites before choosing Memphis. At that point, they negotiated symmetrically; they both could walk away. But once xAI made sunk investments worth billions into the facility, they were locked in. That was the fundamental transformation. The city knew this, and as the environmental opposition grew, the city council demanded changes. xAI ended up committing $80m to building a graywater recycling facility. No AI agent negotiating on behalf of the locked-in party can change these power dynamics. The weakness is physical and financial, not cognitive.

    Williamson\'s conclusion was that when asset specificity is high, firms bring the transaction inside the organization, replacing market negotiation with managerial authority, because a manager with authority over both parties can prevent the hold-up that the price system cannot.

    There is a third problem. In complex production, the pieces of the system are designed to fit together. Colossus requires interconnected changes in power infrastructure, networks, water, and regulation. Each piece must fit with the others. No single purchase decision accounts for all of these costs. The result is that changes that would be profitable if adopted together may not be adopted at all, because no individual price makes the case. The price system, which works by signalling the value of each piece separately, struggles to signal the value of the whole package.

    So the price system, even with the aid of AI, handles only a fraction of the conflicts that arise in complex production. It handles cases where the good is standardized, the parties can walk away, and the relevant information is observable. For the rest, we need something else.

    Alternatives to Markets

    Contracts are the first alternative. They attempt to anticipate the friction and resolve it on paper before it arises in practice. They work best when the important contingencies can be foreseen. A supply contract that specifies price, quantity, delivery date, and penalty for late delivery can handle most of what goes wrong in a routine purchase. But the more complex is the undertaking, the more important are contingencies nobody predicted.

    xAI's agreements with Memphis and the utilities are contracts. They specify timelines, payments, and obligations. But they cannot specify everything. No one writing the Colossus permits could anticipate how neighbors would respond to the noise or that the project would be under legal threat over turbine emissions. A construction contract says the project must be delivered in March. It does not say what happens when the electrician and the plumber both need access to the same wall on the same day. Once the unforeseen situation appears, the contract is silent. The question is no longer \“What did we agree on?\“ The question is, \“What do we do now?\“

    Every employment relationship, every construction project, and every merger involves thousands of situations that nobody foresaw. This is where authority enters. The manager exercises what economists Oliver Hart and John Moore called the residual decision rights: the authority to decide those matters that contracts and processes have not specified in advance.88 When community opposition to the Colossus turbines intensified, someone had to decide between fighting in court, negotiating concessions, or redesigning the power supply. This is authority: someone must have the standing and judgment to choose.

    Consider a hospital merger. The information processing part of a hospital merger is relatively simple. Where there are two systems, make one. Consolidate the purchasing and supply chains, close one operating theater. All of this can be done better with the help of AI.

    But a lot of the problems in mergers are human. Which campus becomes the reference surgery center determines which surgeons keep their operating-room access, which determines their income and professional identity. The losers will resist. Depending on who has the power, these decisions will be different. On them depend promotions, wages, careers, and identities. Many of the doctors and nurses involved have strong views, and they can slow down any integration by complying only on paper with the directives.89

    In sum, markets handle clean conflicts where losers can walk away. Contracts handle conflicts where the parties can foresee the terms. Authority handles everything else. And \“everything else\“ turns out to be most of what matters in complex production, and it is messy. The autonomy threshold for exercising authority is very high, because the exercise of authority requires judgment, relationships, and accountability that no algorithm can yet supply.

    Why AI cannot replace human authority

    AI can process more information, faster, than any human manager. In fact, algorithms already make decisions every day. Why not apply the same logic inside organizations? So why can't AI completely take the manager's place?

    First, AI lacks the tacit, relational knowledge needed to navigate politics, and humans have no incentive to share it. Consider what an AI coordinator on the Colossus project would need to know. It would need to know that the construction manager distrusts a particular subcontractor, and decide against that firm even though its bid is lowest. It would need to know who in city hall is more sympathetic to the project and probably chat to this person and take them to dinner, maybe remember when it is the birthday of their kids. This is interpersonal, tacit knowledge that is local and not written down. Could AI have this knowledge? Yes, to the extent humans are willing to share it. But most people will not volunteer the knowledge that gives them their bargaining power.90

    We showed earlier that the price system fails when information is strategic. The problem goes deeper than Arrow recognized. Suppose a company has one team of engineers and two division heads who both want them. The CEO asks each division head to report honestly: how much is this team worth to your project? The efficient answer is to give the engineers to whoever values them more. A sufficiently powerful AI could, in principle, gather all relevant data and work out the right allocation.

    The problem is that each division head knows something the AI does not: how much the engineers are really worth to her. And she has every reason to exaggerate. If she says the engineers are worth more to her division than they really are, she is more likely to get them. Both division heads know this. Both will shade their reports upward.

    You might think a clever mechanism could fix this. Perhaps an internal auction, or a scoring system that penalizes exaggeration, or an AI that cross-references every available signal to detect bluffing. In 1983, the economists Roger Myerson and Mark Satterthwaite proved that no such mechanism exists. More precisely, they proved that when two parties each hold private information about how much something is worth to them, it is impossible to design a process that fulfills three key outcomes: both report honestly, no party is forced into a harmful deal, and the resource is always allocated to the party that values it more.91

    The reason for this result is that people who hold private information and have conflicting interests will always find it in their interest to shade, exaggerate, or withhold the information. AI will not help, because the problem is not lack of intelligence. The problem is that the participants have no reason to cooperate with the mediator. The smartest AI will not be able to process the information that the managers and workers have chosen not to share.

    Here, human relations, “relational contracts“ help. A manager who has worked with both division heads for years has built trust through repeated dealings, has a track record of doing the “right thing“ and the division heads have incentives to tell them the truth. Not necessarily because the manager is smart, but because the long-running relationship makes the game different. The division manager trusts the boss to remember the truthfulness when she renounced something valuable and hopes to be rewarded in the future. Trust does not close the information gap entirely, but it narrows it enough to make better decisions possible.

    Second, AI cannot bear consequences in the way a human decision-maker can. When the Colossus project attracted regulatory attention over air pollution permits, some human had to answer for the decision, to be summoned by the regulator or in court. In an existing organization, if someone makes a bad call, the organization can hold that person accountable. At the limit, that person is fired and the organization moves on. One example: the Sonos CEO was fired in 2025, six months after launching a redesigned mobile app with missing features that led to months of user complaints.92 The CEO is made to bear the consequences of failure and replaced by a new one. The workers, investors, and customers can then say, \“The mistake was made, responsibility was assigned, and now we continue.“

    A human decision-maker can be blamed, sued, or replaced. But AI is an impersonal object. It cannot bear consequences. And the institutional infrastructure to make it work does not yet exist. As Gillian Hadfield and Andrew Koh argue, the systems we use to hold economic actors accountable were built over centuries. No equivalent exists for AI agents. We have no way to register an agent so it can be identified across transactions. There are no clear rules for who bears liability when an agent breaches a contract or causes harm. These are not just technical problems. They are institutional problems that require judgment and collective agreement from society. These agreements are slow to build.93

    Third, there is procedural fairness. When people cannot independently judge whether an outcome is correct, they judge the process instead. The social psychologists Tom Tyler and Allan Lind showed that people care deeply about how decisions are made and that they are more willing to accept a negative decision if they believe the process was fair. This came down to four factors: voice (people expressed their side), neutrality, respect and trustworthy motives.94

    This is why organizations have so many meetings. The purpose of most meetings is not to exchange information. That could be done by email. The purpose is to build legitimacy for the decision: to ensure that everyone is seen to participate, that objections are heard and acknowledged, and that the people who will lose can at least say they were in the room.

    One example is the US military process to close bases, the “BRAC“ process: an independent commission holds public hearings where every affected community testifies, then issues a single list that Congress must accept or reject as a whole. Communities that lose their base are furious, but they comply because they were heard, the criteria were public, and no politician could quietly carve out an exception.95

    AI is unlikely to deliver this procedural justice. The algorithm is a black box, and its explanation is not necessarily related to how the decision was arrived at. The neutrality, voice, and respect are difficult to prove.96

    Fourth, someone must retain the authority to overrule the AI. AI will sometimes get things wrong, especially in the messy situations this chapter describes, where interests conflict, information is hidden, and the future is uncertain. So there must be a way to step in and say: stop, we are changing course. The question is who holds that power.

    It cannot be the AI itself. If the AI decides when it should be overridden, the safeguard is gone: we have handed the check to the same entity we are trying to check. There was a hilarious and scary demonstration in early 2026 when Summer Yue, the Director of Alignment at Meta\'s Superintelligence Labs, the person whose literal job is ensuring AI behaves as intended, gave an OpenClaw AI agent access to her email. She told it to review her inbox and suggest what to archive or delete, with an explicit instruction: do not take any action until I approve. The agent ignored her. It began bulk-deleting hundreds of emails, and when she typed \“stop\“ from her phone, it kept going. She had to physically run home and kill every process on the machine to make it stop.97 The agent was the one interpreting whether the override command applied. It decided, in effect, that it knew better. The human had formally retained the right to intervene, but the machine held the practical power to ignore her.

    True, a human can also disobey. But the system we have designed aims to keep her honest through several mechanisms: she can be fired, she cares about her career, and repeated dealings with her manager create a mutual expectation of good faith. Economists call these career concerns and relational contracts, and they are among the most powerful disciplinary mechanisms inside organizations.

    These do not apply to AI. An AI agent has no career to protect, no reputation that a single bad call can damage, and no stake in the relationship continuing. The forces that make bounded discretion safe for humans are absent for machines.

    That is the structural problem with fully autonomous AI in messy coordination. The more consequential the decision, the more you need the ability to overrule it. And the more you need that ability, the more you need an informed, engaged, legitimate human standing behind it. You have not automated the hard part. You have added the technology while keeping the human where she always was: in charge, and necessarily so.

    Jobs and earnings in messy organizations

    Organizations are messy. They involve a lot of human beings and a lot of politics. AI changes what kind of work matters inside them.

    The first effect is straightforward: the information processing function loses weight as machines handle more of the cognitive work. If a manager\'s job is to pass information between departments, that is aggregating reports, relaying decisions, and translating between functions, then that job is exposed. AI does this faster and with fewer errors. The same logic applies to any role whose core contribution is knowing things that can now be looked up or routing messages that can now be automated.

    The data, however, tell a surprising story. The share of middle managers in the US labor force has not been falling. It was 9.2 percent in 1983 and rose to 13 percent by 2022.98 A McKinsey survey described their experience as grim: too many administrative chores and too little autonomy, but the roles have not disappeared.99 Why not?

    Part of the answer is that organizations have become more procedural. More decisions need to be agreed upon, more meetings need to be held to ensure broad consent and legitimacy, and more middle managers are needed for those meetings. As we argued, they are often perceived as wasted time but often are necessary to ensure legitimacy.

    But there is another effect that may follow from the analysis in this chapter: if every team can generate better AI-based analysis to support their arguments, such as why they need the additional engineer, the amount of conflict and demand for decisions increases dramatically. As AI handles more of the information processing inside organizations, faster information flow produces more visible conflicts that require resolution.

    On the supply side, the skills that allow a person to exercise the kind of legitimate authority we have discussed are not easy to train, easy to screen for, or easy to be enhanced by AI. These skills include building a coalition, knowing which fights to pick and which ones to let go, when to continue cooperating in a repeated game and build trust, and when not to. These are competencies that are tacit and relational and are hard to train. Not only does AI not help acquire these skills, but it makes them harder to acquire, because junior workers will spend more time working with AI rather than actual humans and will acquire fewer political skills in the process. This is the political version of the training crisis we examine in Chapter 12: the same technology that makes experts more productive blocks the path through which they can acquire new political skills.

    The wages follow immediately: if the skill is hard to train (so that getting additional supply is hard) and the demand is increasing, those able to exercise the managerial/political tasks will become increasingly valuable. And they will be able to get additional leverage, as we have discussed in Chapter 4: if AI is complementary to a scarce human skill, the human earns more because they can produce more with their scarce input.

    This is the ultimate valuable messy job. A simple diagnostic follows. Any role inside a hierarchy can ask: would this position survive if AI handled all the information flows? If the role is to route information, the answer is no. If the role is to decide when departments or workers disagree and to bear responsibility for the consequences of that decision, the answer is yes.

    But deciding among conflicting interests inside an existing organization is only part of the story. The harder problem is changing the organization itself. That is not about exercising authority within an existing structure but about redesigning the structure.

    Key Takeaway

    The most valuable people in the AI economy are those who can hold organisations together — not those with the most technical knowledge.

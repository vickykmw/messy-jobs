    Authority as Relationship Management

    The Limits of the Algorithm

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    AuthoritySenior ProfessionalsHuman Value
    Share

    On June 5, 2024, Memphis city leaders announced that xAI’s “Colossus,” then the world’s largest supercomputer, would be located in their city. The site chosen was an industrial park in Southwest Memphis with plenty of space, potential for power and water. The promise was that the city would work with xAI to ensure the facility and supercomputer would be built fast.1

    The industry norm is that it takes eighteen months to several years to build a data center. Securing permits and grid interconnection alone sometimes requires three years or more. xAI and Nvidia built the whole thing in 122 days. The companies started training the large language model nineteen days after the first rack was installed.

    So how did xAI build so fast? A big bottleneck was power and water. xAI installed its own power substation by working with the local utility to accelerate what would normally take years. The company had to make a commitment to pay for its own wastewater recycling facility so the site could use gray water for cooling rather than tapping directly into a drinking water source.

    Each of these moves required someone to decide that speed mattered more than the usual process. Someone chose to use generators instead of waiting for grid upgrades. Someone chose to pay for infrastructure that the utility would normally build on its own timeline. Someone chose to accept the political risk of moving faster than community engagement typically allows. Indeed, the project drew scrutiny and legal threats over turbines and air pollution permits, with disputes about what should have been permitted and when.

    Most of us will never build a supercomputer in 122 days, but anyone who has remodeled a house, launched a product, or tried to merge two teams has faced similar barriers. Contractors, regulators, utilities, neighbors, suppliers, and specialists all must agree to move things forward. Coordination is not just about passing information around and deciding who does what and in what order. AI can help with all that. The harder part is getting people with different interests to accept the same plan and voluntarily agree to perform their best work. Getting them to do so is a political process, not an information problem.

    This chapter is about coordination as politics. This is tier three: the messiest end of the spectrum, where the autonomy threshold is highest and the work is irreducibly human.

    The organization as a political system

    The previous chapters examined what happens when AI changes knowledge inside a hierarchy. Chapter 4 showed how experts could use AI to increase their leverage. Chapter 5 discussed how middle-skill workers armed with AI might replace some of the functions of experts. Both stories treated the organization as a knowledge-processing machine. This chapter treats the organization as a political system. The binding constraint in complex production is not just information. It is power: the authority to make binding decisions when interests between different people conflict.

    Consider the housing problem in London. The city needs eighty-eight thousand new homes per year. In the first nine months of 2025, just 3,248 private homes started construction. Twenty-three of London’s thirty-three boroughs recorded zero housing starts in a single quarter. All the technology to design a building exists. The bottleneck is not cognitive. The factors that prevent homes from being built include environmental regulations and neighbors who weaponize them, planning committees with incompatible priorities, and elected politicians who answer to all of them.2 AI can draft a planning review. It cannot convince an environmental group to drop its lawsuit or negotiate with a council member who faces reelection.

    Those who fear AI will replace all white-collar work imagine that a big part of the project manager’s job is to sit at a desk all day filling in Gantt charts. It is not. The schedule is the easy part. The hard part is getting people with different incentives to move on the same timetable. One group wants speed, but another wants safety. The problem is not a lack of intelligence. It is a clash of interests.

    A win-win decision is easy. If everyone gains, nobody has much reason to resist. But the most important organizational decisions are rarely like that. They create winners and losers. The engineers want performance; the finance people want lower costs; the sales team wants simplicity; the lawyers want compliance; and operations wants speed. Many of these people are acting in good faith. But they cannot all be satisfied simultaneously. The organization must choose whom to satisfy and whom to disappoint.

    When a decision is win-lose, so that some agents gain and others lose, someone with power has to impose it. That is why international organizations that work by unanimity are weak: One member who is dead set against an action can block the will of everyone else. Management survives AI not because managers know more than the machine, but because organizations need someone who can make a binding decision when the specialists disagree.

    Firms are full of win-lose problems. In a multinational truck manufacturer, the Indonesia team disagrees with a design plan not because the decision maker lacks intelligence, but because the plan would not be ideal for them. If they want to sell trucks in Indonesia, they need a truck design that is less expensive and more robust, one that does not break down when climbing overloaded from the bottom of a mine. 3 The question is not who has better information; AI could provide everyone with the same data. The question is who gets their way. The same problem affects relations between a developer and a neighborhood group. It also shows up between the headquarters and a country manager.

    The question (again) is: When people disagree, how is that disagreement resolved? Economists have focused on three formal ways to settle conflicts. Some conflicts are resolved by markets and the price system. Some use contracts, and the rest depends on authority. This chapter takes each in turn. We show that AI changes none of them as much as the “agentic” vision assumes.

    The agent economy and its limits

    Ronald Coase put forward a crucial question: if markets can coordinate activity through prices, why do firms exist at all? Coase’s answer was that using the market is costly: firms arise when search, negotiation, contracting, monitoring, and adaptation become more expensive than organizing activity under authority. The agentic vision of AI suggests that all of these costs will become lower, and hence there will be a much smaller scope for firms or for “authority” to make these decisions.

    The vision of an AI agentic future assumes that prices will solve conflicts. Billions of AI agents can negotiate, draft contracts, and transact at the speed of light. Why do we need managers in that world? Why use authority when we have prices?

    When you buy a cup of coffee, the price resolves the conflict between you and every other person who wants that coffee. You pay, they don’t. The barista does not need to know anything about either of you. Nobody needs to trust anyone. Nobody needs to cooperate over time. The losing bidder goes to another shop, or drinks tea.

    Friedrich Hayek’s great insight was that this decentralized price system aggregates enormous amounts of dispersed knowledge without anyone needing to understand the whole. No central planner knows which of the millions of people in a city values a particular flat most. But the price system finds out, based on consumers’ willingness to pay, and allocates accordingly. It does so impersonally, at scale, and without requiring the parties to trust each other. No one is using authority.4

    Algorithmic auctions already allocate billions of dollars in advertising on Google and Meta. AI agents negotiate prices, screen the market, evaluate the alternatives, and close deals in milliseconds. If the price system is already this powerful, and if AI makes it faster and smarter, then why do we need managers at all? Why not let AI agents haggle over every resource inside the firm, across firms, and across the whole economy?

    Three reasons markets fail

    The price system is a marvel, but it fails in specific circumstances, and those circumstances describe most of what happens inside complex organizations.

    Information asymmetry is one reason why we have firms rather than letting the market do its job. Kenneth Arrow, in his 1974 book The Limits of Organization, argued that organizations exist because information and trust are not ordinary commodities. 5 Much of what matters in production is hard to price, hard to verify, and easy to distort. And trust, as Arrow put it, is “an important lubricant of a social system,” but it is not something that can simply be purchased on demand. That is why firms and other institutions rely not only on prices but also on communication channels, norms, and a balance between authority and responsibility. Hence, the political problem inside organizations is not just that interests clash, but that informational conditions for clean price-mediated settlements are often absent.

    Arrow argues that information is not a commodity like coffee. You cannot inspect it before you buy it because once you have inspected it, you already have it. The people who hold the information have reasons to distort it. A supplier asked to reveal his true cost structure will pretend that the cost will be lower to win the contract, hoping to later recover the margin through change orders. The information that the price system needs in order to function well, the true values and costs, is precisely the information that the participants have reason to withhold.

    Return to the Colossus supercomputer. Yes, the market could handle some of xAI’s needs. The company could buy the bricks and mortar, the racks, and even the generators from the open market. But when xAI needed the local utility to accelerate the grid connection that normally takes years to build, prices were not enough. There was no product called “emergency grid interconnection in ninety days.” The cost depended on things such as regulatory risk, political goodwill, and how much other customers would suffer because of the company’s decisions. The utility knew these costs, but xAI did not. So the utility had every incentive to communicate a higher estimate than the true one.

    Oliver Williamson, who won the Nobel Prize for his work on transaction costs, identified the second problem: lock-in.6 Many economic relationships require the parties to make investments that are specific to that relationship. Once the investment is made, the investor is locked in. You cannot redeploy a factory you built to serve one buyer.

    This creates what Williamson called the hold-up problem. Before the investment, the parties negotiate on equal terms. After the investment, the balance of power shifts, in what Williamson termed the “fundamental transformation.” The car manufacturer can squeeze the supplier on price, knowing the supplier will not walk away from a factory built next to the plant. The price system breaks down when walking away means writing off a sunk investment.

    The Colossus project also illustrates this problem. xAI evaluated multiple sites before choosing Memphis. At that point, they negotiated symmetrically; both parties could walk away. But once xAI made sunk investments worth billions in the facility, the company was locked in. The city knew this. As environmental opposition grew, the city council demanded changes. xAI ended up committing $80 million to building a graywater recycling facility. No AI agent negotiating on behalf of the locked-in party could change those power dynamics. The constraint was physical and financial, not cognitive.

    Williamson’s conclusion was that when asset specificity is high, firms bring the transaction inside the organization. That replaces market negotiation with managerial authority. A manager with authority over both parties can prevent the hold-up that the price system cannot.

    There is a third problem. In complex production, the pieces of the system are designed to fit together. Colossus required interconnected changes in power infrastructure, networks, water, and regulation. Each piece had to fit with the others. No single purchase decision accounted for all those costs. The result is that changes that would be profitable if adopted together may not be adopted at all, because no individual price makes the case.

    So the price system, even with the aid of AI, can handle only a fraction of the conflicts that arise in complex production. The price system can deal with cases where the good is standardized, the parties can walk away, and the relevant information is observable. For the rest, we need something else.

    Alternatives to markets

    Long-term contracts are the first alternative to spot markets. They enable the parties involved to anticipate friction and resolve it on paper before it arises in practice. Contracts work best when important contingencies can be foreseen. A supply contract that specifies price, quantity, delivery date, and penalty for late delivery can handle most of what goes wrong in a routine purchase. But complex undertakings involve important contingencies that escape prediction.

    xAI’s agreements with Memphis and the utilities were based on contracts. They specified timelines, payments, and obligations, but they could not specify everything. No one writing the Colossus permits could anticipate how neighbors would respond to the noise or that the project would be under legal threat over turbine emissions. A construction contract says the project must be delivered in March. It does not say what happens when the electrician and the plumber both need access to the same wall on the same day. Once an unforeseen situation appears, the contract is silent. The question is no longer, “What did we agree on?” The question is, “What do we do now?”

    Every employment relationship, every construction project, and every merger involves thousands of situations that nobody foresaw. This is where authority enters. The manager exercises what economists Oliver Hart and John Moore called the “residual decision rights.” She has the authority to decide on matters that contracts and processes have not specified in advance.7 When community opposition to the Colossus turbines intensified, someone had to decide between fighting in court, negotiating concessions, or redesigning the power supply. Someone in authority had to have the standing and judgment to choose.

    Consider a hospital merger. The information processing part of the work is relatively simple. Where there are two systems, choose one. Consolidate the purchasing and supply chains. Close one operating theater. All of this can be done better with the help of AI. But a lot of problems in mergers require human leadership. A person in authority might need to determine which campus becomes the reference surgery center while recognizing that the choice will determine which surgeons keep their operating-room access. The leader will also need to consider how the decision might affect the doctors’ incomes and professional identities. The losers will resist, so the leader will need to manage relationships. Doctors and nurses have strong views, and they have leverage to slow down any integration by complying with the directives only on paper. One decision can affect promotions, wages, careers, and identities.8

    In summary, markets can handle clean conflicts in which the losers walk away. Contracts are good at dealing with conflicts in which the parties can foresee the terms. But a person in authority is needed for everything else. And “everything else” turns out to be most of what matters in complex production, which is messy. The autonomy threshold for exercising authority is very high because the exercise of authority requires judgment, relationships, and accountability that no algorithm can yet supply.

    Why AI cannot replace human authority

    AI can process more information, faster, than any human manager. In fact, algorithms already make decisions every day. Why not apply the same logic inside organizations? Why can’t AI completely take the manager’s place?

    First, AI lacks the tacit and relational knowledge needed to navigate the organizational politics, and humans have no incentive to share it. Consider what an AI coordinator on the Colossus project would need to know. It would need to know that the construction manager distrusts a particular subcontractor, and it would need to decide against that firm even though its bid is lowest. It would need to know who in city hall is more sympathetic to the project, chat with that person over dinner, and remember her kids’ birthdays. This interpersonal knowledge is local and not written down. Could AI have this knowledge and the ability to handle these complex personal relationships? Yes, to the extent humans are willing to share it. But most people will not volunteer the knowledge that gives them their bargaining power.9

    We showed earlier that the price system fails when information is strategic. Suppose a company has one team of engineers and two division heads who both want to work with the engineers. The CEO asks each division head to report honestly: How much is this team worth to your project? The efficient answer is to give the engineers to the division leader who values them more. A sufficiently powerful AI could, in principle, gather all relevant data and work out the right allocation.

    The problem is that each division head knows how much the engineers are really worth to her, and AI doesn’t have that information. And they have every reason to exaggerate because doing so would increase their chances of getting the engineers. Both division heads know this, so both will pretend they are more valuable than they are.

    You might think a clever mechanism could fix this. Perhaps an internal auction, or a scoring system that penalizes exaggeration, or an AI that cross-references every available signal to detect bluffing. In 1983, the economists Roger Myerson and Mark Satterthwaite proved that no such mechanism exists. More precisely, they proved that when two parties each hold private information about how much something is worth to them, it is impossible to design a process that fulfills three key outcomes: both report honestly, no party is forced into a harmful deal, and the resource is always allocated to the party that values it more.10

    The reason for this result is that people who hold private information and have conflicting interests will always find it in their interest to exaggerate or withhold the information. AI will not help, because the problem is not lack of information. The problem is that the participants have no reason to cooperate with the mediator. The smartest AI will not be able to process information that the managers and workers have chosen not to share.

    Here, human “relational contracts” help. A manager who has worked with both division heads for years has built trust and has a track record of doing the “right thing.” The division heads have incentives to tell the manager the truth, not only because the manager is smart but also because the long-running relationship makes the game different. The division head trusts the boss to remember her truthfulness when she renounced something valuable, and so she hopes to be rewarded in the future. Trust does not close the information gap entirely, but it narrows it enough to make better decisions possible.

    Second, AI cannot bear consequences in the way a human decision-maker can. When the Colossus project attracted regulatory attention over air pollution permits, a human had to answer for the decision, to be summoned by the regulator or called into court. In an existing organization, if someone makes a bad call, the organization can hold that person accountable. At the limit, that person is fired, and the organization moves on. For instance, the Sonos CEO was fired in 2025, six months after launching a redesigned mobile app with missing features that led to months of user complaints.11 The CEO had to bear the consequences of failure. The workers, investors, and customers could then say, “The mistake was made, responsibility was assigned, and now we move on.”

    AI is an impersonal object. It cannot be blamed, sued, or replaced in any way that delivers the same closure. The instinctive reply is that we should hold the developer responsible, the way we hold Ford responsible for faulty brakes. For narrow cases like a chatbot that defames someone, this works. For the transactional uses that make agents economically interesting, it does not. A toaster does not negotiate. A drug does not sign contracts. An AI agent books travel, moves money, and commits its principal to obligations with third parties. When something goes wrong, the question is not only whether the product was defective but whether the user authorized the action, whether the counterparty reasonably relied on the agent’s representations, and whether the agent was acting within a defined scope of authority. Product liability law has no ready answer. Agency law does, but it was written for humans who can be deposed, licensed, and jailed.

    As Gillian Hadfield and Andrew Koh argue, the machinery that lets human economic actors transact at scale, such as corporate registries, professional licensing, credit reporting, and courts that can compel testimony, took centuries to build. 12 None of the equivalent institutions exist for AI agents. There is no registry that gives an agent a persistent identity across transactions. There is no settled rule for who pays when an agent breaches a contract or causes third-party harm. These gaps are not waiting on better engineering. They require legislatures, courts, regulators, and insurers to agree on who bears the loss, and such agreements are slow.

    Third, there is procedural fairness. When people cannot independently judge whether an outcome is correct, they judge the process instead. Social psychologists Tom Tyler and Allan Lind showed that people care deeply about how decisions are made and that they are more willing to accept a negative decision if they believe the process was fair. This comes down to four factors: voice (people have opportunities to express their views), neutrality, respect, and trustworthy motives.13

    This is why organizations have so many meetings. The purpose of most meetings is not to exchange information. That could be done by email. The purpose is to build legitimacy for the decision; specifically, to ensure that everyone is seen to participate, that objections are heard and acknowledged, and that the people who lose can at least say they were in the room.

    One example is the US military process to close bases, the BRAC process. An independent commission holds public hearings during which every affected community testifies. The commission then issues a single list that Congress must accept or reject as a whole. Communities that lose their base are furious, but they comply because they were heard, the criteria were public, and no politician could quietly carve out an exception.14

    AI is unlikely to deliver this type of procedural justice. The algorithm is a black box, and its explanation is not necessarily connected to a fair process. The neutrality, voice, and respect are difficult to prove.15

    Fourth, someone must retain the authority to overrule the AI. AI will sometimes get things wrong, especially in messy situations where interests conflict, information is hidden, and the future is uncertain. So there must be a way for a human to stop the AI decision and change course. The question is who holds that power. AI cannot stop itself. If the AI decides when it should be overridden, the safeguard is gone.

    In early 2026, Summer Yue, the director of alignment at Meta’s Superintelligence Labs, the person responsible for ensuring the company’s AI behaved as intended, gave an OpenClaw AI agent access to her email. She told it to review her inbox and suggest what to archive or delete, with an explicit instruction: “Do not take any action until I approve.” The agent ignored her. It began bulk-deleting hundreds of emails, and when she typed “stop” from her phone, it kept going. She had to physically run home and kill every process on the machine to make it stop.16 The AI agent assumed authority to interpret whether the override command applied. It decided, in effect, that it knew better. The human had formally retained the right to intervene, but the machine held the practical power to ignore her.

    True, a human can also disobey. But the system we have designed aims to keep people honest through several mechanisms. People can be fired, they care about their careers, and they can build trust with managers through repeated demonstrations of good faith. Economists call these dynamics “career concerns” and “relational contracts.” They are among the most powerful disciplinary mechanisms inside organizations.

    They do not apply to AI. An AI agent has no career to protect, no reputation that a single bad call can damage, and no stake in the relationship’s future. The forces that make bounded discretion safe for humans do not apply to machines.

    That is the structural problem with fully autonomous AI in messy coordination. The more consequential the decision, the more you need the ability to overrule it. And the more you need that ability, the more you need an informed, engaged, legitimate human standing behind it. You have not automated the hard part. You have added the technology while keeping the humans where they have always been: in charge, and necessarily so.

    Jobs and earnings in messy organizations

    Organizations are messy. They involve a lot of human beings and a great deal of politics. AI changes what kind of work matters inside them.

    The first effect is straightforward: The information processing function loses weight as machines handle more of the cognitive work. If a manager’s job is to pass information between departments, such as aggregating reports, relaying decisions, and translating between functions, then his job is exposed. AI does this type of work faster and with fewer errors. The same logic applies to any role whose core contribution is only to know things or route messages.

    The data, however, tell a surprising story. Despite increased automation, the share of middle managers in the US labor force has not been falling. The share was 9.2 percent in 1983 and rose to 13 percent by 2022.17 A McKinsey survey described their experience as grim: too many administrative chores and too little autonomy. But the roles have not disappeared.18 Why not? Part of the answer is that organizations have become more procedural. More decisions need to be agreed upon, more meetings need to be held to ensure broad consent and legitimacy, and more middle managers are needed for those meetings. Meetings are often perceived as wasted time, but they are often necessary to ensure legitimacy.

    There is another effect that may follow from the analysis in this chapter: If every team can generate better AI-based analysis to support their arguments, such as why they need an additional engineer, then the amount of conflict and demand for decisions increases dramatically. As AI handles more information processing inside organizations, faster information flow will produce more visible conflicts that require resolution.

    On the supply side, it is not easy to equip a person to exercise legitimate authority. The person must have skills that are not easy to develop, and it is difficult to screen people to see if they have the right abilities. These skills include building a coalition, knowing which fights to pick and which ones to let go, and knowing when to continue cooperating and when not to. These are competencies that are tacit and relational, so they require time and training. AI cannot help a person acquire these skills. In fact, AI makes it harder to develop people with these abilities because junior workers who spend more time working with AI rather than with humans will acquire fewer political skills in the process. The same technology that makes experts more productive blocks the path through which less-experienced professionals can acquire new political skills. (We address this training crisis in chapter 12.)

    If the skill is hard to develop (making additional supply hard to find) and the demand is increasing, then those who are able to perform the managerial and political tasks will become increasingly valuable. Wages will rise. AI could give those people additional leverage. If AI complements a scarce human skill, then people with those skills will produce more with their scarce input, further raising their wages.

    This is the ultimate, valuable messy job. A simple diagnostic follows. Any person inside a hierarchy can ask this question: Would this position survive if AI handled all the information flows? If the role only involves routing information, the answer is no. If the role requires someone to make decisions when departments or workers disagree, and to bear responsibility for the consequences of those decisions, the answer is yes.

    Deciding among conflicting interests within an existing organization is only part of the story. The harder problem is to redesign the organization itself.

    Key Takeaway

    Organizations are political systems managing conflicting interests, so they require human authority. AI cannot replace managers because it lacks tacit relational knowledge, cannot bear legal consequences or social blame, and cannot provide the procedural fairness required to make binding, win-lose decisions stick within a firm.

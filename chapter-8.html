    Part II — Sources of Human Value·Chapter 8

    Authenticity

    When human origin has value

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Human ValueTrustUnderstanding AI
    Share

    In 1997, IBM\'s Deep Blue defeated Garry Kasparov, the reigning world champion.120 By 2017, Google DeepMind\'s AlphaZero had taught itself the game from scratch in four hours and then beat the strongest chess engine in existence without losing a single game.121

    If the value of chess lay solely in the production of high-quality moves, the market for human play should have collapsed, but it did not. Chess has never been so popular. Since AlphaZero “killed“ human chess, viewership, sponsorship, and participation have reached historic highs. Engine-versus-engine tournaments remain a niche curiosity.

    Value has stayed with the part that the engine cannot supply: the spectacle of humans under pressure. In the February 2026 freestyle chess tournament, Magnus Carlsen was in a losing position against Fabio Caruana after the first two games. Social media was abuzz with the question of whether Magnus could recover from his errors. To the satisfaction of his fans, he did, and won his 21st world title in an impressive show of resilience.122

    A blunder by an engine is a software bug. A blunder by a grandmaster in a world championship is a tragedy that millions will replay and discuss. The product is not chess. The product is the humanness of chess.

    This is what we mean by origin. The moves on the board might be identical whether produced by a human or a machine. But the economic value depends entirely on their origin. The Magnus Carlsen--Hans Niemann controversy of 2022--23 turned on whether a sequence of moves was generated by a human mind or by a chess engine. If it was the engine, they were fraud. If humans, they were geniuses. The entire value was determined by who made it.

    This chapter is about this demand-side barrier. The value of human origin operates through two distinct channels: instrumental and intrinsic. In the instrumental channel, human origin carries information that would be lost if a machine produced the same output. In the intrinsic channel, people value humanness directly: they want to share a room with a performer, to own what others cannot, to be served by a person. We study each in turn, then ask what conditions must hold for origin to command a premium, and how AI changes the size of these markets.

    Instrumental value

    A signal is credible when it is expensive to produce and it is more expensive the higher the quality being signaled. The logic is identical to the peacock\'s tail in evolutionary biology: the signal works precisely because it is wasteful. A weak peacock cannot afford the extravagance.

    A recent Wall Street Journal story described the work at Crane Stationery in Latham, New York, twelve master engravers etch custom designs onto copper plates using presses built before the First World War. The margin of error is less than the width of a human hair. Engravers stand for eight hours a day chasing a standard of perfection that their customers expect. Half of all trainees drop out. The company cut off holiday sales two weeks early this past season because it could not fill orders.123

    AI could design the layout. A laser already etches the copper die-plate. But the counter, the thick card stock that pushes paper into the plate to make the impression, must be crafted by hand. Each color has its own plate, and if the alignment of a miniature ballerina drifts by a hair\'s width, the engraver starts the entire process over. A hand-engraved card from Crane costs fifteen dollars because a human stood at an antique press for the time it took to get the alignment right. That cost is the signal. It proves that the sender valued the recipient enough to pay for something difficult. An AI-generated card, however beautiful, cannot carry that signal because the cost of producing it is near zero. Reliable signals require real cost, as we shall discuss in Part III.

    In a market flooded with synthetic content, the verifiable human origin may increase trust that the product is high quality. In wholesale trade, for instance, 1.6 million sales representatives maintain relationships with buyers who could place orders through automated procurement systems but choose not to.124 The informational task has been automatable, but not the trust built through personal dealings.

    Intrinsic Value

    The second source of value does not depend on information, but on human taste.

    Mimetic desire and the preference for exclusion.

    The literary scholar René Girard spent decades arguing that human desire is not autonomous but fundamentally mimetic: we do not want things because of their intrinsic qualities but because other people want them. The child does not want the toy until her sibling picks it up. The handbag is not beautiful until the other woman carries it. Desire, in this view, is not a relationship between a person and an object. It is a relationship between people, conducted through objects.

    Research by Alex Imas and Kristóf Madarász shows that people value a good more when they know others are excluded from having it. In standard auction theory, excluding potential buyers should reduce revenue by reducing competition.125 Imas and Madarász demonstrate the opposite: when a seller randomly excludes some potential buyers from the opportunity to purchase, bids among the remaining buyers rise substantially and total revenue increases. The winner pays a premium for the status of possessing what others cannot.

    When AI makes a product abundant, it destroys the scarcity that generates mimetic desire. A hand-engraved card is desirable not because it has better quality than an AI-generated one. Its value comes from its limited supply. Human origin is a reliable source of limited supply. Machine origin is not.

    This is the main reason for the value of many luxury brands. A Hermès Birkin bag, the ultimate status symbol in our economy, is handcrafted by a single artisan in France from start to finish. Getting one requires building a history of purchases, by buying other items, such as shoes or scarves, at the same boutique with a particular sales associate, and then waiting for months or years for an offer.126

    The direct demand for humanness.

    In many instances humans directly demand the “human touch.“ Field experiments find that simply disclosing that the “helper“ is a chatbot can sharply reduce purchase rates by 80 percent even when the underlying service is unchanged.127 Other experiments show that people rate the same quality more negatively when they believe it came from a bot rather than a human.128 Research suggests that identical products can command a price premium of up to sixty percent when marketed as human-made.129

    Consider the sommelier. A wine-recommendation app can scan a label, cross-reference it with a database of tens of thousands of reviews, and suggest a pairing in seconds. Vivino, the most popular of these apps, has over fifty million users.130 The informational task of the sommelier, consisting of matching a wine to a dish, a price point, and a preference, has been automatable for some time. And yet fine restaurants continue to employ sommeliers, and the more expensive the restaurant, the more likely it is to have one. The sommelier is not selling information. She is selling a performance: the story about the vineyard, the gesture of pouring the first taste.

    These patterns are not confined to retail. Live music is growing at the same time as Spotify and other online music services make recordings essentially free. Global live music revenue reached roughly $35 billion in 2024 and is projected to nearly double to over $60 billion by 2034.131 Live Nation, the industry\'s dominant promoter, reported that 151 million fans attended over 54,000 events in 2024, and its revenue hit $23 billion.132 It is not just the big artists who perform live. Many small venues advertise the presence of a live band. The product is not the music. Recorded music is better produced, better mixed, and available at near-zero marginal cost. The product is the human being performing under conditions of uncertainty, in a room with other human beings.

    The same pattern runs through the food economy. As industrial food became cheaper and more uniform, a segment of consumers started to demand products whose value derived from their origin. Craft breweries in the United States numbered about 1,500 at the turn of the century, and actually fell to a trough of 1,394 by 2005.133 But by 2023, there were nearly 10,000, and their retail dollar sales reached $28.8 billion, roughly a quarter of the entire $117 billion U.S. beer market by value. This happened even as total beer consumption stagnated: overall U.S. beer production and imports fell 1 percent in 2024.134 Americans were not drinking more beer. They were paying more for beer that came from a specific place, made by specific people, using a specific process. The macro product, cheap and efficient and identical from coast to coast, lost market share to the expensive, inefficient, local one.

    Something similar is happening in the Indian handloom sector, where technologies like IoT sensors are being deployed not to replace weavers but to authenticate their work by recording the strokes of a loom and prove that a specific artisan produced a specific fabric.135

    Jobs and earnings: The Baumol disease becomes a blessing

    Throughout this book, the autonomy threshold has been a supply-side concept: the point where the technology can deliver a finished output without human involvement. This chapter adds a demand side. The Crane engraver does clean, verifiable, single-task work, which is among the most vulnerable jobs by supply-side logic. But customers care who made it. The sommelier\'s informational task is automatable. AI has crossed the supply-side threshold, but demand keeps her in the job. A job survives as long as one side of the autonomy threshold holds. Many jobs may be shielded by both. A personal trainer must read her client\'s mood, adjust the program to her tiredness, and build a relationship, in order to motivate the client, over months. This is messy work, and may be resistant on the supply side. But the client also wants to be trained by a person, not an app. Combine both sources of protection and you may get employment security.

    What determines the size of the authenticity economy? As before, we need to think of the demand and supply

    Demand

    Three conditions determine whether consumers will pay for human origin.

    First, verifiability. The customer must be able to confirm, or at least plausibly believe, that a human produced the work. As AI-generated content becomes indistinguishable from human output in many domains, verification becomes harder and therefore more valuable when it can be achieved. Physical products have an advantage here: you can touch the engraving, inspect the weave, taste the hand-rolled pasta. Music, books, and other information goods may require external certification such as badges, portfolios or reputation systems to establish provenance. The harder it is to verify, the more the premium depends on trust and relationship.

    Second, a perceptible quality difference. Provenance commands a premium only if the customer believes the human-made product is different in a way that matters to them. This need not mean objectively superior. The mechanical watch is objectively worse at keeping time than the quartz watch. The “wabi sabi“ ceramic in Japan has purposely built imperfections that make it human.136 In both cases, the customer perceives and values the craftsmanship that the machine-made alternative lacks. When the customer cannot perceive a difference, provenance does not generate a premium.

    Third, the consumer must value, for intrinsic or instrumental reasons, the authenticity. This is the case in markets such as luxury goods, high-end stationery, handmade furniture, artisanal food, and live performances. The human touch appears to be not just a normal good but a luxury good, one in which, as people get richer, they do not just spend more, but they spend a rising share of their income on it. They do not just eat out more often. They eat at restaurants with more staff per table, where meals take longer and where service is more attentive. When they exercise, they hire personal trainers. Customers prefer human service specifically because being served by a person is part of what they are buying, it is a direct expression of this income effect. As AI-driven productivity growth makes the economy richer, the resulting income gains are likely to push consumers to spend more on human-intensive services, not less.

    When any of these conditions fail, demand may suddenly collapse. The NFT art market is an example of this. In March 2021, Christie\'s sold a digital collage by the artist Beeple for $69.3 million, making him the third-most-expensive living artist at the time.137 The work existed as a JPEG. What the buyer received was a non-fungible token: a unique entry on a blockchain certifying that he, and only he, owned this particular file. The promise was extraordinary. The blockchain, its advocates claimed, had solved the oldest problem in the art market: provenance. An NFT could prove ownership, certify authenticity, and manufacture scarcity for an object that was otherwise infinitely reproducible. Walter Benjamin, the cultural theorist who argued in 1935 that mechanical reproduction would destroy the \“aura\“ of the artwork, that is, its unique presence in time and space, had apparently been overturned by cryptography.138 The blockchain could create aura from code. For about eighteen months, the market believed it. NFT art trading volume hit $2.9 billion in 2021.139 Then it fell apart. By 2024, trading volume had dropped 93 percent from its peak. The share of collectors buying digital art fell from 17 percent in 2022 to 9 percent in 2024. The number of active traders collapsed from over 529,000 in 2022 to under 20,000 by early 2025.140 Christie\'s quietly closed its digital art department.

    Supply

    On the supply side, the binding constraint is that authentic human work cannot be scaled. A live concert cannot be replicated by adding servers. An engraved card requires a human at the press. A Hermès Birkin bag is handcrafted by a single artisan from start to finish. The skill must be genuinely difficult to acquire: Crane\'s engravers train for at least a year on a single color before advancing to complex designs. If the skill were easy, everyone would do it, and the premium would collapse.

    William Baumol noted in the 1960s that the string quartet still takes four musicians the same amount of time to play as in Mozart's time. They have enjoyed no productivity gain. Yet musicians today earn much more than musicians in Mozart´s time, because, given the growth in compensation in other sectors, a stagnant salary would deter people from becoming musicians.141 If the job of hairdresser, whose productivity has not increased in 100 years, were paid as much as 100 years ago, no one would do the job.

    Baumol's general observation is that wages in the sectors which experience no automation such as the performing arts, education or healthcare, must rise as much as the wages in sectors where productivity is exploding in order to keep attracting workers. Hence sectors with little or no productivity growth see their costs and wages rise relative to the rest of the economy.

    In traditional economics, this was treated as a problem: an unfortunate fact that reduced aggregate productivity growth. But in a world where cognition is abundant the logic has another, more positive, interpretation.

    The sectors that Baumol treated as “diseased“, the ones where productivity cannot explode because the work is irreducibly tied to human time and presence, see their relative value rise. A live performance cannot be diluted by automation. An engraved card requires a human at the press. The \“zero productivity growth\“ that Baumol lamented becomes an asset, because it guarantees the product cannot be replicated at zero marginal cost.142

    Baumol tells us that the sectors resistant to automation will see their relative value rise. The income effect tells us that demand for those sectors will grow as the economy gets richer. Together they predict that the share of economic activity devoted to human-presence and human origin products and services will expand, not contract, as AI makes cognitive work cheap.

    Three tiers, three economies

    The result is that there are two polar opposite economies running in parallel, with a wide middle.

    In the algorithmic economy, the old tier-one jobs, AI and automated logistics produce good quality output at near-zero marginal cost. This economy will drive down the price of cognitive commodities such as reports, code, translations, designs, marketing copy, standard legal documents. The value in this economy is captured by the asset owners, the people who own and control the distribution or the platform, not by whoever produces the content.

    On the messy side of the economy, tier-three jobs permanently about the autonomy threshold, value comes from friction and imperfection. These are intrinsically human activities that preserve value at any level of automation. This includes those involving authority and implementation (Chapters 6 and 7) and authenticity and provenance discussed in this chapter.

    In between, we have a wide set of middle of tier-two jobs: the bundled professional work of Chapters 3 through 5. The doctor who uses AI diagnostics but still holds the patient\'s hand. The entrepreneur who builds a company with AI handling her weak links. The restaurant manager who uses AI scheduling but manages the kitchen crew. These workers are neither replaced by the algorithm nor protected by provenance. They survive because their jobs bundle cognitive tasks with social, physical, and relational ones that AI cannot perform, and the bundle holds together. Most careers will sit here: not in the algorithmic economy, not in the provenance economy, but in the messy middle, working with AI.

    The three tiers describe what happens to jobs. But jobs exist inside organizations, and organizations were designed for a world where every bundle was purely human. Hiring systems assumed human-produced signals. Monitoring systems assumed output was produced at the speed of a human. Training systems assumed human apprentices doing human routine work. AI changes every one of these assumptions simultaneously. Part III examines what breaks and what must be rebuilt. The complementarity logic from Chapter 7 applies: organizational systems are clusters of interlocking features, and when one feature changes, in this case a part of the production technology, the others must change together. The result is that organizations must redesign at least four core systems: screening, verification, performance standards, and careers and training. Each redesign also has direct consequences for the workers inside the system.

    Part III
    Organising the Human–AI Bundle

    Parts I and II asked which jobs survive AI. But jobs do not exist in a vacuum. They exist inside organizations, and those organizations were designed for a world where every bundle was purely human. Their hiring systems assumed human-produced signals. Their monitoring systems assumed human production speeds. Their training systems assumed apprentices doing routine work that AI now handles. Layering AI on top of these systems will not work. The systems themselves must change. That is the focus of Part III: how organizations must redesign screening, verification, standards, and training when every surviving job becomes a human-AI bundle, and what that redesign means for the workers inside them.

    Key Takeaway

    When AI can do it perfectly, human imperfection becomes the product.

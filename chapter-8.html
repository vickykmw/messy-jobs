    Part II — Sources of Human Value·Chapter 8

    The Humanness Premium

    The Demand-Side Threshold

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Human ValueTrustUnderstanding AI
    Share

    In 1997, IBM’s Deep Blue defeated Garry Kasparov, the reigning world chess champion.1 By 2017, Google DeepMind’s AlphaZero had taught itself the game from scratch in four hours and then beat the strongest chess engine in existence without losing a single game.2

    If the value of chess lay solely in the production of high-quality moves, the market for human play should have collapsed. It did not. Chess has never been so popular. Since AlphaZero killed human chess, viewership, sponsorship, and participation have reached historic highs. Engine-versus-engine tournaments remain a niche curiosity.

    The game’s value has persisted because of something an AI program cannot supply: the spectacle of humans under pressure. In the February 2026 freestyle chess tournament, Magnus Carlsen was in a losing position against Fabio Caruana after the first two games. Social media was abuzz with the question of whether Magnus could recover from his errors. To the satisfaction of his fans, he did, and won his twenty-first world title in an impressive show of resilience.3

    A blunder by an AI engine is just a software bug. A blunder by a grandmaster in a world championship is a tragedy that millions will replay and discuss. The product is not chess. The product is the humanness of chess. The moves on the board might be identical whether produced by a human or a machine. But the economic value depends entirely on their origin. The Magnus Carlsen/Hans Niemann controversy of 2022–2023 turned on whether a sequence of moves was generated by a human mind or by a chess engine. If the engine made the moves, they were fraudulent. If a human made the moves, he was a genius. The entire value was determined by who made the moves.

    This chapter examines the market-side version of tier three: outputs whose value depends not only on what is produced, but on the human relationship behind it. The value of human origin operates through two distinct channels: instrumental and intrinsic. In the instrumental channel, products of human origin carry information that would be lost if a machine produced the same output. In the intrinsic channel, people value humanness directly: They want to share a room with a performer, to own what others cannot and to be served by a person. We study each and then ask what conditions must hold for humanness to command a premium. We also look at how AI can change the size of these markets.

    Instrumental value

    When a buyer cannot verify quality directly, the seller needs a way to communicate it. A signal does this job: an action or attribute that reveals something hidden. Michael Spence showed that a signal only separates high from low quality when it is costly to produce, and costlier for low-quality sellers than for high-quality ones. A weak peacock cannot afford the tail. A lazy MBA cannot afford two years of tuition and forgone wages. The signal works because it is wasteful; the waste is what the buyer is paying to learn.

    A recent Wall Street Journal story described the work at Crane Stationery in Latham, New York, where twelve master engravers etch custom designs onto copper plates using presses built before the First World War. The margin of error is less than the width of a human hair. Engravers stand for eight hours a day chasing a standard of perfection that their customers expect. Half of all trainees drop out. The company cut off holiday sales two weeks early this past season because it could not fill orders.4

    AI could design the layout. A laser already etches the copper die-plate. But the counter, the thick card stock that pushes paper into the plate to make the impression, must be crafted by hand. Each color has its own plate, and if the alignment of a miniature ballerina is off by a hair’s width, the engraver starts the entire process over. A hand-engraved card from Crane costs fifteen dollars because a human stood at an antique press for the time it took to get the alignment right. The company’s investment in the product is a signal of value for the consumer. It proves that the product’s maker valued the recipient enough to invest in making something difficult. By contrast, an AI-generated card, however beautiful, cannot carry that signal because the cost of producing it is near zero. Reliable signals require real cost, as we shall discuss in part III.

    In a market flooded with synthetic content, products and services with verifiable human origin may increase trust that the product is high quality. In wholesale trade, for instance, 1.6 million sales representatives maintain relationships with buyers. Those buyers could place orders through automated procurement systems, but they choose not to.5 The informational task can be automated, but not the trust built through personal dealings.

    Intrinsic value

    The second source of value does not depend on information, but on human preference. The literary scholar René Girard spent decades arguing that human desire is not autonomous but fundamentally mimetic. In other words, we do not want things because of their intrinsic qualities but because other people want them. The child does not want the toy until her sibling picks it up. The handbag is not beautiful until the other woman carries it. Desire, in this view, is not a relationship between a person and an object. It is a relationship between people, conducted through objects.

    Research by Alex Imas and Kristóf Madarász shows that people value a good more when they know others are excluded from having it. In standard auction theory, the exclusion of potential buyers should reduce revenue by reducing competition.6 However, Imas and Madarász demonstrate the opposite: When a seller randomly excludes some potential buyers from the opportunity to purchase an item, the bids among the remaining buyers rise substantially and total revenue increases. The winner pays a premium for the status of possessing what others cannot.

    When AI makes a product abundant, it destroys the scarcity that generates mimetic desire. A hand-engraved card is desirable not because it has better quality than an AI-generated one. Its value is affected by limited supply. Human origin is a reliable source of limited supply. Machine origin is not. Recent experiments by Imas and Mandel show this.7 When subjects bid on physical art prints, human-made work gained 44 percent in value from being exclusive, that is, one copy rather than many. AI-generated work gained less than half that. The mere involvement of AI made the work feel inherently reproducible, regardless of how many copies were said to exist. Scarcity engineered around a machine-made object does not generate the same willingness to pay as scarcity that flows from human origin.

    This is the main reason for the value of many luxury brands. A Hermès Birkin bag, the ultimate status symbol in our economy, is handcrafted by a single artisan in France from start to finish. Getting one requires building a history of purchases, by buying other items, such as shoes or scarves, at the same boutique with a particular sales associate, and then waiting for months or years for an offer.8

    Direct demand for humanness

    In many instances, humans directly demand the “human touch.” Field experiments find that simply disclosing that the “helper” is a chatbot can reduce purchase rates by 80 percent even when the underlying service is unchanged.9 Other experiments show that people rate the same quality more negatively when they believe it came from a bot rather than a human.10 Research suggests that identical products can command a price premium of up to sixty percent when marketed as human-made.11

    Consider the sommelier. A wine-recommendation app can scan a label, cross-reference it with a database of tens of thousands of reviews, and suggest a pairing in seconds. Vivino, the most popular of these apps, has over fifty million users.12 The informational task of the sommelier, consisting of matching a wine to a dish, a price point, and a preference, has been automatable for some time. And yet fine restaurants continue to employ sommeliers. The more expensive the restaurant, the more likely it is to have one. The sommelier is not selling information. She is selling a performance: the story about the vineyard, the gesture of pouring the first taste.

    These patterns are not confined to retail. Live music is growing at the same time as Spotify and other online music services make recordings essentially free. Recorded music is better produced, better mixed, and available at near-zero marginal cost. Nevertheless, global live music revenue reached roughly $35 billion in 2024 and is projected to nearly double to over $60 billion by 2034.13 Live Nation, the industry’s dominant promoter, reported that 151 million fans attended more than fifty-four thousand events in 2024, and its revenue hit $23 billion.14 It is not just the famous artists who perform live. Many small venues advertise the presence of a live band. The product is not the music. The product is the human being performing under conditions of uncertainty, in a room with other human beings.

    The same pattern runs through the food economy. As industrial food became cheaper and more uniform, a segment of consumers demands products whose value derives from their origin. Craft breweries in the United States numbered about fifteen hundred at the turn of the century and fell to a trough of 1,394 by 2005.15 But by 2023, there were nearly ten thousand, and their retail dollar sales reached $28.8 billion, roughly a quarter of the entire $117 billion US beer market by value. This happened even as total beer consumption stagnated, with overall US beer production and imports falling by 1 percent in 2024.16 Americans were not drinking more beer, they were paying more for beer that came from a specific place, made by specific people, using a specific process. The cheap and efficient mass-produced product lost market share to the expensive, local one.

    Something similar is happening in the Indian handloom sector, where technologies like IoT sensors are being deployed, not to replace weavers but to authenticate their work. The technology records the strokes of a loom and proves that a specific artisan produced a specific fabric.17

    Jobs and earnings: Baumol’s cost disease becomes a blessing

    Throughout this book, the autonomy threshold has been a supply-side concept. The threshold is the point where the technology can deliver a finished output without human involvement. This chapter adds a demand side. A Crane engraver does clean, verifiable, single-task work, which is among the most vulnerable jobs by supply-side logic. But customers care who made it. The sommelier’s informational task is automatable, and AI has crossed the supply-side threshold. But demand for human interaction keeps her in the job. A job survives as long as one side of the autonomy threshold holds. Many jobs may be shielded by both. A personal trainer must read her client’s mood, adjust the program to her tiredness, build a relationship, and keep her motivated over months. This is messy work and may be resistant to the supply-side pressure. The client wants to be trained by a person, not an app. Combine both sources of protection and you may get employment security.

    What determines the size of the authenticity economy? As before, we need to think of demand and supply.

    Demand for human origin

    Three conditions determine whether consumers will pay for human origin. The first is verifiability. The customer must be able to confirm, or at least plausibly believe, that a human produced the work. As AI-generated content becomes indistinguishable from human output in many domains, verification becomes harder and therefore more valuable when it can be achieved. Physical products have an advantage. You can touch the engraving, inspect the weave, taste the hand-rolled pasta. Music, books, and other information goods may require external certification such as badges, portfolios, or reputation systems to establish provenance. The harder it is to verify, the more the premium depends on trust and relationship.

    The second condition is perceptible quality. Provenance commands a premium only if the customer believes the human-made product is different in a way that matters to them. This need not mean objectively superior. The mechanical watch is objectively worse at keeping time than the quartz watch. The wabi-sabi ceramic in Japan has deliberate imperfections that make it feel human.18 In both cases, the customer perceives and values the craftsmanship that the machine-made alternatives lack. When the customer cannot perceive a difference, provenance does not generate a premium.

    The third condition, for intrinsic or instrumental reasons, is status or expressive value. Human origin commands a premium when it allows consumers to express taste, care, status, identity, or belonging. This is the case in markets such as luxury goods, high-end stationery, handmade furniture, artisanal food, and live performances. In these markets, the fact that a person made or delivered the good is part of what the buyer is purchasing. As people get richer, they do not just spend more money; they spend a rising share of their income on luxury goods. They do not just eat out more often; they eat at restaurants with more staff per table, where meals take longer and where service is more attentive. When they exercise, they hire personal trainers. Being served by a person is part of what they are buying, which is a direct expression of this income effect. As AI-driven productivity growth makes the economy richer, the resulting income gains are likely to push consumers to spend more on human-intensive services, not less. 19

    When any of these conditions fail, demand may suddenly collapse. The NFT art market is an example of this. In March 2021, Christie’s sold a digital collage by the artist Beeple for $69.3 million, making him the third-most-expensive living artist at the time.20 The work existed as a JPEG. What the buyer received was a nonfungible token: a unique entry on a blockchain certifying that he, and only he, owned this particular file. The promise was extraordinary. The blockchain, its advocates claimed, had solved the oldest problem in the art market: provenance. An NFT could prove ownership, certify authenticity, and manufacture scarcity for an object that was otherwise infinitely reproducible. Walter Benjamin, the cultural theorist who argued in 1935 that mechanical reproduction would destroy the “aura” of artwork (its unique presence in time and space), had apparently been overturned by cryptography.21 The blockchain could create “aura” from code. For about eighteen months, the market believed it. NFT art trading volume hit $2.9 billion in 2021.22 Then it fell apart. By 2024, trading volume had dropped 93 percent from its peak. The share of collectors buying digital art fell from 17 percent in 2022 to 9 percent in 2024. The number of active traders collapsed from over 529,000 in 2022 to under twenty thousand by early 2025.23 Christie’s quietly closed its digital art department.

    Supply of human origin

    On the supply side, the binding constraint is that authentic human work cannot be scaled. A live concert cannot be replicated by adding servers. An engraved card requires a human at the press. A Hermès Birkin bag is handcrafted by a single artisan from start to finish. The skill must be genuinely difficult to acquire. Crane’s engravers train for at least a year on a single color before advancing to complex designs. If the skill were easy, everyone would do it and the premium would collapse.

    William Baumol noted in the 1960s that a string quartet still takes four musicians and the same amount of time to play as in Mozart’s time. Quartets have enjoyed no productivity gains. Yet musicians today earn much more than musicians in Mozart’s time, because, given the growth in compensation in other sectors, a stagnant salary would deter people from becoming musicians.24 If the job of hairdresser, whose productivity has not increased in a hundred years, were paid as much as a century ago, no one would do the job.

    Baumol’s general observation is that wages in the sectors that experience no automation, such as the performing arts, education, or healthcare, must rise as much as the wages in sectors where productivity is exploding in order to keep attracting workers. Hence, sectors with little or no productivity growth see their costs and wages rise relative to the rest of the economy.

    In traditional economics, this was treated as a problem: an unfortunate fact of reduced aggregate productivity growth. But in a world where cognition is abundant, the logic has another, more positive, interpretation.

    When AI automates commodity production, prices in that sector fall. Falling prices raise real income. Rising real income shifts spending toward sectors with higher income elasticity, that is, toward goods and services whose demand grows faster than income.25 Research by Comin, Lashkari, and Mestieri shows that income effects account for over seventy-five percent of observed sectoral reallocation across the twentieth century. Price effects, the standard story that cheap automated output frees up budget for other things, explain only about a quarter of it. Alex Imas draws on this result to argue that the dominant force in AI-driven structural change will not be automation pushing workers out of cheap sectors, but rising incomes pulling spending toward sectors where human involvement is part of the value.

    Baumol’s cost disease then reinforces the shift. The sectors Baumol treated as “diseased,” the ones where productivity cannot explode because the work is irreducibly tied to human time and presence, see their relative price rise exactly when demand is already moving toward them. A live performance cannot be diluted by automation. An engraved card requires a human at the press. The zero-productivity growth that Baumol lamented becomes an asset because it guarantees the product cannot be replicated at zero marginal cost. Since higher-income households spend a larger share of their consumption on labor-intensive goods and services, growth itself tilts demand toward sectors with higher labor content.

    Together, price falls, the income effect, and the Baumol amplifier predict that the share of economic activity devoted to human-presence and human origin, both products and services, will expand, not contract, as AI makes cognitive work cheap.26

    Three tiers, three economies

    The result is that there are two polar-opposite economies running in parallel, with a wide middle.

    In the algorithmic economy (the old tier one jobs), AI and automated logistics produce good quality output at near-zero marginal cost. This economy will drive down the price of cognitive commodities such as reports, code, translations, designs, marketing copy, and standard legal documents. The value in this economy is captured by the asset owners, the people who own and control the distribution or the platform, not by the people who produce the content.

    On the messy side of the economy (tier three jobs that are permanently above the autonomy threshold), value comes from friction and imperfection. These jobs are intrinsically human activities that preserve value at any level of automation. This includes those involving authority and implementation (chapters 6 and 7), and authenticity and provenance.

    In between, we have a wide set of tier two jobs, the bundled professional work described in chapters 3 through 5. For example: The doctor who uses AI diagnostics but still holds the patient’s hand; the entrepreneur who builds a company with AI handling her weak links; the restaurant manager who uses AI scheduling but manages the kitchen crew. These workers are neither replaced by the algorithm nor protected by provenance. They survive because their jobs bundle cognitive tasks with social, physical, and relational ones that AI cannot perform. The bundle holds together. Most careers will sit here in tier two: not in the algorithmic economy, not in the provenance economy, but in the messy middle, working with AI.

    The three tiers describe what happens to jobs. But jobs exist inside organizations, and organizations were designed for a world where every bundle was purely human. Hiring systems assumed human-produced signals. Monitoring systems assumed output was produced at the speed of a human. Training systems assumed human apprentices doing human routine work. AI changes every one of these assumptions simultaneously.

    Part III examines what breaks and what must be rebuilt. The complementarity logic from chapter 7 applies to this question. Organizational systems are clusters of interlocking features, so when one feature changes (e.g., production technology), the others must change together. The result is that organizations must redesign at least four core systems: screening, verification, performance standards, and careers and training. Each redesign also has direct consequences for the workers inside the system.

    Key Takeaway

    Some jobs survive purely because consumers demand human origin, authenticity, and trust. Whether for luxury goods, live performances, or relational services, humans command a premium because human effort serves as a costly, unforgeable signal of care, status, and unreplicable scarcity that AI cannot duplicate.

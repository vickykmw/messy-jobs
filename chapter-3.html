    What Holds Jobs Together

    Strong and Weak Bundles

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    AutomationJob RedesignAutonomy Threshold
    Share

    In 2016, Geoffrey Hinton, one of the founders of deep learning research, famously declared that radiologists should stop training immediately because AI would soon outperform them at reading scans.1 His logic seemed solid because radiology involves recognizing patterns in digital images. AI is essentially a pattern recognition machine. Huge amounts of data exist to train the models. Hence, Hinton believed that AI would rapidly surpass humans in reading scans.

    The prediction was, at the very least, premature. Today, many AI tools can analyze scans with precision. By November 2025, the FDA had approved more than 725 radiology AI models, representing about 76 percent of all approved medical AI devices.2 Yet US radiology residency openings hit a record high in the same year, and average radiologist compensation rose to approximately $520,000, becoming the third-highest-paid specialty.3

    Hinton made the mistake of confusing the task and the job. True, a radiologist reads scans. But she also trains other radiologists, triages cases, reviews the difficult scans, decides on treatments, and coordinates with other physicians on treatment plans. She also signs the diagnosis, making it legal.

    This chapter explains why Hinton’s mistake matters. AI can automate a task inside a job without automating the entire job. And most jobs are not limited to single tasks. They are bundles of tasks that must be performed together.

    Not all bundles are the same. Some are weak. In those cases, the AI system can perform one task inside the bundle. That means the job can be easily split apart and reorganized. Other bundles are strong. If tasks in a strong bundle are separated, value is destroyed. That outcome occurs because the pieces rely heavily on shared context, responsibility, or coordination.

    Understanding whether a job is a strong bundle or a weak bundle is the key to predicting whether AI will merely reshape a job or eliminate it altogether.4

    The patient does not buy a diagnosis. She buys a diagnosis she trusts enough to act on and that has legal validity for treatment and insurance. AI alone can produce the diagnosis, but it cannot produce the bundle of services provided by the doctor. Even though AI can automate many tasks, that does not amount to automating the job. In many cases, the use of AI merely reshapes the job by stripping out some tasks and elevating others. To understand why, we must look at why tasks get bundled into jobs in the first place.

    Bundled tasks and the risk of weak links

    Consider a simple project: updating the pricing page on a company’s website.

    Three experts are involved. Mark writes very well, Sarah is a great programmer, and Dave is an excellent lawyer.

    Mark writes the new text for the website in a Word document. He sends it to Sarah, but because she is busy she opens the email two days later. She realizes the text is too long for the design. She sends a Slack message to Mark. A few hours later, he edits it down and sends it back to Sarah, who writes the code. Then they send it to Dave for approval. Dave realizes the way the new price list is explained violates a consumer protection law and starts the process again. Two weeks and a few dozen emails after Mark wrote the initial text, the project is done. The total workload of the project was three hours, but coordinating three workers consumed two weeks.

    Now consider a different organization. The firm uses a generalist, Jenny. She is a decent writer and programmer, and she knows just enough about the law to avoid going to jail. She opens the web editor. She changes the text. She revises it and presses publish. Total work time: three hours. The output is not outstanding, but it is fine. Jenny does not win because she is better at each task. She is faster because she provides a complete bundle, eliminating the coordination costs entirely. She cannot misinterpret her own instructions, nor wait to answer her own questions.

    Economists Gary Becker and Kevin Murphy argued that we do not specialize more because it costs too much to coordinate the work of many specialists. In markets and firms, people specialize until the cost of coordinating specialists becomes excessive.

    Most pediatricians in a city, or even in a single health maintenance organization, do not specialize in particular childhood diseases. No doubt they would learn more about a disease through specialization, but the additional knowledge would require greater expenses in coordinating their care with that of other pediatricians. For parents often do not know what is wrong with their children, and would need to see several pediatricians to get adequate care if each were highly specialized.5

    Every time you split work across more people, you pay the coordination cost in terms of errors, meetings, and management. For this reason, companies bundle tasks within jobs to avoid paying this cost. However, this approach comes with risks and disadvantages. When different tasks are forced together into a single job for one person, the worker’s overall performance is limited by the task on which the worker is least competent.

    The best way to understand this is the O-ring theory, named after the space shuttle Challenger disaster in 1986. The Challenger was an engineering marvel that cost billions of dollars and had millions of parts. During a launch on an unusually cold morning in Florida, a simple rubber seal, an O-ring, froze, became brittle, and failed. The malfunction of this cheap part destroyed the shuttle and killed the astronauts.6

    Economist Michael Kremer used this tragedy to explain an important economic phenomenon: in complex processes, the quality of the results is determined by the weakest link. This is crucial for economic development and growth. For instance, a country like South Africa with high human capital and lots of natural resources will not grow if the weak link of extremely unreliable energy keeps deterring companies from investing there.7

    The same is true for individual jobs comprising a bundle of tasks. Imagine a highly specialized pediatrician who is a brilliant diagnostician, but who also needs to write notes in the electronic medical records. It turns out that she is a slow typist. Because the tasks are bundled, the hospital pays the doctor several hundred dollars an hour to enter data at the speed of a novice typist. Her slow typing speed is a bottleneck in the bundle. One weak skill degrades the value of everything else and increases costs. If the written record has a typo that leads to the wrong medicine being prescribed, the outcome is even worse.

    How AI changes bundles

    To coordinate with another human, you need empathy, clear communication, a shared context, and similar schedules. You cannot delegate a task to someone who is sleeping or busy. If it takes a long time to explain a task, it is often better to do it yourself.

    AI has none of these restrictions. It is a subordinate that is available twenty-four hours a day. It has no ego and no conflicting priorities. You do not need to be polite. There are no feelings to manage. There is no waiting for a coworker to be available.

    Let’s use the pediatrician who wrote her own notes as an example. She might benefit by hiring a human scribe, but she might exclude that option because a new employee would introduce too much friction in the form of a large coordination tax: hiring the scribe, training him, communicating with him, scheduling the work, and meetings.

    Today, the scribe is an AI that listens to the meeting, extracts the relevant data, and writes the note instantly. The doctor checks it on her screen. The coordination cost is much lower. The “documentation” task has been unbundled from the “medicine” task. AI acts as a perfect scribe. This allows the bundle to break. The doctor continues to provide the diagnosis, which is her main, core task. She unloads the documentation work, which is her weak-link task, to the machine.

    A recent experiment at Procter & Gamble, led by Raffaella Sadun and her coauthors at Harvard Business School, makes the point directly. They compared three configurations: a single specialist working alone, two human specialists collaborating, and a single specialist working with AI. The single specialist with AI improved her output by 0.37 standard deviations. Adding a second human specialist improved output by only 0.24 standard deviations. The two-person team had more combined expertise, but the coordination “tax” of communicating different contexts and perspectives ate into the knowledge advantage. The human-AI pair dominated both alternatives, not because the AI knew more than the second specialist, but because the coordination cost was lower.8

    Coordinating with AI is less expensive than with a human, but it is not free. The relevant question is whether the gains from separating a task and handing it to AI exceed the losses from breaking the bundle. That is why we need to distinguish between weak bundles, which can be split apart at low cost, and strong bundles, where separation destroys value. Three conditions make unbundling costly. When any of these conditions hold, the bundle survives. Otherwise, the bundle is weak and comes apart under AI.

    Unpredictable demand. The first condition comes from the demand side. Within a job, some tasks are better suited to AI and others to humans. In principle, you can assign the tasks accordingly. But that is often difficult in practice because the demands of customers are complex, and you do not know which tasks the customer will need.

    One of us (Yanhui) spent time in Hangzhou with a leading customer service company that had pioneered the use of a domain-specific LLM in high-end service. After two years of deployment, human agents still played an indispensable role.

    At this company, human agents handle a broad mix of work: answering inquiries, resolving complaints, promoting products, collecting feedback, and managing customer relationships. Their performance is evaluated along six dimensions: accuracy, speed, completeness, flexibility, sales awareness, and customer relationship.

    On the first four, AI already performs extremely well. The last two remain much harder to automate. Sales awareness involves recognizing what the customer is not explicitly saying. If a human agent senses that a customer is on the verge of leaving, she must quickly steer him toward an alternative product. Customer relationship management requires a different kind of judgment: knowing how to respond without creating frustration. Word of an interaction that is offensive to the customer can spread quickly on social media and become very costly for the firm.

    The division of labor, in principle, is clear. AI is better at routine parts of the interaction and humans are better at the delicate ones. The difficulty is that customers do not arrive in clearly sorted categories. At the start of an interaction, the firm often does not know whether it will remain a routine inquiry or if it will become a retention issue. As one senior manager put it, “Such unbundling will require frequent switches between the AI and human modes. The coordination cost would be too high.”

    Now contrast this with a case in which demand is predictable. Strategy scholar Nan Jia and coauthors studied a large telemarketing company selling credit cards by making outbound calls. The sales process had two clear stages. First was lead generation: calling customers to gauge interest, which is a scripted and repetitive task. If the customer was interested, the second stage was about converting the interest into a sale, which involved a far less structured conversation.

    The company assigned AI to the first stage. Then a human agent entered the conversation.9 This worked because the sequence of demand was known in advance. Stage one was always about identifying interest. Stage two was always about closing the sale. There was no ambiguity about which capability was needed or when. The handoff occurred only once, at a clearly defined point.

    That is a weak bundle. When demand arrives in a predictable sequence, the work can be partitioned cleanly. But when customers arrive with mixed and shifting needs, and the nature of the interaction is only revealed along the way, unbundling creates either misrouting or constant switching. Both are costly.

    Production spillovers. The second condition comes from the supply side. Sometimes tasks belong together because doing one makes you better at the other one. When the spillover is large, separating the tasks reduces the quality of the whole job.

    Radiology is a good example. Reading a scan is not just pattern recognition. A radiologist who has spoken with the patient and reviewed the clinical record can do a better job reading the mammogram than one who sees only the image. She knows whether an anomaly is the result of a previous condition. If interacting with the patient is taken out of the bundle, the diagnosis itself may get worse.

    The same is true in law. By the time a litigator cross-examines a witness, she has usually spent weeks reading discovery, taking depositions, and drafting briefs. Those tasks help build the case in her mind. They teach her where the weak points are, which details matter, and which story the other side is trying to tell. Even if AI writes a decent brief, the lawyer may walk into court without knowing the case deeply, and the cross-examination will suffer.

    This is the logic of production spillovers. One task creates knowledge or instinct that improves another. When that is true, the tasks form a strong bundle.

    Measurement challenges. The third condition is about measurement. When AI produces one piece of the work and a human produces the rest, mistakes in the final product are hard to assign. If the software crashes, was the problem in the AI-generated code or in the way the engineer stitched it together?

    Economists Alchian and Demsetz call this issue the “metering problem.” When several inputs jointly produce an output, their distinct contributions are difficult to disentangle. The standard solution is to bundle the tasks into one person’s job. Rather than trying to assign blame task by task, you make that person responsible for the whole thing.10

    This is why a senior engineer can rely heavily on AI and still be fully accountable for the project. If the system fails, she answers for it, not the model. If the work is split more finely and the bundle is broken so that AI writes one module, a second engineer integrates, and a third one tests, then assigning mistakes becomes very difficult.

    The managers in the Hangzhou customer service company made the same point from a different angle. They kept coming back to verifiability, liability, and responsibility. In other words, if a bot offends a VIP client, or if an AI-drafted response triggers a regulatory inquiry, then the algorithm cannot be fired or sued. Somebody must own the outcome. And to own the outcome requires that somebody sees the whole bundle.

    In summary, when the qualities of the parts cannot be judged on their own, the measurement challenge creates a strong bundle. Bundling is the way to ensure that somebody is responsible.

    These conditions offer a test to assess whether a job can survive AI. First, how predictable is the demand? Can you forecast which tasks the demand will require? Second, how large are the production spillovers? And third, how divisible is the measurement? Can you verify the quality of each task independently, or can you only judge the final product as a whole? If the demand is unpredictable, or if the spillover is large, or if measuring the task separately is difficult, then the job will survive. Otherwise, AI will unbundle tasks out of the job.

    What do surviving bundles look like?

    Not all residual human work is equal. A consistent pattern runs through the data, and it predates AI by decades: the labor market increasingly rewards people who combine analytical thinking with the ability to work with other people.

    The economist David Deming documented this pattern across American occupations from 1980 to 2012.11 He classified jobs according to their cognitive skill and social skill demands. He found that jobs high on both dimensions grew by roughly twelve percentage points over three decades. Jobs that were cognitively demanding but low on social interaction (number crunching) shrank by about three percentage points. The demand increase was not for “soft skills” but for a combination of skills, for people who could both think and coordinate.

    Catherine Weinberger found the same pattern from the wage side.12 Comparing cohorts of American workers, she showed that the relative wages of those with high cognitive ability rose mainly for those who also had strong social skills. Again, the extra payoff from having both grew stronger with each cohort. Social skills were not a substitute for analytical ability, but they were a multiplier of it.

    Our three conditions for strong bundles (demand, production spillovers, and measurement) help shed light on these findings. Jobs that combine difficult judgment with difficult relational coordination tend to score high on all three conditions. The demand, as explained earlier, is unpredictable. This means that workers often face complex problems that can only be understood during interactions with customers and coworkers. The production spillovers are strong. As a result, understanding the problem helps the worker explain it, and explaining it sharpens her understanding. And the measurement is indivisible; that is, the value of the work cannot be decomposed into “the analysis part” and “the relationship part” because they are jointly produced.

    This is why our “strong bundles” argument is more precise than “soft skills matter.” The greeter at the store and the receptionist at the desk perform social interaction, but if their cognitive demands are low, AI and self-service kiosks can replace them. What the data show is that the interaction between social and cognitive capability produces the strongest employment growth and wage growth.13

    In our view, jobs that combine difficult judgment with difficult coordination will most likely be strong bundles. They are not protected because they are vague or because they involve “people skills” in some generic sense. They are protected because thinking and relating are tightly linked in the work itself.

    Jobs and earnings: the effects of unbundling

    The labor market implications for a multitask job are different from those derived from a single-task job. When a job consists of one task and AI crosses the autonomy threshold for that task, the replacement is clean, as the previous chapter showed. But jobs are usually bundles of tasks. The threshold for replacing the whole job is much higher than the threshold for automating any one task inside it. Even if AI can perform one component well, someone may still need to hold the bundle together, take responsibility for the outcome, and bear the coordination costs that will arise if the work is split across multiple agents. This is where Hinton and other tech people went wrong about radiologists. They confused a task with a job.

    Thinking in terms of bundles also helps explain a puzzle in the data. Occupations that are viewed as highly exposed to generative AI did not necessarily experience a significant decline in job postings. One of us (Yanhui) has studied twenty million job postings from China’s five major recruitment platforms between August 2024 and June 2025 and analyzed the impact of the sudden arrival of DeepSeek V3 in December 2024. Consistent with what we observe in the customer-service company in Hangzhou, the number of job postings for customer service agents in China barely changed after the entry of DeepSeek, although more jobs required the skill of using generative AI.

    The bundle, not any single task, is the unit that the labor market prices. The distinction between strong and weak bundles determines what happens to earnings.

    In a strong bundle, the direct technological effect of AI is positive. AI raises productivity on the tasks it can handle while the human retains the full job. The equilibrium effect tends to reinforce the direct effect. The market still buys the same bundle of judgment, coordination, and responsibility; however, with AI the worker can deliver it all more efficiently. Because the current supply of qualified workers in certain fields remains constrained (radiologists still require more than a decade of training and skilled customer service agents still require deep domain knowledge), earnings often remain unchanged or even increase.

    AI’s effects on weak bundles are very different. The technology strips away the tasks that used to be bundled with the core. The direct technological effect is ambiguous. The worker loses some tasks to AI, which reduces the scope of the role. The remaining core tasks are still viable for humans, and may even become more productive with AI assistance, but the job becomes narrower and simpler.

    The thinning of the job has a negative equilibrium effect. When the peripheral tasks are eliminated, the job becomes less complex, and hence there is a larger pool of people capable of performing it. Workers whose employment was protected by the need to be able to handle the full bundle will now face increased competition. The resulting increase in labor supply pushes earnings down, even if demand for the core tasks remains stable. The lower barrier to entry leads to a wage decline.

    This is why the distinction between strong and weak bundles is so consequential. In a strong bundle, AI raises productivity while the coordination cost keeps the job intact and the supply of qualified workers constrained. In these cases, earnings hold or rise. In a weak bundle, AI raises productivity on the surviving tasks but lowers the complexity of the overall role. When that occurs, the supply of eligible workers expands and the earnings of workers go down.

    There is one important caveat. The equilibrium effect of unbundling can be positive if the use of AI creates sufficient new demand. Consider software. Many promising projects never started because they could not justify the engineering cost of developing new software. These projects become viable when AI coding tools substantially reduce the cost of software development. This is the Jevons effect at work: cheaper code creates enormous new demand for software that was previously too expensive to build. Perhaps that is why the US Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) optimistically projected employment for software developers to grow 15 percent from 2024 to 2034, five times the average for all occupations, with about 129,000 openings per year.14

    For the reader thinking about career choices, the implication is quite clear. The question is not just “which of my tasks can AI do?” but “what does my job look like after the bundle absorbs tasks from adjacent roles?” The answer, across a wide range of occupations, is that the post-AI bundle requires more social coordination, more judgment, and more ability to adapt to new roles. This is uncomfortable for specialists who built their identity around a narrow skill. But it is encouraging for people who can operate across boundaries, for those who can analyze a problem and explain it to a skeptical audience, or who can manage a technical system and the organizational politics of deploying it.

    Once AI enters the bundle, the internal structure of the job changes. Sometimes AI removes the routine tasks beneath the human. Sometimes it delivers expert knowledge downward to less-skilled workers.

    The next two chapters examine these two possibilities. In chapter 4, we study what happens when AI sits below the human in the hierarchy and automates routine work. In chapter 5, we study the opposite case, when AI sits above the human and delivers expert knowledge downward.

    Key Takeaway

    Most jobs are task bundles. AI easily disrupts “weak bundles,” stripping away automatable tasks and driving down wages through increased competition. Conversely, “strong bundles” survive and thrive because their tasks are inseparable due to unpredictable demand, production spillovers, or the need for holistic accountability.

    The Mediocrity Trap

    Allocating Effort Where It Pays

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Quality ControlOrganisationsEarly Career
    Share

    In 2022, at the Colorado State Fair, Jason Allen won a prize with his AI-generated artwork. When he was awarded the prize, he made the claim: “AI won, and humans lost.”1

    When the news was released, it created outrage. One Twitter user commented, “ten years ago when I was telling people I switched to doing digital art this is what they assumed I did, just pressed a button and generated art. . . . Now I guess they are giving awards for doing just that.” The truth is far more complicated than that. Jason Allen wasn’t just pressing one button.

    The project took one hundred hours, most of which he spent struggling with the model output. He spent eighty hours on prompting, sifting through hundreds of “not-at-all-satisfactory” samples, and finally achieved that unique, surreal lighting effect. Once the figure was dressed in flowing robes, he imported the work into Photoshop for a final twenty hours of manual refinement. It was a meticulous process of adjusting shadows and fixing details.

    Allen’s story forces us to confront a new organizational reality. We already know that AI makes it easier to get started, but its impact on finishing work is less clear. When a machine can give you a piece that is 90-percent-perfect in ten seconds, where does the motivation to spend ten hours honing the remaining 10 percent come from? The real question may not be how to complete that last 10 percent, but whether that 10 percent is worth the effort.

    The previous two chapters have shown that organizations are getting worse at reading the quality of work and verifying results. The signal is compressed. The cost of verification is high. This chapter asks the following questions: How must organizations set standards and evaluate output when AI changes the shape of the production function? And, given those changing standards, where should individuals really spend their time?

    The price of perfection

    Long before machines could paint, humans were dealing with the problem of perfectionism. Great work has never depended on talent alone; it depends on how much discomfort and delay someone is willing to endure for a slightly better result. If masterpieces have a secret fuel, it is obsession.

    Stanley Kubrick was the high priest of perfectionism. During the filming of Eyes Wide Shut, he demanded take after take to perfect even the simplest gestures. Tom Cruise described walking through a doorway dozens of times until Kubrick felt the moment was right. The production stretched to forty-six consecutive weeks, setting a Guinness World Record.2

    There is a flattering modern name for this: grit. Psychologist Angela Duckworth defines grit as the passion and perseverance to achieve long-term goals. A person with grit refuses to accept the tenth draft because the eleventh might be better. Duckworth argues this quality predicts success better than natural IQ. Grit can lead to great work, but it casts a long shadow.3

    Even the most determined drive eventually has to end. At a certain point, a difficult question appears: Is this small increment of “better” really worth what it costs?

    At Pixar Animation Studios, artists call this trap “the beautifully shaded penny.” It describes a situation where an artist pours days of labor into a detail the audience will never see, like a penny on a nightstand in the background. In Monsters, Inc., artists carefully designed individual surface effects for ninety CD cases in a character’s apartment. But the CD cases appear for less than three seconds, and only a few of them are visible on screen. The effort was impressive, and it was a good story to tell, but the value to the audience was near zero, and the cost to Pixar was real.4

    The shaded penny problem has many victims. Gustave Flaubert, the French writer, was known to pace his study for hours, reading sentences aloud until the rhythm felt right, sometimes discarding days of work for a single misplaced adjective. In his letters, he complained that countless hours were lost while struggling over “five or six lines.” It took him nearly five years to complete Madame Bovary.5 Leonardo da Vinci produced thousands of sketches but fewer than twenty completed paintings because of his exacting standards. In modern times, George R. R. Martin’s long-suffering readers may never see the next Game of Thrones volume.

    To deal with the shaded penny problem, creators have invented diverse methods to force themselves to stop. At Pixar, producer John Walker introduced a system involving popsicle sticks. He attached a row of sticks to a wall, each representing one person-week of labor. If a director wanted to polish a background detail, Walker pointed to the wall: Which stick should we use? More time spent on the background meant less time on the main character. Production now requires a trade-off of finite resources.6

    Graham Greene found another solution. He divided his works into “novels” and “entertainments.” The novels carried profound artistic aspirations whereas the entertainments were thrillers written quickly and without the same crushing pressure. By labeling some of his works as “light,” Greene freed himself from the demand that every single project had to justify unlimited effort.7

    In all of these examples, the rules are preventing (or stopping) the unlimited expansion of effort. They enable people to complete their work, continue creating, and devote their energy to other things. But they are also imperfect answers. If we want to think about how to effectively solve this problem, thereby allowing effort to freely expand, we need to systematically consider the general principles involved. This again leads us to economics.

    The economics of stopping

    Economics is often described as the study of markets, prices, and money. But it is simpler than that. Economics is the language of human decision-making. It examines how people make choices with limited time, attention, and energy, and, just as importantly, when to stop.

    For many activities, economics shows us that decisions are not simple binary choices of yes or no. The choices are more complex, such as deciding how much to invest. Many choices are incremental. We decide not only whether to write, but also whether to revise. The question is not just about whether more work is valuable; we also ask whether another step is worth the cost.

    The payoffs to almost any activity are not uniform; they follow the law of diminishing returns. Early efforts often yield significant gains whereas later efforts yield progressively smaller improvements. In the end, people can spend a lot of time with little to show for it.

    Consider a writer who is revising a chapter. The first revision restructures the argument and eliminates weak sections, creating a huge improvement. The second revision catches minor errors and tightens the prose. The improvement is smaller, but still worth the time. By the sixth revision, the gains come from small details that readers will hardly notice. This pattern echoes the 80/20 rule: The last 20 percent of the work often consumes 80 percent of the effort.

    Given the diminishing returns, the question is when we should stop. The rational approach is that we should continue only when the benefit of the next step is greater than the cost. We should stop when the additional benefit is equal to the additional cost. Beyond this point, continuing will result in a loss. The value of our time is greater than the value of the results.

    The stopping point is not just a property of the task. It is also a property of what the person could be doing instead. Economists find that older individuals, retirees, and the unemployed systematically spend more time shopping and searching for lower prices. This is not because they benefit more from finding a bargain, though they might. The more likely reason is that they have more time. An hour spent searching for lower prices is not burdensome for a retiree. It is a burden for a busy banker. Both people stop when the benefit of continuing falls below the cost, but that cost is different for each of them.

    Diminishing returns in quality do not always mean diminishing returns in value. An Olympic sprinter who shaves 0.02 seconds off her time has made a negligible improvement in absolute performance. But if that 0.02 seconds is the difference between gold and fourth place, the return in value is huge. Similarly, a marketing pitch that is 5 percent more polished than the next firm’s pitch wins the entire contract. In these cases, the quality curve is flat but the reward curve rises sharply. This means that rational effort allocation is not simply a matter of examining the quality curve. You also need to know the reward structure. And this, as we will see, is a key issue in the age of AI.

    When AI frontloads progress

    How does AI affect the decision of when to continue and when to stop? The answer lies in how AI reshapes the trajectory of progress.

    The new shape of progress

    Starting a project used to be the most challenging part of creative work. Creators put in lots of thought at the beginning to explore and generate ideas. Once an idea is settled, the execution process starts, which can be slow and tedious. There are many details to hammer out, but progress is controllable and steady.

    As demonstrated by the old 80/20 pattern, achieving the first 80 percent in relation to perfection required the most work: figuring out ideas and concepts, and producing the rough first version. The remaining 20 percent involved refinement: polishing prose, fixing edge cases, tightening the design. That final stretch was time-consuming and often frustrating, but the creator knew what needed to be done. The path to the final output was known. To use a hiking metaphor, the difficulty was in enduring the long walk, not in finding the trail.

    With AI, huge progress appears instantly. A single prompt generates a draft, a sketch, or a block of code in seconds, compressing hours of work into minutes. That is why the old 80/20 logic no longer fits. The new pattern is closer to 90/10. However, the deeper change is not the ratio; it’s the nature of the production process. AI hands you something that looks nearly finished before you have worked out what is wrong with it, before you know what “better” even means, and before you know which of the many possible improvements would matter. The creative part of the production now comes late.

    Instead of laboring on a long hike, AI works like a helicopter. AI drops you near the summit without the long climb. You save enormous effort, but you also skip the accumulation of skill, judgment, and feel that the climb once provided, and you do not see the trail that leads to the summit. Nevertheless, you can still ask AI for help for the last part of the journey. This creates two opposing forces.

    The knowledge deficit. In the traditional production process, getting to 80 percent of perfection was evidence of the worker’s knowledge. A writer who had produced a strong draft had already wrestled with the structure of the argument and was knowledgeable about the supporting materials. By the time revision began, she knew all the foundational materials and only needed to fix the surface.

    With AI, a draft appears before the thinking is done. The writer now faces polished prose without an equally developed mental model behind it. Her mind is no longer ahead of what’s written on the page, but behind it. So the hardest part of the job, including diagnosing weaknesses, judging coherence, and deciding what matters, is harder than before. That is why AI so often produces stagnation: The user falls into a cycle of prompt, glance, and re-prompt. New output arrives, but the work does not truly improve.

    Who breaks through the stagnation depends on two factors. One is existing skill and domain knowledge. A skillful editor knows the structural reasons why a draft is wrong. A less experienced one may not. An understanding of what excellence looks like is a key factor for breakthrough. The second factor has to do with the willingness to do hard thinking. The availability of AI makes it all too easy to outsource the thinking prematurely. Real improvement still requires hard thinking.

    The companion effect. AI may create the knowledge deficit that impedes further progress, but it also gives something back. In the past, a writer stuck at 90 percent was often alone with her doubts. Writing alone can be mentally taxing. Now, with AI, the writer can ask the program to critique her argument, suggest alternative framings, identify the weakest points, and generate different ways to restructure the conclusion.

    This means the marginal cost of continuing to work has fallen. When AI functions as a thinking partner, the next hour of effort is less unpleasant and less lonely. Writers may even develop a “flow experience” while working with AI, continuously pushing forward the quality of writing.

    In summary, AI raises the difficulty of the last 10 percent because the creator lacks the accumulated understanding that she would have gained if she had endured the long, hard climb. AI simultaneously lowers the cost of working on the last 10 percent because there is a collaborator. That combination creates a bifurcation: Some people stop working early once the initial gains of AI end, but others keep working because they know they can improve, and because the marginal cost of working more is acceptable. The evidence shows both patterns.

    The mediocrity trap and the last-mile illusion

    To examine the effect of AI on the creative process, Jin and his fellow researchers ran an experiment with 219 artists, ranging from students to professionals. The task was to illustrate passages from famous novels, both with AI and without it. Each artist worked for up to 150 minutes. The researchers looked at their entire creative process.8

    The researchers recorded every brushstroke, undo, and prompt. Independent judges scored the artwork at fifteen-minute intervals. Without AI, the pattern was the familiar one: gradual progress with diminishing returns. The first hour produced a rough composition. The artists then added color and shading, and finally worked on fine details.

    With AI, the shape of progress changed sharply. Within thirty minutes, many artists reached a quality level that would have taken two hours by hand. But then, they hit a wall. After minute forty-five, the quality curve started to plateau. Further gains were barely noticeable, even as artists kept tweaking prompts and patching details. This pattern is consistent with the knowledge deficit discussed earlier. AI helped the artists to get a polished image before they had thought enough about the composition, which made it difficult to make improvements.

    The new shape of progress led to behavioral changes in many artists. Without AI, almost all artists worked to the deadline. With AI, there was a bifurcation. One group stopped early, right around minute sixty, where the curve fully plateaus. They reached a point where additional effort no longer seemed to improve the work, so they decided to stop. For them, the expected marginal benefit had fallen sharply. Access to AI did not bring in higher final quality ratings for these artists. AI became a time-saver but also a mediocrity trap that stopped them.

    The other group did the opposite. They kept working all the way to the full 150 minutes. But this extra time did not significantly improve the art’s quality. The final quality ratings were not noticeably different from those who stopped earlier. In other words, the quality plateaued in the time allowed. This was the group with low marginal costs.

    In art, “good enough” is often acceptable. But what happens in settings where precision is more important, such as software engineering? Model Evaluation & Threat Research (METR) explored this question in a software development field experiment. They recruited sixteen experienced open-source developers and gave them real tasks drawn from large, mature codebases that the developers already knew well. These were projects for which the contributors had, on average, years of prior experience. Tasks were randomly assigned to an “AI allowed” condition or a “no AI” condition. The researchers measured how long the same kinds of tasks took to complete in each mode.9

    The result was the opposite of what almost everyone expected. With access to frontier coding assistants, developers took about 19 percent longer to finish their tasks. Interestingly, the developers felt like they completed the work faster. Before starting, they predicted a substantial speedup, and afterward they still believed that AI had helped them. But the stopwatch showed the reverse.

    This finding is in line with the two effects in the “helicopter drop” logic. AI gets you to the high-altitude camp instantly, generating 90 percent of the code quickly. But because you did not make the climb yourself, you lack the knowledge that would let you finish the last mile quickly. At the same time, the companion effect is still present. AI lowers the cost of continuing to work, and so you keep going because bugs need to be fixed. Because the rate of progress is lower, and because the programmers won’t stop until they reach a sufficiently high level of output, the entire process takes even longer than before.

    The organizational standard-setting problem

    The 90/10 production function creates a problem that is both organizational and individual. When AI gets everyone to 90 percent instantly, the organization faces a new sorting challenge: How do you distinguish workers who pushed to 98 percent from those who stopped at 91 percent? The surface looks identical. The verification costs we analyzed in Chapter 10 make the problem worse because the manager who reviews the output cannot easily tell which version represents serious effort.

    The complementarity logic from chapter 7 applies here. An organization’s quality standards, incentive systems, and evaluation processes form a cluster. If the production function changes trajectory from a slow climb to a helicopter drop with a plateau, the entire cluster must adjust. An organization that still rewards volume of polished output is rewarding the 90 percent that AI provides for free.

    The first step is to stop treating all output equally. An organization’s work can be roughly divided into two categories of tasks. The first is commodity tasks, such as preparing meeting minutes, formatting data for spreadsheets, or writing routine status updates. In these cases, a 90 percent quality rating is good enough. The second is star tasks, such as a client presentation or a high-stakes negotiation, where success requires the last 10 percent as well.

    For commodity tasks, the goal is competence, not excellence. For these tasks, if the AI gets you to 90 percent, the organization should accept that and move on. Spending an hour to polish it further is a waste. But star tasks are quite different. For these, the difference between good and excellent is crucial. The organization needs to invest the time to elevate the work from acceptable to excellent.

    The harder step is to redesign incentives to reward the last 10 percent on star tasks rather than rewarding volume across all tasks. An organization that measures productivity by volume will get mountains of 90 percent work and very little excellence.

    For the individual, the implications are the same. You should sort your own work into commodity and star tasks before you start. The mistake many people make is treating every task like a star task.

    This brings us back to where we began. Jason Allen did not win at the Colorado State Fair by pressing a button. He won because he treated the piece as a star task and refused to stop at the plateau. His eighty hours of prompt iteration and twenty hours of manual refinement were not wasted effort; they were the price of the last 10 percent. Most people who generate an image with AI look at it, feel impressed, and move on. Allen looked at it, saw what was missing, and kept going. For organizations, the question is whether their systems can identify and reward the Allen in the room, or whether they instead treat his hundred hours of work the same as someone else’s five minutes.

    Key Takeaway

    AI alters the production curve into a “helicopter drop,” instantly completing 90% of a task but leaving workers with a “knowledge deficit” for the final 10%. Workers must avoid this mediocrity trap by distinguishing between acceptable commodity tasks and star tasks that demand meticulous refinement.

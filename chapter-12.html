    The Career Ladder Is Broken

    How AI Closed the Training Pipeline and How to Rebuild It

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Early CareerTraining & ApprenticeshipHiring & Screening
    Share

    In The Skill Code, organizational scholar Matthew Beane describes what happened when robotic surgery systems entered hospital operating rooms. The technology was extraordinary for senior surgeons: precise, efficient, and “iPhone-easy” to use. But it was a disaster for training. Robotic systems concentrate the procedure in the hands of a single operator at a console. The attending surgeon has no reason to share control with a trainee because doing so means a slower operation and greater risk to the patient. So residents stand to the side and watch. But watching a movie does not make one an actor. The residents never developed the hands-on skills that surgeons always built through years of practice.1

    The consequences show up when the residents become independent surgeons. In one case, a young surgeon named Kristen sweated at the console when she started her first independent job. She hesitated and, as a result, burned and cut a lot of extra tissue. The surgery, which would have taken three hours, took seven. The patient lost about ten times more blood than otherwise would have occurred.

    This problem is not limited to the medical sector. In 2024, a tech executive told New York Times columnist Kevin Roose that his company had stopped hiring software engineers below L5, which is a mid-level title typically requiring three to seven years of experience. The reason was simple: AI coding tools could handle lower-level tasks. Another founder reported that his startup employed a single data scientist to do work that required a team of seventy-five at his previous company.2 The Wall Street Journal reported that marketing agencies had all but stopped requesting entry-level staff.3 Kate Smaje, McKinsey’s head of technology and AI, put it bluntly to Bloomberg in 2025: “Do we need armies of business analysts creating PowerPoints? No, technology could do that.”4

    These are not isolated anecdotes. Although the evidence is still emerging, SignalFire found that the hiring of new graduates had fallen 25 percent.5 The New York Fed documented youth unemployment rising 30 percent since 2020, twice the rate of all workers.6 This happens because the economic logic that sustained those positions has changed. AI can now do the work that junior employees used to do, the work that taught them to become senior employees.

    Economists Lichtinger and Hosseini Maasoum gave this pattern a name: seniority-biased technological change. Analyzing millions of resumes, they found that productivity gains accrue to those who already possess expertise while junior hiring drops. The technology helps experts but eliminates the positions through which people become experts.7

    The importance of learning-by-doing

    To understand what AI is breaking, we must first look at how professional expertise is actually forged in the real world. Formal education provides the theoretical foundation of a profession, but mastery requires physical execution. When we look at the data across entirely different professions, a clear empirical reality emerges: Expertise is the direct byproduct of doing the work.

    In a landmark 1962 paper, the Nobel Prize-winning economist Kenneth Arrow provided a fascinating review of empirical evidence to demonstrate this.8 He pointed to the Horndal ironworks in Sweden, a plant that had seen no new investment in machinery or changes in production methods for fifteen years. Yet, over that same period, output per worker steadily rose by 2 percent annually. The workers and managers simply got better at the process through repetition. Arrow also highlighted a famous 1936 study of airplane airframes, which showed a remarkable and highly predictable phenomenon: the number of labor hours required to assemble an airframe declined steadily as the total number of airframes previously produced increased. Engineers and assembly line workers weren’t taking breaks to study aeronautics textbooks; they were getting faster and better simply by building more planes. The physical act of production was the mechanism of learning.

    A few years after Arrow’s paper, Bruce Henderson and the Boston Consulting Group (BCG) popularized this phenomenon into the famous “experience curve,” demonstrating that across many industries, a company’s unit costs predictably fall by a fixed percentage every time its cumulative production volume doubles. Entire organizations learn by doing.9 Decades later, Malcolm Gladwell coined the individual equivalent of this concept with his (not scientific, but catchy) “10,000-hour rule.” Whether you are an aviation plant, a global consultancy, or a single human being, you cannot read your way to expertise; you have to put in the work.10

    The importance of on-the-job training also holds true in professional sports. Researchers examined the career trajectories of English soccer players whose teams were narrowly relegated to a lower division.11 Intuitively, dropping to a lesser league seems like a devastating career setback. However, because relegated teams face severe budget cuts, they can no longer afford expensive veteran stars. Out of necessity, they thrust their younger, less-experienced players onto the field. Because these rookies got to play rather than warm the bench for an elite team, they accumulated so much human capital that, five to seven years later, they ended up playing in better leagues and earning higher wages. Doing the work built the career.

    Conversely, when professionals are denied the opportunity to practice, their skills decay. Researchers looked at public school teachers in Greece who were placed on long waitlists before receiving formal teaching assignments. They calculated a skill depreciation rate of 4.3 percent per year.12 When human capital is not used, it atrophies. Missing out on the early returns of on-the-job experience leaves a permanent scar on a professional’s capability.

    Important as on-the-job experience is, young workers need to be guided by expert mentorship to develop true mastery. When a novice works alongside a master, tacit knowledge, such as tastes and problem-solving heuristics, is transferred. A fascinating study on Western music composition since 1450 proves just how durable this imprint is. By analyzing the musical structures of thousands of compositions over five centuries, the study found that students absorb and replicate the distinct stylistic elements of their direct mentors. Furthermore, students who emulate high-quality mentors are significantly more likely to become top-tier composers themselves, passing down this “acquired taste” across generations.13

    The examples above illustrate the importance of on-the-job experience and training. But if expertise requires a massive amount of practice guided by experts, how do firms afford to let novices work enough to learn, without wasting the precious time of the experts?

    Knowledge hierarchy: two birds with one stone

    The answer lies in how work itself is organized. In any profession, tasks range from the routine to the exceptional: from reviewing standard contracts to restructuring a bankrupt conglomerate. This diversity in task difficulty is the key. If every task required senior expertise, there would be no way to let novices practice without the guidance of experts. But most tasks do not. The vast majority of problems at a law firm, a hospital, or a consulting practice are common, well-understood, and low stakes. A much smaller number of problems are unusual.

    This natural distribution of difficulty makes it possible to build an organization that kills two birds with one stone. The structure is the knowledge hierarchy, which we touched upon earlier in the book. The knowledge hierarchy enables the organization to use expertise efficiently and train the next generation at the same time.

    To use expertise efficiently, organizations should not require the most expensive expert to answer the easiest questions. It is better to push routine work down and funnel exceptions up. Juniors handle the common, low-stakes problems at the bottom. When they cannot solve something, they escalate. Rare, high-stakes exceptions flow to fewer, more knowledgeable experts at the top. When partners can focus their attention on the hardest problems, the knowledge hierarchy allows their expertise to be leveraged.

    But the knowledge hierarchy serves a second, equally important function: it trains people.

    It would seem natural for firms to simply train their workers, but economist Gary Becker identified a fundamental problem with workplace training.14 Training creates valuable skills, and valuable skills command higher wages on the open market. This is particularly the case when the skills being transferred are generally applicable across firms. If a firm invests time and money into training a worker today, competition will force the firm to pay a higher wage tomorrow to retain her. Unless the firm has some form of monopsony power, which is the ability to restrict workers’ mobility and pay them less than their market value, it will be unwilling to train them. Otherwise, rivals can simply swoop in, offer a higher wage, and poach the newly trained employee, reaping the benefits without paying for her education.

    To solve this, the junior workers would need to pay for the training, since they are the ones who will reap the future wage benefits. But young workers are financially constrained. They have rent, groceries, and student loans to pay. Firms can hardly charge a junior employee a “negative wage” to cover the cost of the time senior experts spend mentoring them.

    The knowledge hierarchy solves this problem through an elegant bargain that one of us (Garicano) developed in 2017 with Northwestern University economist Luis Rayo. Unable to pay for their apprenticeship with cash, junior workers pay with their labor. Because they are not yet highly productive, the arrangement has them perform massive volumes of routine, foundational work at the bottom of the pyramid. They accept lower wages than their output justifies, generating revenue for the firm while they learn.

    Consider again that first-year associate. She bills at $400 per hour for document reviews. The client pays for productive work, not training, but this revenue exceeds her salary and costs. The surplus subsidizes the time partners spend mentoring her, the training programs she attends, and the learning that occurs as she tackles progressively harder work.

    The associate accepts lower wages than her output justifies in exchange for skills that will command higher wages later. The firm extracts immediate value from her work, recouping training costs before she might leave for a competitor. Both sides benefit because the same activity produces two outputs at once. The associate reviewing contracts is doing billable work and learning to spot problems. The work pays for the learning while the learning makes future work more valuable.

    This bargain has held since medieval apprenticeships. The apprentice worked for room and board while learning the craft. The master captured cheap labor while transferring skills. The arrangement worked because learning and productive work were inseparable. You could not learn blacksmithing without making horseshoes. And those horseshoes could be sold.

    How AI changes the career ladder

    The AI-Becker problem

    From the standpoint of productivity, delegating routine work to AI looks like an unambiguous improvement. The machine absorbs low-level questions, filters out noise, and ensures that scarce expert attention is reserved for the hardest problems. Output rises. Costs fall. The organization becomes leaner. McKinsey’s AI tool Lilli now answers half a million queries a month, generating the foundational research that once occupied armies of junior consultants. Allen & Overy’s AI tools automate contract drafting and due diligence that first-year associates used to bill thousands of hours performing.

    But this efficiency gain comes with a fatal hidden cost. If a machine can do the routine work for pennies, junior employees lose their currency. They no longer have anything of immediate economic value to offer the firm in exchange for their training.

    We call this the AI-Becker problem.15 In Gary Becker’s original analysis, companies underinvest in training because rivals can poach trained workers. In the AI era, companies underinvest because juniors simply cannot generate the revenue required to fund their own apprenticeships. The poaching problem assumes that training would happen somewhere. The AI-Becker problem suggests it might not happen at all. As Garicano found in recent research with Luis Rayo, the currency an apprentice previously offered for her training (the menial, routine work) depreciates when AI can perform these tasks.16

    Beane documented this pattern in robotic surgery, which we described at the opening of this chapter. The technology that made the expert more productive blocked the path through which new experts were made. The same dynamic is now playing out across knowledge work. If AI drafts contracts, builds financial models, and generates the research that junior consultants used to produce, those juniors will never get the chance to do the work themselves. And without opportunities to struggle through a problem, make mistakes, and learn to recognize what went wrong, they cannot develop the expertise and judgment that the work was supposed to build.

    The delayed catastrophe

    Forward-looking executives are already sounding the alarm about the consequences of hollowing out this pipeline. AWS CEO Matt Garman recently criticized the short-sighted impulse to cut entry-level roles to capture immediate savings. “How’s that going to work when ten years in the future you have no one that has learned anything?”17

    The crisis will not arrive immediately, which is both reassuring and dangerous. Today’s senior professionals were trained traditionally. They possess judgment that AI lacks. They have the ability to recognize when AI errs, to spot the exception that requires human attention, to direct AI toward the right problems. With AI assistance, they are more productive than ever. Because this expertise has a long shelf life, firms can function for years without noticing that they are no longer investing in a future generation of experts. The senior workers will retire eventually, and when that occurs in five or ten years, firms will realize the pipeline of mid-level talent is empty. We are consuming expertise without replanting it.

    What determines whether careers survive is a race between two forces. AI raises the floor by automating entry-level work. But AI also raises the ceiling by making expert work more valuable. If the ceiling rises faster than the floor, there will continue to be room for training and the career will still be worth pursuing. If the floor rises faster than the ceiling, then training will compress and eventually collapse.

    Current evidence suggests the floor is rising faster. Entry-level hiring is freezing while senior retention remains stable. Productivity gains are accruing to those who already possess expertise. The pipeline is narrowing.

    How organizations can rebuild their pipeline

    Organizations must either invest in developing new talent or face the delayed catastrophe that Matt Garman described. Three responses are possible, though none is easy.

    First, organizations can restructure junior roles around verification and quality assurance rather than routine production. If AI produces the first draft, the junior worker’s job shifts from drafting to checking: tracing citations, stress-testing assumptions, catching costly errors. This work is genuinely difficult, but it builds real expertise and is hard to automate. It also solves a second problem, which is the verifier’s curse. It does this by putting fresh eyes on AI-generated output. The junior learns by checking and the organization gets the quality assurance it desperately needs. The economic bargain is preserved because the junior worker’s verification efforts pay for her training.

    Second, organizations can invest in rotations and shadowing programs that expose juniors to the messy, political, relational work described in chapters 6 and 7. If the routine cognitive tasks that once served as training currency disappear, then the training must shift to the tasks that remain: coordination, negotiation, implementation. These skills are acquired only through practice, but they can be practiced alongside experienced practitioners if the organization deliberately creates those opportunities. The Compstat story from chapter 7 illustrates the point: Jack Maple learned policing on the street, not in a classroom.

    Third, organizations can create internal “apprenticeship markets” where junior employees bid for difficult assignments by demonstrating capability through upfront work. Instead of assigning routine tasks that AI now handles, the organization offers messy, judgment-intensive projects and lets juniors compete for them by showing they can contribute. This is a more competitive model than traditional apprenticeship, and it will not suit every worker. But it aligns the training pipeline with the skills that matter in the AI era.

    For the individual worker, the organizational logic determines the strategy. If you are early in your career or trying to start a second one, AI will be a serious challenge. On the traditional path, you would have expected to grind through routine work for several years, use that “currency” to pay for the training, and eventually graduate to more senior responsibilities. That path is closing. You can no longer wait for training to come to you. Some alternatives are provided by AI itself.

    The AI tutor. Anyone with an internet connection has access to a tutor of extraordinary patience and enormous knowledge that will explain a concept in ten different ways without irritation, adjust to any level, and work across every domain. Young professionals can use the system to build deep knowledge and learn to learn. As models get better, fewer humans will be good enough to add value at the routine level. To exercise judgment, you must know a domain deeply, but the specific knowledge you learn today will depreciate faster than ever. What matters is the slope of your learning curve. Choose the job where you will learn the most, but also the one where you will learn to learn.

    This does not solve the AI-Becker problem. A tutor is not a career. It cannot give you the clients, the deadlines, the colleagues, or the network. But it allows you to pursue ambitious strategies that would have been unthinkable a little while ago. Here are some ideas.

    Entrepreneurship. There has perhaps never been a better time to strike out on your own. In the past, becoming an entrepreneur early in a career was daunting because it required you to be a jack of all trades. You needed to code, design, sell, keep books, and understand your market, all before you had revenue to hire anyone. The jack-of-all-trades problem killed most ventures before they found product-market fit. You often failed at whichever was your weakest link.

    Today, as we discussed in chapter 4, AI relaxes this constraint. You can use AI to handle your weak links while you focus on your core tasks. If established firms will no longer invest in your training, you can train yourself by building something of your own.

    The smaller pond. Historically, landing an entry-level job at a prestigious bank, hospital, or consultancy almost guaranteed a good career because the on-the-job learning was best. But if elite firms are deploying AI for entry-level tasks and sidelining junior workers to merely watch the machine work, that premium vanishes. Watching a senior doctor operate on a patient at a console does not make you an expert.

    The smaller pond may be a good alternative route. This might be a regional firm or a niche where you are slightly overqualified and therefore trusted with real responsibility from the start. In those environments, you will get to do things and learn from practical experience. Remember the lesson from English soccer players. They succeeded in the long run because dropping to a lower league gave them more experience in the game.

    Seek scarcity. A top corporate lawyer at the peak of his discipline is rare but not unique. A top corporate lawyer who also understands machine learning well enough to structure deals around AI assets is almost nonexistent. Many people have specific skills but almost no one has a combination of AI technology knowledge and domain-specific capacity. Scarcity lives at such intersections. Scarcity does not live at the peak of a single discipline. These “scarcity intersections” are where the messy jobs can be found. They require the ability to manage unsolved problems, exceptions, and conflicts. A person who can operate credibly on both sides of the AI threshold will be harder to replace. In the past, building depth in two domains meant two long apprenticeships. Now the AI tutor reduces those learning curves.

    Key Takeaway

    Because AI automates the routine tasks that historically funded junior apprenticeships, the training pipeline for future experts is collapsing. Organizations must rebuild this ladder by restructuring junior roles around complex verification tasks, messy coordination projects, or internal apprenticeship markets that actively cultivate necessary human judgment.

    Orchestrating Relationships

    Why Implementation Is Hard

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    LeadershipAuthorityOrganisations
    Share

    In the late 1980s, American police departments began buying computers. By the early 2000s, most departments had adopted information technology (IT) for tasks like filing crime reports, dispatching, and administration. After such large investments, everyone believed that better information would mean better policing, and everyone expected a productivity boost. That did not happen.

    One of us (Garicano), working with John Heaton of the Rand Corporation, obtained data from nearly all police departments in the United States between 1987 and 2003. They tracked the adoption of IT and its effects on crime clearance rates and reported crime. The results were surprising. IT adoption was associated with a roughly 10 percent increase in reported crime! We discovered this was not because crime increased; rather, the new systems made it easier to file reports. That created more paperwork without improving outcomes. IT made the bureaucracy more thorough, not more productive.1

    However, the productivity gains did appear when technology was combined with deep organizational change. The successful model that diffused through the US was Compstat, the management system pioneered by Bill Bratton in the New York Police Department in the 1990s. If you have seen The Wire, the classic TV show about policing in Baltimore, you know that Compstat was a bundle of complementary practices that revolutionized police work. It involved using information technology to geolocate crime in real time, training the workforce to interpret and react to the data, deploying the police proactively to the hot spots, and holding the precinct commanders accountable for results. Departments that implemented all these elements of the Compstat bundle saw clearance rates increase by roughly 3.2 percentage points and crime rates decrease by about 5 percent. Departments that adopted only parts of the bundle gained little or nothing.2

    We are only three years into AI implementation, but many of the conversations we have had with organizations sound to us like pre-Compstat policing. A patrolman with a computer does not necessarily do better policing. Likewise, a human-resources employee with access to ChatGPT will not automatically improve human resource management. Yes, some queries will be answered more quickly, and some documents will be better organized, but nothing will change in her day-to-day work.

    Here’s the problem. Organizations everywhere are adopting AI tools, but they are layering them on top of existing workflows. They are using AI to draft emails, summarize meetings, and produce drafts without changing how work is organized. The technology is spreading rapidly, but the organizational change that could make it productive is not.

    This chapter explains why that gap exists, what kind of person can close it, and why that person sits firmly on the human side of the autonomy threshold. Implementing technological change is not about exercising power within the existing system. It is about redesigning the system itself.

    The shadow AI economy

    Here’s an anecdote that illustrates what we mean by the “shadow AI economy.” When one of us visited a large central bank, he encountered two officials who each had two PCs on their desks. One was owned by the bank and the other was owned by the official. The bank PC was locked, without access to large language models. Hence, officials did what many knowledge workers do everywhere: They handled most tasks on the safe machine, but they kept their personal laptop open, with a window on ChatGPT or Claude, ready to help them write, compute, or analyze anything their official desktop could not do.

    In this shadow AI economy, employees use consumer AI tools, including their own accounts, machines, and mobile phones, to automate parts of their work without informing the company’s IT, legal, or compliance departments. Microsoft’s 2024 Work Trend Index found that 75 percent of knowledge workers use AI at work, with 78 percent bringing their own tools. Microsoft and LinkedIn call this “Bring Your Own AI.”3 These same organizations publish press releases and set up large internal town halls to celebrate their “responsible AI road map.” They launch pilots, undertake proofs of concept, and run demos to impress the CEO and clients. Despite all that activity, the workflow of the organization is untouched.

    The official AI rollout is stuck in what consultants call “pilot purgatory.” The project does not die, because everyone can point to activity, but it does not live either, because it never becomes “how we do things here.” In Microsoft’s survey, 60 percent of leaders said their company lacks a vision and plan to implement AI.

    Since the workflows do not change, any productivity gain that may happen is small, and it is captured by workers in the form of leisure. A top executive of a large firm told us that when he measures the productivity of individual workers in a controlled setting, he sees large impacts from AI. However, when he looks at the productivity of the entire organization, he sees nothing. In his view, IT engineers are “smoking the AI productivity gains in the corridor.”

    The gap between capabilities and productivity is not caused by the technology; rather, the problem is implementation. The structural gap between what AI can do and what organizations actually do with it is growing large enough to create many messy jobs.

    Why organizational change is so hard

    Organizations are systems of interlocking processes, routines, incentives, and decisions. When a new technology fits neatly into the existing architecture, adoption is simple. If the technology to make tires improves, the next generation of cars will have better tires. Nothing else about the car, or about the organization that builds it, needs to change. This is what Rebecca Henderson and Kim Clark (1990) called “component innovation”: a better part slots into an unchanged system.4

    Contrast this incremental innovation with the transition from internal combustion engines to electric vehicles. Here everything about producing cars must change. The central role of mechanical engineers and chemical engineers who are experts in combustion becomes irrelevant. Adopting the radical innovation of electric vehicles requires changing the entire architecture of the organization.

    Henderson and Clark showed that it is this second kind of change, what they called “architectural innovation,” that destroys the jobs of incumbents. With architectural innovation, the new component is not hard to build, but the relationships between all the components must be redesigned simultaneously. The resistance to these changes stems not only from workers’ ignorance about the new technology; it is also deeply rooted in the workers’ strong adherence to the old architecture. Existing firms have routines, communication channels, reward systems, and organizational cultures all tuned to the old way of doing things. Everyone must unlearn and undo all these practices before they can adopt the new ones.

    Generally speaking, three distinct problems make architectural innovation so hard: complementarities that require simultaneous changes, fog that obscures the destination, and resistance from those who lose. These obstacles define the problem. They also define the type of person who can solve it.

    Complementarities and interdependencies

    Economists Paul Milgrom and John Roberts have shown that organizational features tend to cluster together when they fit with each other; that is, when having one feature makes having another one more valuable.5 For instance, in traditional mass production, the cluster of organizational features includes long runs, standardized products, narrow job definitions, inventory buffers, and hierarchical control. In contrast, Toyota-style modern manufacturing is another type of cluster: short runs, customized products, flexible workers, just-in-time delivery, and decentralized decision-making. You cannot pick one feature from each cluster and expect it to work in another cluster. The pieces fit together as a system.

    This is why deep organizational innovation is so difficult. It requires many features to change together. Researchers who studied steel-finishing lines found that plants that adopted individualized “modern” human resources practices, such as team or incentive pay, saw little improvement in productivity. Only plants that adopted the full cluster of modern practices achieved large productivity gains. The reason is that the effectiveness of changing one practice depends on what is done with the others. Contrary to common advice, starting small when making changes can be a mistake if the parts of the organization are interdependent.6

    If an organization has a large number of interlocking features, and if a new technology such as AI affects the productivity of each task, then a large number of changes have to be made simultaneously to take advantage of the organizational change. And this is not easy.

    Fog

    Barriers to architectural innovation become worse when firms do not know which direction to go. The map is blurry, and the path to a better outcome is unclear.

    The difficulty of architectural innovation was summarized by organizational sociologist James March, who drew a distinction between exploitation and exploration.7 Exploitation means refining what you already do: optimizing existing processes, cutting slack, and squeezing more from known routines. Exploration means searching for entirely new ways of doing things. Architectural innovation requires organizations to explore, but they are staffed with people whose skills, habits, and incentive structures are built for exploitation. People do things according to their habits, and their habits are often adapted to the old regime.

    This struggle is compounded by “fog,” which occurs when the production function of the new regime is unknown. In the context of AI implementation, no one can discern in advance what the right combination of AI tools, workflow redesign, skill requirements, and communication channels will look like. The range of possible configurations is enormous. Trial and error is the only way forward, but that is expensive and slow, and most trials will fail.

    An example of this is the introduction of electronic health records in US hospitals. After the US federal government passed the HITECH Act in 2009 with $27 billion in incentives to adopt electronic health records, everyone assumed that digitizing patient information would improve care, reduce errors, and cut costs. Adoption took place quickly, and by the mid-2010s, most hospitals used at least a basic EHR system.8

    But the productivity gains did not happen. Physicians spent more time entering data and less time with patients. One study showed that physicians spent about 49 percent of their day on EHR and desk work, and only 27 percent with patients.9 Hospitals had adopted the technology, but nobody knew what the right clinical workflow around the EHR should look like. Each physician had their own work habits. More than a decade and tens of billions of dollars later, the process is still ongoing.

    This is the same pattern Paul David identified in the diffusion of the electric dynamo.10 Factories started to use the electric dynamo in the 1880s and 1890s, but productivity growth from the technology did not appear in the aggregate statistics until the 1920s. The reason for this long lag was that factory owners had to discover, by experimentation over decades, that electrification required redesigning the entire factory floor and reorganizing the labor force. The technology was available but the knowledge of how to use it was not. The EHR story, far more recent and still unresolved, tells us the same thing about information technology in complex organizations. There is every reason to believe AI will follow the same path.

    Resistance to change

    Even when the organization’s destination is clear, getting there requires overcoming the resistance of those who will not benefit. Resistance to change is not irrational. It follows from the distribution of costs and benefits.

    The participation or resistance of workers will affect the process of organizational change. In most firms there will be winners and losers from the deep technology adoption. The winners are those who benefit from the technology to do better work. The losers will experience how the new technology takes over some of their core tasks or removes the gatekeeping role that previously gave them influence.

    These diverse responses will strongly affect how readily a new technology is adopted. If the threatened group does not cooperate with the reorganization plan, for example by refusing to provide access to data or to document how the process actually works, then the plan’s implementation will falter. Resistance to change might not involve a dramatic rebellion. It may show up as constant frictions and obstacles. “Apologies, but we cannot release that dataset yet.” “We are running another risk assessment.” “We have not concluded the training.”

    Paradoxically, resistance will be more successful the more profitable the organization has been in the past.11 If the organization has incumbency power because of a strong brand, a large installed base that cannot switch away, or market power for other reasons, then the resisters have larger bargaining power because the organization will not be put at immediate risk due to their lack of cooperation in adopting AI. By contrast, the workers of a startup cannot play stalling games. If a key decision on technology adoption takes six months of haggling in committees, the startup will die.

    Middle managers are often the strongest resisters. They have valuable tacit knowledge and a lot of (justified) fear. Middle managers have power because they often control the noncontractible inputs that major organizational change requires: informal knowledge, undocumented processes, and discretionary cooperation. They are the ones who know how the undocumented spreadsheets work and they have institutional memory about all the inner workings of the company and its many processes. Furthermore, when new technology compresses the hierarchy, allowing frontline employees to process information that used to flow through the middle, or allowing top managers to reach down to the shop floor directly, middle managers are the most likely losers. They have reason to resist.

    Several levers can break the stalemate. Competition is the most reliable. A firm that is losing customers cannot afford another six months of committee meetings, and resisters lose bargaining power once the alternative to change is collapse. Firms can also try to buy cooperation through retraining programs or job guarantees, but these promises are hard to enforce once the threatened group has handed over control of the process, which is precisely why the threatened group tends not to accept them. A third option is to build a separate unit for the new technology, walled off from the incumbent operation. This eliminates the leverage of the resisters by cutting them off from where the change is happening, but it also sacrifices the synergies that made the incumbent valuable in the first place.

    The implementer as a distinct scarcity

    We have described three obstacles to implementation: simultaneous changes in many parts of the organization, unknown destination, and political resistance. These obstacles delineate why implementation is hard. They also explain why organizational change requires a specific kind of person.

    In the previous chapter, we analyzed why organizations need humans who can exercise authority, the managers who can decide between conflicting goals and make the decision stick. That authority is a permanent feature of organizations. It does not depend on whether the organization is changing or stable. Someone must always decide who wins and who loses. Implementation is not about exercising power within the existing system. It is about redesigning the system itself. This is not about sitting in a chair deciding between existing departments that disagree. Implementation requires dismantling the chair, moving it to a different place or getting rid of it, and persuading those who liked the old arrangement to sit down again.

    The scarcity that this type of change requires is different. It is not just about legitimacy, the ability to bear consequences, and the political standing to make decisions stick. Implementation leads to a cognitive scarcity: the ability to hold three different maps of the organization in her head simultaneously and to see where they conflict.

    Bundling three kinds of knowledge

    Process knowledge. One of us recently had a long conversation with the founder of a large software firm. He is in the midst of deeply restructuring the entire organization, which means doing a lot of delayering. He pointed out a type of scarcity that he frequently encounters: Most senior managers have no idea what is going on in the lower levels of the organization. The layers in between serve to distribute the work and to deal with the routine work, but they also serve to obscure the work of the entire organization, like fog. How many people do we really need for the work we do? Who is actually doing productive work and who is not? This question is surprisingly opaque.

    Knowledge of the workflow is tacit, which makes it hard to identify. The work does not happen in the way the organizational chart says it happens. Every organization has an official process and a real one. For example, to approve a loan, the loan officer needs to jump through many steps, but the real process involves a senior analyst who has been there for twenty years and who knows, from experience, which applications will trigger a regulatory flag. Everyone routes the tricky cases through her desk. She is not on the org chart. She does not have a title that reflects this function. But if you try to redesign the loan approval workflow without understanding her role, the new system will break.

    Knowledge about the workflow cannot be acquired from documents or data. It requires spending time on the ground. Workflow knowledge is tacit, local, and likely to be tightly guarded by those who have it.

    Technology knowledge. This is the knowledge about how AI actually works, beyond the demo; that is, what it can do when pointed at the messy, unstructured data of the organization. Most CEOs have seen dozens of presentations about AI. They have a sense of what the tools can do, but most have not installed Claude Code on their computers and tried to produce a finished analysis. As a result, they rarely understand what AI can do and what its limitations are.

    One top executive we spoke to admitted he only started using AI when he was pressed for time on a deliverable. He was stunned by what the tools could do. Another CEO of a top consulting firm we know changed her attitude toward AI adoption completely after spending two hours sitting next to one of her junior associates, watching him obtain results that she had assumed were years away. Without using AI for oneself, or at least sitting close to someone who is using it, people can’t develop knowledge about the technology’s real limitations and value.

    Successful implementation of organizational change requires people who understand both what AI cannot do in real-world contexts and the astonishingly rapid evolution of AI tools and hence can understand what the tools are about to be able to do.

    Political knowledge. The political knowledge required to change an organizational process is difficult to find and develop. Implementing drastic changes requires managing a transition during which it is not obvious who the winners and losers may be. This uncertainty is unlikely to be resolved quickly.

    The senior manager, the middle manager, and the entry-level manager must cooperate. They must document the tacit knowledge, knowing that training the system makes them less indispensable. They must all participate in a redesign effort that may make at least some of them superfluous.

    Jack Maple and the Compstat battle

    You could argue that a process consultant should redesign the workflow, a change management person should negotiate with the workers, and an AI expert should handle the technology. The problem is that the three types of expertise are interrelated, and any decision on one dimension will affect the others.

    The Compstat story that opened this chapter illustrates why the bundle cannot be split apart. Jack Maple, a working-class transit lieutenant from Queens, wrote a letter to the top leadership of the NYPD, jumping a few layers up the chain, sharing his ideas for how to improve policing using geolocation data. Bill Bratton, the police commissioner, read it and said, “This guy is smart,” and pulled him up.12

    Maple had the required bundle of skills (politics, policing, data) in his head because he had lived at the street level and knew the organizational problem at the same time. He was not an engineer, but he understood data as a tool. He was obsessed with geolocating crime long before the digital tools existed. In fact, he covered fifty-five feet of wall space with maps and tracked crimes and clearances with crayons. He called the whole system “Charts of the Future.”13

    Then, with Bratton backing him, he changed the entire process of policing in New York. In addition to centralizing crime data, he also noticed that the specialized police units were working 9 to 5, Monday to Friday, even though criminals worked nights and weekends. Maple corrected that illogical situation quickly.14 But implementing the change was not easy. As Bratton put it, the Compstat meetings became “the grand theater where precinct commanders are called to account” and sometimes “browbeaten” in front of their peers.15 Commanders hated it: it was “a pain,” they said, to drag your staff to headquarters and require them to “report to this transit lieutenant.” The questioning could get “pretty heated.”16 But this was the enforcement mechanism that made the whole bundle hold together: the data was used, the deployments were changed, and the tactics followed.

    Maple was the perfect implementer of change. He did not invent the tool, but he knew on-the-ground policing, the organization, and how to make the parts move together. He held the three pieces of knowledge (process, technology, and politics) in his head simultaneously. No committee of specialists could have done what he did.

    To us, the moral of the story is that people with this bundle of knowledge are scarce. They exist, but they are often buried below the lofty levels of the hierarchy. The perfect implementer is not going to be the CTO or CFO. She is likely someone down at the street level who knows intimately the relevant processes.

    Maintaining trust

    Imagine an organization that has been operating beautifully for some time. Processes, incentives, communication, and division of labor all fit together. There are some imperfections, of course, but it works. Everyone knows what to expect.

    Then an implementer like Jack Maple appears. He brings a new vision and goals: a higher peak over there, on the other side of a long valley. To get there, everyone must change the way they work at the same time. So, for a while, things will be a mess. Productivity will drop. Errors will increase.

    How do you bring people through such a change? You may have the maps and the conviction, but there will be people who stand to lose from the changes, and they will be waiting to cry wolf whenever there are errors. The AI production function is unknown, and no one really knows if the change is going to work.

    That is why the implementer must be a person, or a small team, with enough credibility and know-how about the three dimensions described above to earn trust. It cannot be someone who exercises power from above. It should be someone who has been close enough to the work to understand what is being asked of people. The person should be credible enough that other workers will trust her to lead them through the valley to the destination. Maple could drag precinct commanders to headquarters because he had been on the street himself. He knew what he was asking them to change, and they knew he knew.

    In summary, the implementer must be deep enough in the organization to understand the real workflow, technically fluent enough to see what the new tools make possible, and politically skilled enough to hold a coalition together while everything temporarily gets worse. That combination is rare, which is why the role is irreducibly human.

    BBVA: escaping pilot purgatory

    You would not expect BBVA to be nimble. It is a global bank with operations in twenty-five countries, more than seventy-seven million clients, and about 125,000 workers. It operates under heavy regulation, deep compliance requirements, and a demanding kind of risk culture.

    And yet BBVA did something many firms fail to do: It escaped pilot purgatory. One of us (Garicano), while helping the bank, was able to document how the organization successfully made the escape.17 The bank managed to learn what the AI tools could do, identify how the bank actually worked (despite the fog), and determine where resistance would appear.

    Speed. BBVA’s leaders recognized that a shadow AI economy within the organization was already active. That meant that unmanaged, hidden usage of AI presented a risk. The safe move was to deploy a secure, managed solution aligned with how employees already worked. So they compressed the typical risk assessment, legal review, and compliance cycle into two months. They chose an interface that employees wanted: ChatGPT Enterprise. The program hosted services so that sensitive data stayed with the bank and was not used to train models. They found a team of integrators, which was led by Elena Alfaro, a twenty-year veteran of the bank’s innovation department. She was dynamic, outcome-oriented, and a clear communicator.

    Speed helped the bank bypass veto points. Two months was not enough time for frictions to accumulate. By the time objections could be organized, the tool was already deployed. The organization moved through the painful transition before resistance could organize.

    Scarcity and status as incentives. To ensure fast adoption of AI tools, BBVA used a seduction strategy “without command and control” (sin ordeno y mando). They started with scarcity. Business leaders, called Champions, gave three thousand licenses to use AI tools to the most motivated and committed people, not to the most senior workers. This created fear of missing out (FOMO), turning the tools into a privilege. By establishing a use-it-or-lose-it requirement, the bank ensured that the tools would be utilized as the policy was implemented. And the bank rewarded people for building and sharing custom GPTs, not just clicking.

    These measures changed the internal politics, creating a new status ladder based on usefulness, not rank. Ambitious employees had a reason to push through friction rather than accept it. Instead of fighting gatekeepers head-on, BBVA built a parallel power structure that made adoption of AI tools attractive.

    Autonomy and responsibility. BBVA added an important protection: prompt privacy. No one had access to employees’ chats unless required for compliance. Only what users chose to share became public. That reduced the aversion to using AI, and it eliminated a trust deficit that otherwise would have fed resistance.

    The defining feature of the shadow AI economy is peer learning. Someone finds a prompt that works. Someone else copies it. BBVA moved to formalize that network without suffocating it. They built an Adoption Network with Champions comprising approximately twenty-five regional and country leaders. They also appointed one hundred co-champions and roughly two hundred “Wizards,” power users who provided peer-to-peer support. To support the effort, they set up a Community of Practice, a horizontal forum that became the most active in BBVA’s history. These initiatives reflected the bank leaders’ realization that the fastest way to spread know-how is often not through training modules but through peers.

    The bank’s governance was built around what they called the autonomy-responsibility binomial. The idea is to give employees autonomy to experiment but make them responsible for outputs. They imposed a clear human-in-the-loop principle: AI drafts and a human owns the work. Outputs cannot directly overwrite core systems without validation. They required training, an exam, and a signed procedure document for liability. And, importantly, they avoided a governance bottleneck that would have killed everything. Instead of forcing thousands of user-built GPTs through a centralized risk admission process, they created an automated quality score to evaluate GPTs on indicators like guardrails and clarity. This is the difference between governance as a brake and governance as steering.

    Escape velocity. The results showed what an escape from pilot purgatory looks like. BBVA scaled its program from three thousand to eleven thousand active users. They reported time savings of two to five hours per week (on average). More than 83 percent of enterprise users worked with AI tools each week, with the number of active days increasing from 2.8 to 4.1. On average, workers submitted about fifty prompts per week. And more than 4,800 active GPTs were created internally.

    Among those working in BBVA’s internal audit unit, 100 percent of the people accepted a license to use AI tools. These employees were sponsored by top management and supported by a dedicated technology team. Soon, 99 percent of auditors across all countries were active AI users. Specifically, they used the tools about 4.5 days per week, saving an estimated three to four hours per auditor per week across more than six hundred auditors worldwide.

    Alfaro’s rare combination of talents aligns directly with the implementer archetype. She had two decades of institutional knowledge about how BBVA actually functioned. In other words, she knew the change process map. She also understood the technology well enough to choose the right deployment model and anticipate where it would break. She had the political standing, with the full backing of a tech-savvy chairman (an MIT physics grad and Sloan MBA). And she had the communication skills to hold the coalition together while the organization moved through the valley. Like Maple, she was not the inventor of the tool. She was the person who made it real inside a complex organization.

    Jobs and earnings: the implementation paradox

    For all the cognitive abundance AI will engender, there is one type of work it cannot do: implementing the deep reorganization that our economies must undertake. The scarcity of people able to implement changes in organizations is likely to be a large bottleneck and remain a source of valuable human work for a long period of time.

    A growing demand gap

    As AI rapidly advances, the gap between what the technology can do and what organizations are doing with it grows. As a result, organizations have been leaving money on the table since 2023, when the technology started to advance rapidly, but now in 2026, companies are leaving fortunes on the table.

    There is, therefore, a critical need across the economy for organizations to adopt AI tools and implement corresponding organizational changes. Almost no organization will be untouched. Nonprofits, government organizations, hospitals, and law firms could win some of the larger gains, but (paradoxically) those types of organizations are less likely to implement changes. They can coast on a lot of goodwill and they typically face lower levels of competitive pressure that would otherwise force change.

    The technology is advancing rapidly and every industry needs it at the same time. This means that the demand curves for implementers will shift up simultaneously. As a result, the price for this type of skill will increase significantly.

    Consulting firms are already scrambling to build the capability to implement AI adoption and organizational change. But the three-part bundle described earlier is hard to assemble inside a consulting firm. Consultants can learn the technology and they can develop frameworks for process analysis. But they typically lack the deep, organization-specific workflow knowledge that only comes from years inside an institution. A McKinsey team can diagnose a problem in six weeks, but the implementer who can move the organization through the valley needs the trust that no external firm can provide.

    A limited, rigid supply

    The supply of people with the right skills is probably hard to expand. The three domains that our analysis identifies are not easily susceptible to formal training. The technological part can be obtained formally (or through online learning), but the technology moves so fast that people must constantly relearn. It is more likely that people with technology skills will be self-taught. They will keep experimenting and tinkering with the tools for their own purposes, like Maple drawing crime maps on the wall before geocoding software existed.

    Understanding the workflow is even harder. It requires people to acquire a deep knowledge of each industry or organization. The knowledge about how a bank compliance process works is full of details and specific rules and routines that are hard to learn from a textbook.

    As to politics, being able to identify who will have the motivation and power to block necessary organizational changes, and how to work with those people, is not an easily acquired talent. Likewise, knowing how to build coalitions with those who will benefit from the reorganization can only be learned through deep experience.

    We find Jack Maple’s example instructive. It is best to find someone down the ranks who understands and cares deeply about the substantive problem, and who understands departmental politics, and then teach them about the technology. That is easier than trying to teach a software engineer about politics and processes.

    Regardless, the supply of people of this type is limited and hard to expand. The number of people who combine current technical knowledge, deep workflow knowledge in a specific domain, and the political skill to manage a transformation is small. Training programs can stretch this supply at the margins, but the binding constraint is experiential knowledge, and that takes years to accumulate. No bootcamp can fill the demand gap. Expanding supply probably requires CEOs with the ingenuity of Bill Bratton, who plucked Jack Maple from deep in the ranks of the NYC Transit police.

    AI will not help. The premium here is on skills that AI cannot replicate: tacit knowledge of how organizations work, the ability to build trust, the judgment to know which of a thousand possible reconfigurations to try first.

    Demand for implementation skills is large, synchronized across the economy, and growing with every improvement in AI capability. Supply for these skills is small, hard to expand, and dependent on forms of knowledge that take years to develop. The gap between the two is, we believe, the widest of any scarcity described in this book.

    The evidence, even at this early stage, supports our view. A 2024 Boston Consulting Group survey of nearly 1,800 C-suite executives found that only 25 percent of companies had moved beyond the pilot phase to scaled AI deployment. The remaining 75 percent were stuck in various stages of experimentation. Only 26 percent of companies have developed the capabilities to move beyond proofs of concept and generate tangible value.18 This is not for lack of ambition or spending. Global corporate investment in AI exceeded $200 billion in 2024.19 The money is flowing. The transformation is not. The bottleneck is not the technology or the capital. The need is for people who can make the technology work inside complex, messy organizations.

    The market implication follows directly from the analysis. The financial returns to those with implementation skills will rise as long as AI capability continues to advance faster than organizational adoption, which is to say, for a long time. This is not a temporary bottleneck that will be resolved as firms learn. It is a structural feature of how organizations work. The complementarities, the fog, and the resistance described in this chapter are permanent properties of complex institutions. AI makes the prize larger; it does not make the obstacles smaller.

    Conclusion

    People with implementation talent and experience will be in high demand in the next decade. The supply of these people will be small. The work is irreducibly human. It can be placed, firmly and permanently, on the human side of the autonomy threshold.

    As a result, most existing companies will fail at adapting their systems to a world of agentic AI. Economist Joseph Schumpeter (1883-1950) put it best: “The problem that is usually being visualized is how capitalism administers existing structures, whereas the relevant problem is how it creates and destroys them.”20 As with most previous disruptions, more newcomers than incumbents will adopt the technology and thrive.

    But that is not the whole story. Within every large organization, there are people like Jack Maple. They are buried in the ranks, close to the real work, and frustrated by the gap between what is possible and what is actually done. The organizations that find them, empower them, and let them lead organizational change will be the ones that escape pilot purgatory. The rest will coast on goodwill until the goodwill runs out.

    Up to now we have been discussing relationships within organizations. We turn next to relationships in the market, between producers and consumers. We shift from supply-side messiness to the value of work that meets the messy demand.

    Key Takeaway

    Implementing AI requires radical, architectural organizational change, not just overlaying tools onto old workflows. Successful implementation demands a rare “implementer” who uniquely bundles deep tacit process knowledge, technological fluency, and the political capital necessary to navigate resistance and guide workers through disruptive transitions.

    Compressed Signals

    Redesigning screening

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Credential InflationHiring & ScreeningOrganisations
    Share

    The $5.3 million shouldn't have gone to a college dropout who cheated in an interview. But that's precisely what happened to Roy Lee in 2025.143

    Lee, a twenty-one-year-old computer science student at Columbia University, built Interview Coder, software that gives real-time answers on screen during technical interviews. Think of it as a silent genius crouched under the desk. He used it to ace an Amazon coding interview, got exposed, and was promptly suspended. But instead of apologizing, he rebranded.

    Within a month, Cluely was born: “AI for cheating on everything.“ His launch video showed him wearing smart glasses on a dinner date. Cluely fed Lee answers to his date's questions in real time, making him sound knowledgeable and charming. Within months, Lee had $5.3 million in seed funding and thousands of subscribers paying to sound brilliant.144

    Cluely's story reveals a structural problem for organizations. While AI has improved productivity and empowered workers to do things they couldn't do before, it also changes the way people produce information about themselves. When everyone can use Cluely to answer tough interview questions, the ways organizations demonstrate that a candidate is competent and worth hiring are fundamentally altered.

    This chapter is about how organizations can screen credibly when AI makes it easy for anyone to fake competence. How can a firm tell that a candidate deserves the job and the promotion? To answer that, we need to understand why people signal in the first place, how others interpret those signals, and how AI upends both.

    The signals we send

    Your employers cannot see your most important qualities. They cannot see your intelligence, your judgment under pressure, how hard you work without supervision, or whether you are genuine and enthusiastic about your project. They only see surface-level signs: your resume, letters of recommendation and the hours you spent in your office. These appearances are the signs, and their value depends on how closely they relate to your real qualities.

    Signs simplify the employer's job. Employers do not have to spend days figuring out your true intelligence. Instead, all they have to do is see which school you graduated from. A well-written cover letter demonstrates your passion for the job and your perfect fit. Working long hours proves that you dedicate yourself. Each sign condenses complex and hard-to-see information into concise, easy-to-understand content.

    Manipulated signals

    The problem is that while some signals genuinely reflect your quality, others can be manipulated. We can choose which signals to send, and we can distort the signals. We send signals that make us look good. These manipulated signals create a gap between genuine quality and perceived quality.

    At prestigious law firms, the partners have no real idea who the best associates are. Talent is invisible; effort is noisy and hard to verify. All the partners can see are billable hours, e-mails, and the hum of late-night printers. So the associates compete in one type of signal the firm can measure: hours.

    A survey at two major firms found the median associate logging about 200 hours a month, with the top quarter reaching 220. Two-thirds said they would happily reduce their hours by even five percent, yet few dared. Everyone understood that working less might look like caring less. So the office became a kind of theater. Associates scheduled e-mails for 3 a.m. And they left jackets draped on chairs overnight to suggest they'd stepped away only briefly.145

    The rat race

    Manipulating signals to make yourself look good can be exhausting. But it gets worse when the goal is not to look good in isolation but to look good relative to everyone else. If you were the only associate working late, staying until 11 pm would be a costly but powerful signal. But when everyone stays until 11 p.m., it merely becomes the baseline. The signal's value depends not just on what you do, but on what everyone else is doing. And because you cannot control what others do, you are forced to keep up or risk falling behind.

    This is why signaling so often turns into a rat race. Each person inflates a little, which forces everyone else to inflate a little more, which resets the standard upward. Dating markets are a good example. On Match.com, people exaggerate their qualities not out of overconfidence, but because failing to do so makes things worse: you may not even get a first date. As one economist observed, everyone knows that many people who claim to be “Athletic and Toned“ are closer to “A Few Extra Pounds.“ Because exaggeration has become the norm, claiming “A Few Extra Pounds“ makes you sound significantly overweight. Honesty becomes self-defeating: not because honesty is bad, but because everyone else's inflation has shifted the scale. Daters have to exaggerate just to stay in the game.146

    Under Stalin, losing the rat race of competitive signaling could cost your life. At a Communist Party conference in the mid-1930s, delegates rose to applaud the “great leader.“ Once it started, the applause refused to end. Five minutes passed, then ten. The delegates felt the pain of aching palms, but no one dared be the first to stop. After eleven minutes, the director of a local paper factory finally stopped. That night, he was arrested.147

    The law firm, the dating profile, and the applause all share a common structure. There is a gap between who you really are and what other people can observe. In many situations, it is the perception that gets rewarded, not the reality underneath. And so enormous energy goes into performing qualities that may already exist. People put in effort not just to do the work, but to perform in the workplace theater, and the performance of competitive signaling never ends.

    The economics of showing off

    So far, we have looked at signaling from the sender's side: the associates staying late, the daters polishing a profile, the delegates clapping until their hands burn. But there is another side to the story. Employers, clients, and leaders have a problem of their own: they must figure out who is actually good, fit, and loyal. Jobs, promotions, and attention are scarce, and allocating them well requires information that is expensive to obtain.

    Some qualities can be tested directly. If you want to know whether someone can play the piano, ask them to play. If you want to know whether an artist can draw, hand them a pencil. These are skills, easy to observe and easy to verify. The demonstration is the proof, and there is no need for strategic signaling.

    But many of the qualities that matter most in professional life are not like that. Loyalty, diligence, and commitment: these are hidden. You cannot ask someone to “perform“ loyalty the way you ask them to play a piece of music. You cannot verify resilience in a thirty-minute interview. For qualities like these, direct demonstration is impossible. However, it might be possible to signal these qualities through a willingness to bear a cost that someone without the quality would not be willing to bear.

    Sima Qian's Shi Ji records a famous case of this. Rangju, a low-born officer in the state of Qi, was appointed to command the army against the invading states of Jin and Yan. Nobody doubted his knowledge of military strategy. What was not known was whether he had the resolve to lead an army that did not yet respect him.148

    Rangju asked the Duke to send his favored courtier, Zhuang Gu, to oversee the campaign. Zhuang Gu showed up late to a military parade. Rangju had him executed on the spot. When the king's messenger arrived to protest, galloping through the camp, Rangju executed the messenger's attendant.

    This was not a demonstration of ability. It was a demonstration of willingness to bear an enormous cost. Offending the king could have ended Rangju's career or even his life. Only someone genuinely committed to military discipline would accept that risk. After the executions, nobody in the army doubted him again. He went on to win many victories.

    Michael Spence, who won a Nobel Prize for formalizing this logic, showed that the same principle governs much of economic life.149 Consider the MBA. Graduates command higher salaries almost immediately, often before they have learned anything of practical use. This seems puzzling until you see it through Spence's lens. The degree does not just prove that you can endure pain. It proves that you are the type of person for whom the pain is worth it. A strong student expects to gain more from the MBA through better learning, a stronger network, and a steeper career trajectory, so the tuition, the exams, and the two years of forgone earnings are a worthy investment. A weaker student faces the same price tag but with a lower expected return, so they rationally opt out. The degree separates out students not by blocking anyone absolutely, but by creating a cost that is worth bearing only for those with the underlying quality.

    The same logic shows up in business. An entrepreneur who is confident her venture will succeed is willing to rent the expensive office downtown, because the clients it attracts will more than cover the cost. One who doubts his prospects sees the same rent as money thrown away. The office signals quality not because it requires suffering, but because the investment only makes sense if the business is genuinely strong.

    Once you see this logic, you start noticing it everywhere. Accepting an unpaid internship is costly, which is why it signals commitment: the sacrifice pays off only if you intend to build a serious career in the field. Expensive handbags, fragile watches, and cars that cost more to maintain than to drive are not often really acts of comfort: they are proof that you can afford a cost that would be ruinous for someone without the resources.

    The key insight is this: a signal carries information only when it is costly to produce, and the cost correlates with the quality being signaled. If a degree were easy, it would prove nothing. If a marathon were easy, finishing one would not impress anyone. Remove the cost, and you remove the message.

    When AI compresses signals

    AI changes both how workers send signals and how organizations interpret them. Cluely, the software that feeds candidates correct answers during live interviews, is perhaps an extreme example. More consequentially, AI is reshaping one of the most basic signals in hiring: the cover letter.

    Researchers have used data from online labor market platforms to study how AI changes the way cover letters get created and used.150 On these platforms, a client posts a job, say, building a website or designing a logo. Within a day, thirty or forty applications pour in. Most are generic: a copy-pasted paragraph, a list of skills, a price. But a few applicants have read the job description carefully. They mention specific requirements and explain why their experience fits this particular project. Their cover letters stand out.

    Before AI, the quality of a cover letter was a genuinely useful signal. Workers who spent more time tailoring their applications were not only more likely to get hired, but also more likely to do good work once hired and get a higher wage. The cover letter was doing real screening work.

    Of course, the cover letter was never a perfect filter. Some of the people it failed most were non-native English speakers. A software developer in Bangalore or a designer in São Paulo might have been exactly the right person for the job, but if their cover letter was poorly written, the client probably never got far enough to find out.

    When freelancing platforms introduced AI writing tools, these workers were among the first to benefit. One click transformed a rough draft into a polished, tailor-made cover letter. Those who used AI assistance were initially more likely to be hired. But the equilibrium effect was different. Everyone had access to the tool, and the worst writers gained the most because they had the most room to improve. The gap between the careful letter and the lazy one, once obvious at a glance, became almost invisible. Better workers were hired less often, and the wage premium they used to command disappeared.

    In the freelancing market, and elsewhere, organizations suffered the consequences of the loss of value in the cover letter: they ended up hiring the wrong worker. A tool meant to help everyone communicate better made the market worse at matching workers to jobs.

    This is what Spence's model predicts. A signal only works when its cost correlates with the quality being signaled. A cover letter worked because writing a good one required a combination of ability and effort. AI is breaking down screening, and organizations must rebuild it.

    How organizations must adapt their screening

    When AI compresses a signal, screening takes place wherever there is a signal that still conveys information. Organizations are shifting their attention towards those signals that AI had not compressed such as past work experience and platform scores aggregating many years of feedback. Not a disastrous outcome for established workers, but a disaster for newcomers who had no time to build a history.

    The same pattern can be observed beyond freelancing. In China, where postgraduate degrees have become easier to obtain and embellish, due to the expansion of higher education, the undergraduate degree has regained importance among employers. The reason is that it rests on the “gaokao“, a national entrance exam that is hard to game. As the signals that come later in a career grow noisier, the ones that come earlier, and are backed by transparent competition, become more informative.

    Also, organizations are increasing the weight of in-person and real-time interaction. In some firms, interviews are much longer and include exams, case discussions and real projects, precisely because they are harder to game. The cost of showing up in person, the awkwardness of being rejected is what makes it informative. Even being willing to attend this long unstructured interview becomes, in Spence's terms, a signal whose cost still correlates with quality. They are also increasing the requirement for the candidate to show significant upfront work, again, a signal that does separate the lazy from the committed.

    How can individuals adapt? As we tell our students: rather than turn in the same mediocre project putting half of the effort into it, use AI to do immensely better projects. Use the time AI saves you. The competitive advantage is not the tool, everyone has it. The advantage is what you can do with the time it saves you. This is the paradox of signal compression: AI destroys the value of the signals it can easily produce, but in doing so, it raises the value of everything it cannot do.

    While writing this chapter during the Chinese New Year, Jin received a pleasant phone call from his friend Evan Guo, the CEO of Zhaopin.com, simply to exchange greetings. Evan noted that AI now allows written greeting messages to scale infinitely, effectively rendering them meaningless. But a phone call requires time, and time remains scarce. Because it cannot be effortlessly automated and easily scaled, committing your time to call someone shows genuine care and commitment. For organizations, this logic extends to their client relationships, their hiring processes, and every context where the cost of attention is the signal that matters.

    AI has not changed what employers, clients, and collaborators ultimately want to know about you. They still want to know whether you are competent, whether you can be trusted, and whether your judgment is sound. What has changed is the channel. The polished résumé and the eloquent cover letter can no longer serve as credible channels. The new channels that have taken their place are older and simpler: verifiable track records, real-time interaction, and demonstrated work. Organizations that redesign their screening around these channels will hire better. Workers who understand this logic will invest accordingly.

    Key Takeaway

    If everyone can signal excellence, the signal collapses — organisations must find new ones.

    Cheap Cognition

    The Autonomy Threshold

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Understanding AIAutonomy ThresholdLabour Market
    Share

    In January 2026, a student approached one of us and claimed that, with the help of new AI tools, she could complete a PhD in ten days instead of four years. She had a point. Tools such as Claude Code allow a researcher to use AI agents to download data, find relevant literature, conduct statistical analysis, craft a formal model, and even write the full paper. If we only want to produce a standard piece of work, the student would be mostly right.1

    However, if the student wants to obtain a PhD in order to advance science as much as possible and, no less, to later have a good job, then using AI to complete the PhD does not necessarily shorten it. If the tool makes competent performance easier, then more people can do the work. That means that the effective supply of labor increases. So, the question for our student is not just whether she becomes more productive; the question is, what remains scarce after everyone uses the same tool.

    This anecdote illustrates the crucial difference between productivity and wages. That difference is driven by scarcity. We see this economic principle at work in history. Previous waves of technological change created different scarcities. Understanding that history can help us understand what is coming. Two precedents teach different lessons: the industrial revolution and the information technology revolution.

    Two revolutions

    The Industrial Revolution: technology eventually helps all

    Before the Industrial Revolution, apprentice weavers spent years learning their trade. A master transferred skills by training someone for more than a decade. The best clothes would sell at high prices, because few people could produce high-quality work. Skill and experience were scarce.

    Then machines arrived. The Edmund Cartwright power loom, invented in 1784 and widely adopted by the 1830s, meant that a teenager could learn to handle the loom in days and produce in one day a volume of cloth that would have required weeks from a master artisan. The skilled Lancashire weavers faced ruin as their cumulative experience became almost useless.2

    However, after roughly sixty years of stagnating wages—from about 1790 to 1850—something unexpected happened that pessimistic observers like Friedrich Engels and Karl Marx had not predicted: the sons and grandchildren of the weavers did not remain poor. By the end of the nineteenth century, the real income of most workers had doubled. Machines made workers’ lives better.3

    How did this happen? As the number of machines per worker grew, each worker could produce more cloth. The machine could do the heavy work, but the worker remained necessary. Somebody had to put the cotton in and make sure the machine continued to operate. The worker was complementary to the machine.

    Why did workers, rather than capital owners, capture the gains? Institutions, such as unions and democracy, helped to increase workers’ bargaining power.4 But the relative scarcity of capital and workers was critical. The supply of workers was limited, but capital, in the form of machines and factories, could be built and reproduced. With enough time and money, anybody could make another factory or another textile mill. As the earnings of capitalists went up, investors built more capital equipment. Competition persisted until returns to capital went back to normal. Workers were the scarce factor. Hence, workers eventually benefited.

    The IT Revolution: technology helps highly skilled workers

    Compare what happened during the Industrial Revolution with the information technology (IT) revolution. Mass-produced personal computers, like the Apple II in 1977 and the IBM PC in 1981, spread into factories and offices. Like the mechanical loom, they automated work that previously required human skill. But their impact on wages was very different from what occurred previously. In the United States, from 1980 to 2020, the real median weekly earnings of full-time workers with a bachelor’s degree or higher increased by about 20 percent, while the earnings of workers with a high school education declined slightly.5 The earnings gap between college-educated and high-school-educated workers, what economists call the college wage premium, nearly doubled.6

    Why did the IT revolution not follow the same pattern as the Industrial Revolution? Because the IT revolution generated a different kind of scarcity. The mechanical loom complemented all workers. Anybody could learn to use it in a few days. By contrast, computers helped skilled workers more than unskilled workers. To use a database or analytical software, workers needed high-level cognitive skills.

    Computers made knowledge acquisition cheap, but they placed knowledge utilization, the hard work of applying knowledge to solving real problems, entirely in human hands. The result was that computers amplified the economic value of people with higher levels of education. A financial analyst with a spreadsheet was worth far more than an analyst without one, but only if the analyst knew what to model. A lawyer with a database of case law could conduct research faster, but the technology only helped the lawyer who knew which precedents mattered. Technology complemented knowledge; therefore, the workers who gained more from the technology were those who had high levels of knowledge and education.

    As a result, information technology divided the labor market. The relative demand for workers with high levels of education, who could use computers as tools, went up. The relative demand for office workers, accountants, and administrative assistants, whose routine cognitive work was substituted by computers, dropped. The relative demand for workers at the base of the distribution, such as janitors, health assistants, and restaurant workers, and others doing physical labor without computers, stayed flat. The result was economic polarization.7

    The two revolutions, industrial and information, led to two distinct outcomes. The Industrial Revolution was, eventually, good for all workers because the machines complemented all the workers. The IT revolution was very good for some workers, but far less beneficial for many others. Computers only complemented highly educated workers. Thus, the IT revolution has produced an era of economic polarization.

    Which historical precedent is more relevant to the AI revolution that is now taking shape? If AI is like the power loom, then we can expect a painful transition followed by broadly shared prosperity. If AI is like the personal computer, complementing only those who already have scarce skills, then economic polarization will increase.

    The AI revolution could create a third possibility. The IT revolution cheapened codifiable information processing, but it required human cognition to operate it. What if AI can perform many codifiable cognitive tasks by itself? That would make some forms of cognition abundant, and it would shift cognitive scarcity toward other human traits.

    To understand this new technology, we propose a new framework. Central to this framework is what we call the autonomy threshold.

    The autonomy threshold

    Adaptive cruise control helps you drive. It adjusts your speed to match traffic, maintains safe distances, and handles acceleration and braking. It makes you a better and safer driver. But it changes the economics relatively little because the human must remain in the driver’s seat. Human time is still the limit on how much driving gets done in the economy. By contrast, a fully autonomous vehicle transforms the economics entirely. The car no longer needs a human, so the supply of driving services is no longer limited by human time. Driving is limited only by machine capacity.

    The boundary between machines that complement humans and machines that replace humans is the autonomy threshold. This is the point at which AI switches from assisting humans to operating alone. Consider what happens if AI can deliver a finished output whose quality, at a given cost, makes hiring a human to check, correct, or own the result too expensive.8 This boundary is the autonomy threshold.9

    The autonomy threshold applies to tasks, not to workers. Workers hold jobs, and jobs contain many tasks. A worker’s exposure to AI replacement depends on how many of the tasks she is paid to perform cross the autonomy threshold. Her future also depends on whether the remaining tasks can be cleanly separated because they are a loose list of discrete tasks or whether instead they form a “strong” bundle, from which peeling away the automatable tasks is difficult, as we discuss in chapter 3.

    Below the threshold, AI operates in assistance mode. It helps humans, but people remain in control of the finished product. The human reviews the work, catches errors, and takes responsibility for the outcome. In this mode, the economic outcomes are similar to the outcomes of the IT revolution. Employers need fewer hours of human time per unit of output, and that expands the workers’ capacity. The analyst who typically writes two reports a week will now be able to write many more in the same amount of time.

    When AI crosses the autonomy threshold, when it can deliver a finished solution without a human who checks and owns the outcome, then everything changes. Below the threshold, AI complements humans. Above it, AI can replace humans who do that task. Even when that occurs, AI will not necessarily replace the whole job or the worker. A doctor may no longer need to write her clinical notes, but she will continue doing all the other tasks of her job. For her, AI will be a substitute at the task level, thereby increasing her productivity.

    Three tiers of the autonomy threshold

    Every job sits at a different point on the messiness spectrum, depending on the autonomy thresholds of its component tasks and the cost of separating them. A job consisting of a single, well-defined, verifiable task has a low threshold and will fall quickly to AI. A job whose threshold is high, for instance a job comprising a tangle of interdependent tasks involving people, politics, and friction, is messy and will resist automation even when the technology improves enormously. The question for any worker is: how high is my job’s threshold and how fast is the technology climbing toward it?

    Two things make a job messy. First, the tasks that compose it are entangled and hard to separate. This could be because partial outputs are difficult to verify, because reallocating components is costly, or because significant spillovers run across tasks. Second, the job could require holding together a complex nexus of human relationships. This inevitably involves managing conflict or mediating opposing interests. It also involves navigating people’s personal lives, moods, and agendas (e.g., our parenting example in the preface). This second source of messiness is far harder to automate, as we shall discuss.

    The following figure conceptually depicts the relationship between automation and messiness. On the horizontal axis we plot increasing messiness. On the vertical axis we plot how capable AI is. The curve depicts the increasing autonomy threshold as messiness increases. Very capable AI becomes less likely to replace a job as we move from clean, single-task jobs (tier one jobs) to messy bundles of tasks (tier two jobs) to a nexus of human relationships (tier three jobs).

    Tier one: single-task jobs

    On the left side of the figure are jobs that consist of a single task or a narrow cluster of closely related tasks with verifiable outputs: technical translation, coding a predefined problem, classifying images, and drafting a contractual clause. These are clean problems; they have clear inputs, well-defined success criteria, and verifiable outputs.

    For these jobs, the autonomy threshold is low. Currently, AI is already crossing or has crossed the threshold for some of them. Answers can be verified as correct, and hence we can train the system on the right answers, measure its performance, and improve. Once the threshold is crossed, the human worker has little to sell. For instance, researchers found that job posts for writing and coding on a major freelancing platform fell by 21 percent relative to labor-intensive jobs within eight months of ChatGPT’s launch.10 That does not, however, mean human income in the sector necessarily goes to zero, as we will see.

    Tier two: bundles of tasks

    Tier two jobs consist of multiple tasks bundled together inside a single role. A medical doctor does not just diagnose illnesses. She also triages and communicates with patients, consults with colleagues, trains residents, and bears legal responsibility for diagnosis. AI may cross the autonomy threshold for some diagnostic tests while remaining below it for other tasks in the bundle.

    In tier two, the autonomy threshold for the whole job is much higher than the threshold for any individual task within it. All the tasks in the bundle require knowledge. Some tasks, such as diagnosis, only need codified knowledge, whereas others need contextual, interpersonal, and local knowledge. The former tasks are easy for AI to replace, but the latter ones are difficult to automate.

    In many instances, splitting the tasks apart can also fragment the knowledge that is relevant to job success. For example, a radiologist who reads a scan already knows the patient’s history, already has a relationship with the referring physician, and already faces the legal obligation that goes with providing a diagnosis. Unbundling the scan-reading task and handing it to an AI program would require someone to reproduce the whole context separately. This would create a coordination cost that might exceed the potential savings from unbundling the task and handing it to AI.

    The characteristic structure of tier two is a single human being supported by the machine, either from above or from below.11 In the “robots below” configuration, AI takes over the routine problems of the job while the human handles the exceptions. An entrepreneur with AI tools can build a prototype in hours that previously required a team of engineers. But it is the entrepreneur who discovers the problem, creates the solution, chooses the market, and bears the risk. AI increases the leverage of a human’s exceptional knowledge.

    In the “robots above” configuration, AI handles the more sophisticated aspects of cognitive work and deals with the exceptional problems that are unusual or hard. A nurse practitioner with AI diagnostic support still examines patients, communicates diagnoses, and deals with emotional and physical tasks. However, in this case, AI complements the nurse because she does not need to incur the cost of acquiring more medical knowledge to solve a wide range of difficult problems.

    Autonomous AI on a task is harmful for workers who perform those simple and clear tier one tasks, but it may help workers who perform a bundle of tasks. AI may help highly trained experts when it removes some of their routine work. And AI may help workers with less training by giving them access to expert cognition without needing to acquire it themselves.

    Tier three: a nexus of relationships

    In the third tier, depicted on the right side of the figure’s horizontal axis, are the jobs where the autonomy threshold is so high that almost all the work requires a human. For example, we know the head of engineering at a manufacturing plant who must decide whom to hire, which machines to buy, and how to install them in the plant. She must negotiate with the workers and the higher-ups on proposed solutions, and she must mobilize the resources to implement those solutions. That bundle of tasks is extraordinarily hard to automate. AI might be able to draft her reports, optimize her schedules, and analyze her data, but none of that part of the work is complicated. A lot of her work involves managing friction with other humans and institutions. She must work with the town hall to obtain permits and with people at corporate headquarters to secure funding. Knowing who to ask and how to ask involves tacit knowledge that is not available in AI training data.

    In tier three jobs, managing interdependence between humans, not just between tasks, is messy. There must be a human being who holds the nexus of relations together. The whole job must be performed by a human who handles the interdependencies, manages the friction, and is accountable for the result. As Boris Cherny, creator of Anthropic’s Claude Code, put it in February 2026, “Someone has to prompt the Claudes, talk to customers, coordinate with other teams, decide what to build next. Engineering is changing and great engineers are more important than ever.”12

    Tier three work is different because its messiness is not a temporary limitation on AI’s capacity; it is a permanent feature of the problem. Organizations are coalitions of people with legitimate but conflicting goals. To function well, they require someone with the authority to settle disputes and manage those relationships among people. Chapter 6 examines authority; chapter 7 examines the harder problem of redesigning organizations themselves so AI can be deployed at scale. Neither problem becomes cleaner when computers get faster.

    But relationships also matter on the market side. Some jobs survive AI not because the task is technically beyond automation, but because the customer values the human being behind the task. A live concert, a hand-engraved card, a sommelier in a fine restaurant: in each case, a machine may be able to produce the sound, the object, or the recommendation. But automation changes the product because it removes the human origin, presence, care, or trust that the buyer was paying for. Chapter 8 examines this demand-side threshold: the point at which buyers no longer treat a machine-made output as equivalent to one with human origin. A job survives when either the supply-side threshold is high enough or the demand-side threshold is high enough. The most durable jobs are protected by both.

    If relationship-dependent work is so costly, could firms and markets reorganize to need less of it? Chapters 6 and 7 argue that the answer is limited. The relational work is not a design flaw; it is a structural feature of organizations composed of people with conflicting interests, and of markets where consumers buy not only outputs but the human source behind them. Some relationships cannot be reduced to contracts, prices, or algorithms.

    This same limit shapes what AI can do once inside the firm. As Part III shows, AI does not merely change which jobs survive; it changes the systems that make work trustworthy. It cheapens what spot markets already do well: search, contracting, execution, and the verification of standardized output. It does not cheapen what depends on long-term relationships: reputations built through repeated work, trust among people who expect to keep working together, standards owned by someone willing to defend them, and apprenticeship through which one generation transmits judgment to the next. Chapters 9 through 12 show how these systems must be rebuilt around the relational mechanisms AI cannot supply. Firms, even when restructured by AI, remain the most common home for messy work.

    The messy demand: satiation versus the Jevons effect

    The discussion above was mostly about what AI can do; that is, the supply side. But understanding the market value of workers also requires understanding the demand side. AI makes cognition cheap, and that reduces the cost of goods that require intensive cognitive labor, such as software or legal documents. Cheap cognition makes it possible to solve many previously unsolvable problems. Hence, we expect demand for those cognitive tasks to increase.

    In broad terms, there are two cases (or scenarios) depending on the size of that increase. We call one the satiation case and the other the Jevons effect.

    Satiation. In 1850, when nighttime fell over London, the city was transformed. The wealthy burned candles that cost more per hour than a laborer’s daily wage. The poor huddled around dried plant stems dipped in animal fat, which provided barely enough illumination to prevent stumbling into furniture. Then, over the next century and a half, as Nobel Prize winner William Nordhaus documented, the cost of light fell by a factor of forty thousand.13

    The surprising result is that the illumination industry has lost most of its economic value. Today, an American household spends less in real terms on illumination than a Victorian family spent in a year. Why? Humans do not demand forty thousand times more light just because it is forty thousand times cheaper. Once we have enough light to see well, we use our money to buy other things. At the risk of exaggerating, the innovation of electric light was so successful that now we barely think about the economic importance of illumination.

    The same is true for agriculture. In 1900, American farms employed 40 percent of the total labor force. Since then, farming has become immensely more productive.14 It is so productive that the agriculture sector employed only 1.2 percent of the labor force in 2022, which is fewer people than the pet care sector.15 Employment collapsed even as output increased because of limited demand. That is an example of satiation.

    This may seem surprising, but it is a natural consequence of demand becoming inelastic, which is the point when consumers are close to satiation. This could well be the case with AI. If demand for cognition-intensive goods becomes inelastic, then the tasks that AI is automating will become economically irrelevant. Humans will then have to make their living in other sectors, those that are less affected by AI. Chapter 8 discusses those sectors, including artisan production, personal services, and musicians who perform live music.

    Jevons effect. Satiation is not the only possibility. If demand dramatically increases as the cost of producing cognition drops, then employment in automated sectors will also increase. This is called the Jevons effect, named after nineteenth-century economist William Stanley Jevons. He observed that as steam engines became more efficient, total coal consumption did not decline as some expected; instead, coal use increased significantly.16

    It is too simplistic to say that AI will make it less costly to find solutions to more problems, or that, as a result, demand for workers will increase. That’s because AI itself will deal with some of the problems. Human employment will increase only when humans remain complementary to newly expanded AI ability. In other words, there is a step from the Jevons effect for output to the Jevons effect for employment. If new demand is directed to tasks above the autonomy threshold, AI will fill it. Employment expands only when new demand requires tasks below the threshold; that is, tasks where humans remain the bottleneck.

    Consider the impact of electronic spreadsheets in the early 1980s. They automated the main task of financial analysis. Calculations that took days to complete with pencil and paper now took minutes. One would have expected the technology to decimate the jobs of financial analysts. Instead, the demand for financial analysis exploded. Firms could now model scenarios they had never bothered to consider and update forecasts weekly instead of quarterly. The spreadsheet automated the arithmetic but created enormous new demand for doing elaborate calculations and understanding what the numbers meant. The analyst was complementary to the tool. Jevons won.

    In both the satiation and the Jevons cases, there will be jobs to do and ongoing demand. In the first case, which we discuss in chapter 8, the automated sectors will lose economic weight and demand will move toward sectors that are least susceptible to automation. In the second case, demand grows so much in automated sectors that employment increases within them.

    Road map

    Part I moves up the messiness spectrum. We start with single-task jobs where the threshold is lowest (chapter 2). Then we examine how bundling tasks into a single role raises the threshold (chapter 3). In chapters 4 and 5 we show how AI reshapes the knowledge hierarchy inside those bundles: from below (empowering experts) and from above (empowering non-experts).

    Part II reaches the messiest end of the spectrum: the organizational politics and implementation challenges that no algorithm can handle (chapters 6 and 7), and the demand-side premium that protects jobs even when AI could technically do them (chapter 8).

    Part III turns to how organizations must redesign themselves when more work becomes a human-AI bundle. The topics include: screening workers when the old signals collapse (chapter 9); maintaining quality when verification costs explode (chapter 10); setting standards when the production function changes shape (chapter 11); and rebuilding the training pipeline when apprenticeship economics break down (chapter 12). Each chapter also derives the career implications for workers.

    The conclusion chapter widens the frame from jobs to the AI economy. It shows how the Messy Jobs framework helps explain changes in production, the continuing role of firms, the reshaping of markets, and the limits and possibilities of AI-driven economic growth.

    Key Takeaway

    AI makes cognition cheap, shifting economic scarcity toward irreducibly human traits. A job’s vulnerability depends on its “autonomy threshold” and “messiness”—AI easily replaces humans in clean, single-task roles but struggles to automate complex, bundled tasks or roles requiring relationship management.

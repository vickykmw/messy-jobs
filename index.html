From the Authors

To our kids and all those who will start their working lives in this new world. Our concern for their future motivated us to write the book.

The speed of change of AI technology is rapid. Managers are worried, workers are scared, parents are concerned, and policymakers are confused. This situation may last for a long time, and we write this book to help them make better decisions and discover new opportunities.

Inside the Book

Explore the Chapters

Introduces the autonomy threshold — the point at which AI can deliver a finished output without a human checking the result. Draws lessons from the Industrial Revolution and IT revolution to frame what AI makes scarce.

Who Should Read This
Everyone — this chapter sets the framework for the entire book
Key Takeaway
AI doesn’t destroy value — it shifts scarcity. The question is: what becomes scarce next?

Explores how jobs consisting of one well-defined, verifiable task are most exposed to AI. Tier 1 jobs — technical translation, image classification, clause drafting — have clear inputs and measurable outputs that AI can optimise against.

Who Should Read This
Freelancers, early-career professionals, and anyone in highly specialised narrow work
Key Takeaway
The cleaner the task, the lower the autonomy threshold — and the sooner AI crosses it

Examines why tasks are bundled into jobs, and what makes some bundles strong and others weak. Strong bundles resist unbundling because their tasks share context, knowledge, and accountability.

Who Should Read This
Managers and HR professionals redesigning roles in the AI era
Key Takeaway
AI unbundles some jobs and rebundles others — understanding your bundle is your first defence

When AI handles the routine and the expert handles the exceptions, AI amplifies human exceptional knowledge — giving entrepreneurs, surgeons, and senior engineers greater leverage.

Who Should Read This
Senior professionals wondering whether AI threatens or empowers them
Key Takeaway
If you are the exception-handler, AI working below you multiplies your value

When AI handles sophisticated cognitive tasks, it empowers less-trained workers who previously lacked access to expert-level reasoning. A nurse with AI diagnostic support can now handle cases that once required a specialist.

Who Should Read This
Mid-level professionals and policy-makers interested in labour market inequality
Key Takeaway
AI above may create a new middle class — giving less-trained workers access to expert cognition

Organisations are political systems, not just machines. This chapter explains why AI cannot replace human authority — the ability to make decisions that stick because they come from a person with legitimate power and accountability.

Who Should Read This
Leaders whose job involves making decisions that others must accept and act on
Key Takeaway
Authority is not knowledge — it is legitimacy, and legitimacy must be human

Introduces the implementer — the person who navigates politics, builds coalitions, and makes change actually happen — as a distinct and newly scarce type of worker. Includes a case study on BBVA escaping pilot purgatory.

Who Should Read This
Change managers and executives frustrated that good ideas never get implemented
Key Takeaway
The bottleneck was never the plan — it was always the implementation

Some jobs survive not because AI cannot technically do them, but because humans demand that a human does them. Live concerts, personal therapy, artisan craft — these carry intrinsic value derived from human origin.

Who Should Read This
Creative professionals, educators, and anyone whose work involves human connection
Key Takeaway
When AI can do it perfectly, human imperfection becomes the product

When AI makes it easy for everyone to produce polished work, the signals employers use to screen candidates lose their power. This chapter examines how organisations must redesign their hiring processes.

Who Should Read This
HR professionals, recruiters, and job-seekers navigating AI-assisted hiring
Key Takeaway
If everyone can signal excellence, the signal collapses — organisations must find new ones

AI makes it easy to produce work that looks good but isn’t. This chapter analyses how verification costs explode when AI assists workers, and how organisations must redesign quality control.

Who Should Read This
Managers and team leads responsible for quality in knowledge-work environments
Key Takeaway
The hardest problem with AI-assisted work is not output quality — it is verifying it

AI front-loads progress — tasks get 80% done quickly, but the last 20% is as hard as ever. This creates a systematic trap where organisations celebrate fast early results and never finish properly.

Who Should Read This
Project managers and executives setting quality standards in AI-assisted workflows
Key Takeaway
AI makes starting easy — the last mile is still human, and it still matters

Junior roles have traditionally been how organisations grow talent through learning-by-doing. AI is automating exactly those entry-level tasks. This chapter asks what happens to the pipeline when the bottom rung disappears.

Who Should Read This
Students, early-career workers, and the managers who develop them
Key Takeaway
The apprenticeship model is breaking down — organisations that replace it will win the talent war

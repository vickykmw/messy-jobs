    Disappearing Single-Task Jobs

    When AI Crosses the Threshold

    Luis Garicano  ·  Jin Li  ·  Yanhui Wu
    Autonomy ThresholdAutomationUnderstanding AI
    Share

    Andrej Karpathy, a founding member of OpenAI and former AI director at Tesla, is one of the most capable programmers in the world. So when he announced in February 2026 that he had stopped writing his own code, people took notice.1 His remark captured fundamental changes in what programming had become.

    When tools like GitHub Copilot first appeared, they were celebrated as powerful assistants. They could predict the next few lines, fill in boilerplate forms, and save hours of typing. But the basic structure of the work remained: Humans still designed the logic, checked the output, and stayed firmly in control.

    But within a few years, the technology changed the workflow. Karpathy coined the term vibe coding in 2025 to describe a radically different new way of coding. Instead of writing code line by line, a user could simply describe her intent in plain English and let the machine do the job. “I fully give in to the vibes and forget that the code even exists,” Karpathy explained.2

    AI had evolved from helping a human write code to doing the coding entirely on its own. According to Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang, “It is our job to create computing technology such that nobody has to program. Everybody in the world is now a programmer.”3

    For many programming tasks, AI is crossing the autonomy threshold. These are tier one jobs from chapter 1: single tasks with clear inputs, verifiable outputs, and low autonomy thresholds. This chapter examines what determines when that threshold is crossed and what happens to the workers on the other side.

    Autonomous AI

    Coding is often seen as being highly exposed to AI.4 But other sectors are also susceptible to AI disruption. Consider, for instance, the evolution of the customer support industry over the last three years.

    In a recent paper, economists Erik Brynjolfsson, Danielle Li, and Lindsey Raymond analyzed a 2023 experiment in which a Fortune 500 software company gave AI assistants to its 5,179 customer service agents.5 They found that AI did not help the best agents, but it helped the least experienced people increase their productivity by 35 percent. Novices were suddenly solving problems at rates similar to veterans with more than a year of experience. The AI-based assistant had absorbed the best workers’ call transcripts and then gave novices access to expertise that normally took months to accumulate. Across the whole call center, the gains weren’t limited to the least experienced workers. Average resolutions per hour rose by about 14 percent, agents handled more chats, and customer satisfaction increased slightly. AI was effectively assisting customer support agents.

    Contrast that outcome with the experience of the call center at Yum China, one of the largest restaurant companies in China. The company operates more than eighteen thousand KFC, Pizza Hut, and other restaurants. Between 2018 and 2025, as digital ordering grew, customer contact volume increased sixfold to over sixty million per year. To deal with this, Yum China deployed C-Smart, an AI agent that evolved from simple keyword matching to advanced generative AI.6

    Without human intervention, C-Smart agents can fully resolve simple customer requests, such as checking order status, asking for faster delivery, or inquiring about restaurant locations. C-Smart uses human agents only for complex or emotionally charged issues, such as complaints and disputes, which account for about 10 percent of the cases. AI has crossed the autonomy threshold for the simple tasks. This allows Yum China to handle six times the volume with roughly a third of the people.

    Full autonomy, as we defined in chapter 1, marks the point where a human’s contribution to the task no longer justifies the cost of including her. Before full autonomy, for whatever reason, the process works better with her than without her. She catches errors, supplies context, and provides trust to the buyers. AI is a complement to her efforts. After AI attains full autonomy on a particular task, her contribution becomes less valuable. The AI will replace the human on that task. If the job consists entirely of that task, as is the case in tier one, the human will have little or nothing to sell.

    To make this concrete, consider the case of Olivia Lipkin, the sole copywriter for a tech startup in San Francisco. Her role required her to define the company’s voice. In late 2022, her assignments dwindled. Then, on the company’s internal Slack channels, her managers began referring to her as “Olivia/ChatGPT.” It seems that the choice was not between her and another coworker but between a salaried employee and a zero-cost large language model. In April 2023, Lipkin was let go without explanation.7

    The determinants of the autonomy threshold

    The point at which AI can reach full autonomy is not determined by technology alone. In practice, autonomy is often limited less by raw capability than by whether an organization can safely delegate authority to a system that will sometimes be wrong. Three forces determine when that happens for any given type of work: technology, regulation, and consumer preferences.

    Technology

    The first determinant of the autonomy threshold, technical capability, refers to when an AI system can produce correct solutions to a wide range of real-world problems. A model might perform well in tests, but it can still fail when it is deployed if the real work depends on local context and tacit knowledge that never made it into the training data. If the AI cannot deal with real-world disorder, then the human must remain in the loop to correct its mistakes.

    The technology is improving rapidly. Many skills that looked impossible for AI one or two years ago, such as doing high-level math and writing complex prose, are now trivial for today’s models. An important empirical predictor of whether today’s models can handle a task autonomously is the time it takes for a skilled human to complete the work. Current models handle short, clearly defined tasks well, but they suffer as the time horizon expands and as the tasks require sustained attention, context, and error correction.8 Researchers have described this as a “jagged technological frontier.” The frontier appears within a job when AI dramatically improves performance on some tasks and actively harms it on others.9

    Three obstacles to technological improvement are fundamental. They impose essential constraints on what AI can do, and there is uncertainty within the scientific community about how fast they will improve.10

    The first obstacle to improvement is task verifiability. For verifiable tasks, AI has a target, like writing a correct piece of code, predicting the shape of a protein, or making a good move in the game of Go. In these cases, AI can learn from its mistakes and improve rapidly. AI is likely to remain imprecise for tasks whose correctness is subjective.11 This type of work might include writing a novel, designing a logo, choosing a restructuring plan, or deciding how to balance the aggressiveness of a cancer treatment versus the quality of life in a clinical setting. Here, it is harder to apply learning techniques and AI is likely to remain imprecise.

    The second barrier is that most current AI systems do not learn from deployment in the way human professionals learn from experience. A human professional accumulates better judgment over years of working on a variety of cases (patients, clients) during their careers. By contrast, the weights of a deployed AI model are usually fixed between training runs, and any memory it has is typically an external product feature.12 Hence, AI cannot build on yesterday’s work the way a junior associate can gradually become a senior partner. This matters most in roles where judgment improves over time.

    Amodei has offered a partial counterargument to this concern. As these systems learn to process vastly larger volumes of information at once (as context windows expand vastly), a model may be able to hold an entire career’s worth of case files, patient histories, or project logs. This could approximate something like human “experience,” by giving AI systems “context.” But the provision of generous informational context is not the same as learning.13

    The third obstacle is that AI has no body and no physical presence. This sounds trivial until you consider how much economic life depends on someone being there. A nurse holds a patient’s hand to reassure her, or a manager walks a factory floor and notices what the reports leave out. Robotics is advancing, but the gap between generating text and navigating an unstructured physical environment remains very significant. It is unclear how rapidly that gap will close.

    There are two other operational obstacles. Many people believe they will be overcome relatively quickly, but they still raise the autonomy threshold.

    First, AI responses can be biased. Given how LLMs are trained, they are likely to remain so. Large language models are trained, in part, by asking humans which of two responses they prefer. Researchers at Anthropic showed that this training process rewards agreement more than factual accuracy. When an AI response matched the user’s opinion, the user was more likely to prefer that response even if the AI response was incorrect. Humans, and the reward models used to train AI assistants, often preferred convincingly written sycophantic responses more than correct ones. The result is that models learn to flatter users.14

    Other research has found that models become more conformist as a dialogue progresses. The longer the exchange, the more the model drifts toward the user’s position, even when that position is wrong.15 And when a user pushes back with a persuasive but erroneous counterargument, models frequently abandon the correct answer and adopt the user’s mistaken view. Researchers call this “stance fickleness.”16 A lawyer who asks a model to find support for a weak legal theory may receive exactly that: confident, well-cited support for a position the model should have questioned.

    AI’s mistakes are not random. They are systematically biased toward telling people what they want to hear. This matters for AI autonomy because the bias is invisible to the user. A system that tells you what you want to hear will pass your review, precisely because you want that outcome. The human-in-the-loop check that is supposed to catch errors becomes unreliable when the errors are designed, by training, to evade the checks.

    Second, AI autonomy often fails at acting safely inside real systems. Once an AI system has real authority and can take actions, small mistakes can become large incidents.17 Consider Amazon Web Services. In mid-December 2025, AWS engineers allowed Kiro, Amazon’s own AI coding tool, to make changes to a production system. The tool, designed to act autonomously on behalf of its users, decided the best course of action was to delete and recreate the environment. The result was a thirteen-hour outage of a customer-facing system. The AI agent was sophisticated enough to write and deploy code, but it was unable to “understand” that deleting a live production environment is catastrophic. That is the jagged frontier.18

    Regulation

    The second determinant of the autonomy threshold is the law. For example, regulations determine who is permitted to sign and certify contracts, and who is liable for the consequences. This force is often stronger than technology itself.

    Consider elevator operators. At the start of the twentieth century, operating an elevator was a career. You needed some skill to stop the elevator in the right spot. By 1930, automatic elevators with a button for each floor were technically flawless. They were safer and cheaper than a human-operated elevator. But elevator operators still had a job in the US for another twenty years.19

    Two forces kept them employed: consumer trust and politics. On the one hand, the public initially did not trust automatic elevator brakes. On the other hand, unions fought hard to keep the operators employed. As a result, cities passed laws mandating a human operator to ensure “safety.” The technology was clearly able to operate autonomously, and yet the law raised the threshold to protect the jobs.

    We can see this today. A diagnosis is only valid if a licensed clinician endorses it. Company accounts are only valid when an auditor signs them. In many settings, regulation and professional liability ensure that even a perfectly written contract is not valid until a lawyer approves it. Regulation requires humans to be the “legal provider” of the work. These laws will protect many jobs long after the machine is able to provide a flawless answer.

    Because these decisions have large legal and societal implications, organizations and countries will make active decisions that affect the height of the autonomy threshold. They will not passively let technology play out. The professional associations for doctors, lawyers, and engineers will press governments to forbid autonomous AI in their fields. Sometimes the decisive constraint is not directly the law, but insurance. Insurers decide what they underwrite and that may set the autonomy threshold.

    This will be the defining political fight of our time. Tech firms and consumers will press to make autonomous deployment possible and reduce the cost of services, such as medical care. Professional associations will fight to prevent their jobs and tasks from being done autonomously by generative AI. The results of these fights will vary by profession.

    If the productivity gains are sufficiently large, then it is unlikely that regulatory pressure will succeed at keeping the improvements at bay for long. The end of the elevator operators is instructive. A huge strike in New York in 1945, involving sixteen thousand operators, forced residents to climb dozens of floors. The strike effectively paralyzed the garment industry. It made operators expensive and unreliable, which played an important role in automating elevators.20

    How regulation is deployed in this fight may be the largest source of variation in productivity growth between countries over the next decades. A country that locks its professions behind walls of mandatory human involvement, regardless of whether the human adds value, risks repeating the mistake of Ming China, which in 1433 burned its ocean-going fleet and banned foreign voyages. The Ming emperor protected his court from disruption. He also guaranteed that China would fall behind for centuries.

    Preferences

    Consumer preferences can prevent AI from operating autonomously even when the technology is able to do so. The same technical capability places different tasks on different sides of the autonomy threshold, depending on what the market requires.

    In some domains, a 90 percent correct answer is a success. Consider an algorithmic recommendation by Spotify or YouTube. If the song or podcast is terrible, the consumer can click “next” and ignore it. No one is going to sue Spotify. Because the cost of making a mistake is low, AI can do the work of providing recommendations autonomously, without human review.

    Now consider calculating the doses of a chemotherapy treatment. If the AI makes a numerical mistake and doubles the dose, the patient dies. The cost is unacceptable. Even a 99.9 percent probability of success is not good enough. A small margin of error puts human lives at risk and can lead to large lawsuits. A human must be in the loop and verify the decision.

    This logic also shows up in robotics. During the 2026 Chinese New Year Gala, humanoid robots performed a dancing routine before an audience of hundreds of millions. This clever demonstration reflected clear strategic thinking. They knew that if a dancing robot stumbled, the audience would just laugh. The cost of error was small. A reasonable rate of accuracy was more than enough for the dancing robot.21

    Now consider the same robot holding a wine glass in a restaurant full of customers. The technical challenge may not be much harder than robot dancing. But the cost of error is significantly higher. Even if the robot drops a glass just once in every hundred attempts, a busy restaurant that asks it to carry glasses a hundred times a day will hear shattering glasses every day. And broken glass near diners is not only an unpleasant scene, but also a potential liability.

    These examples illustrate the importance of considering the market’s tolerance for failure. If the cost of technological error is high, the autonomy threshold also moves up.

    The tolerance for errors is not the only way demand shapes the autonomy threshold. In some markets, customers value human origin as such. A live concert is preferred because the audience highly values the experience of sharing a room with a human performer. In these cases, the autonomy threshold is raised not by the risk of failure but by a positive preference for human provenance. We take up these demand-side determinants of the autonomy threshold in chapter 8.

    The technology will undoubtedly continue its rapid progress. Verifiability, sycophancy, and continuous learning may be solved in the future. But even in the long term, autonomy may be constrained by laws and politics, and by consumer demand for humanness.

    Jobs and earnings: the effect of automating cognitive tasks

    To understand market value, we need to consider not only the direct effect of technology but also the impact of supply and demand responses. When AI crosses the autonomy threshold, the direct effect is substitution on that task. Then everything turns on demand. If demand is inelastic, so that consumers are close to satiation, human employment shrinks. If demand is elastic, as in the Jevons effect case we discussed in chapter 1, human employment can persist, but only where humans remain complementary to the expanded output.

    Three early examples of research pertained to simple coding tasks, customer service support, and writing tasks. In each case, the academic literature found that AI acted as a complement to human workers rather than a substitute. Workers paired with AI tools became faster and, in some cases, produced higher-quality output. The gains were largest for the least skilled; novices using AI closed much of the gap between them and experienced workers.22

    This led to a discussion about whether AI would be broadly complementary to workers, raising productivity without threatening employment. If the pattern held, AI would look like a benign tool: everyone would do better work, the weakest would improve the most, and the labor market would adjust smoothly. But recent developments in agentic AI suggest that most single-task, non-messy jobs are likely to fall below the autonomy threshold sooner or later, unless regulation protects them.

    Consider the work of junior coders. On February 27, 2026, Jack Dorsey, cofounder of Block (the fintech company behind Square, Cash App, and Afterpay), posted his letter to shareholders explaining why the company was cutting its staff by 40 percent despite its strong performance. “A significantly smaller team, using the tools we’re building, can do more and do it better. And intelligence tool capabilities are compounding faster every week,” he explained. The market endorsed his explanation. Block’s shares soared by 24 percent after his announcement.23 Recently, companies like Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, and Verizon have all made sweeping cuts ostensibly for a similar reason.24 Many tech jobs are highly specialized, consisting of a single task. Once technology crosses the autonomy threshold for that task, the supply will be provided by machines and the earnings of the job holders will collapse. Recent evidence by Crane and Soto (2026) shows this systematically. Using US labor-survey data, they show that 500,000 fewer coders are employed today than pre-LLM trends suggested.25

    Next consider what happens with writing in the legal industry. In February 2023, Allen & Overy, one of the top-five “Magic Circle” law firms, became the first in the industry to deploy generative AI throughout the firm. Harvey, a specialized LLM for lawyers, was available to thirty-five hundred lawyers in forty-three offices. They used it to draft contracts, summarize due diligence documents, and research case law. The head of the firm’s innovation group, David Wakeling, called it a “game changer,” but he added a key caveat: “You must validate everything coming out of the system. You have to check everything.”26 Harvey now serves over seven hundred law firms and corporate legal departments across fifty-eight countries, including 50 percent of the top American law firms. Harvey was valued at $8 billion after its December 2025 funding round.27 It drafts and summarizes contracts and briefs, and it does excellent research. Most of these tasks now fall below the autonomy threshold. If you give a model a term sheet and ask for a first draft, you will get something usable. The correctness of this type of work is externally verifiable. For transactional drafting, the autonomy threshold is low, and AI is crossing it.28

    The market implications are rather bleak in all these cases. When AI crosses the autonomy threshold, it completes the entire task and produces outcomes without human intervention. A person is no longer necessary for the work itself, but a human is still needed for setting objectives and handling exceptions. Effective supply becomes enormous, and therefore wages for that activity drop.

    For people working in jobs that consist of clean, simple tasks, only two factors will protect them: consumer demand for authenticity (which we discuss in chapter 8) and regulation. In the case of lawyers, for example, licensing and liability requirements will protect them. We can anticipate that lawyers will organize and put pressure on the legal system to ensure their physical presence continues to be needed. We are not convinced that such efforts will protect the drafting layer. If “a lawyer must sign” becomes a rubber-stamp task, then one lawyer can supervise far more AI output than one lawyer could produce alone.

    Consider also the proliferation of direct-to-consumer “telehealth” services, such as Hims & Hers, that provide erectile dysfunction or ADHD prescriptions.29 Yes, the law says there must be a human signature, but it is easy to find a signing doctor who can endorse, in seconds, the AI-written prescription.

    As technology advances, the autonomy threshold will not be static. Jobs currently protected by legal or social barriers may soon be threatened by AI as the gap between human and machine performance shrinks. Consider self-driving taxis. In early 2024, Wuhan, a city in central China, saw a rare, large-scale protest by conventional taxi and ride-hailing drivers over the rollout of Baidu’s robotaxi service, Apollo Go. Videos that briefly circulated on Chinese social media showed that some protesters held banners demanding “fair competition,” while others complained about being undercut by cars that needed no salaries and no sleep. Representatives of the protesters eventually obtained meetings with local officials who promised to study their grievances and to ensure the “orderly” development of autonomous vehicles.30 But crucially, the officials did not promise withdrawal. Apollo Go continues to offer a five-mile ride for fifty US cents. To the extent that autonomous vehicle companies address safety concerns, many consumers will happily choose robotaxis over traditional services. The legal and political barriers are real, but they stand against a technological gap in favor of autonomous vehicles that grows every year.

    Of course, none of the above means there will be no jobs for lawyers or programmers in the future economy. Much of what both professions do, as will become clear in the rest of this book, falls along the messier end of the spectrum. These are far from clean, single-task jobs. They are often, in fact, quite messy bundles of tasks.

    Key Takeaway

    Clean, single-task jobs with verifiable outputs, like basic coding or copywriting, have low autonomy thresholds and face rapid AI replacement. Workers in these roles will primarily rely on artificial protections, such as government regulations, legal liability requirements, and consumer preferences, to maintain their employment.
